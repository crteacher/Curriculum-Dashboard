Mathematics of Democracy

What Makes an Election Fair?

Every voting method has a flaw. That's not an opinion — it's a theorem. Mathematicians have spent centuries trying to design a perfect election system, and in 1951, Kenneth Arrow proved it cannot exist.

Burlington, Vermont, 2009. The city used Instant Runoff Voting — a system specifically designed to elect the candidate most voters preferred. In the mayoral race, the candidate who would have beaten every other candidate in a head-to-head matchup came in third. The system failed the very test it was built to pass. The city repealed it the following year.
Most elections ask you to pick one candidate. A preference ballot asks you to rank all the candidates in order. This gives us much more information — not just who you want, but the full picture of your preferences.

A preference schedule tallies these ballots by grouping identical rankings together. It's the starting point for every method in this unit.
Preference ballot
A ballot where voters rank all candidates from most to least preferred.
Preference schedule
A table summarizing how many voters chose each ranking combination.

The plurality method is the most familiar: whoever gets the most first-place votes wins — even without a majority. It's simple, but it ignores everything voters feel about candidates ranked 2nd or 3rd.

Think about it: Is it fair to elect a candidate that the majority of voters ranked last? What information did we ignore?
Instant runoff voting (IRV), also called the plurality-with-elimination method, runs repeated rounds: in each round, the candidate with the fewest first-place votes is eliminated and those voters' votes transfer to their next choice. Continue until one candidate has a majority.

IRV is used in Australian federal elections, ranked-choice elections in New York City and Alaska, and was adopted (and later repealed) in Burlington, VT.
Round-by-round with our 9-voter ballot
Round 1: A=4, B=3, C=2. C has fewest — eliminated. C's 2 voters had B as 2nd choice.
Round 2: A=4, B=5. B now has a majority.
Winner: B — different from plurality! IRV elected the candidate who was everyone's second choice or better.
Think about it: The Burlington problem was about monotonicity — a candidate can actually lose by gaining more votes. Can you think of how that could happen?
In the Borda count, each rank earns points. With n candidates, a 1st-place vote is worth n−1 points, 2nd place is n−2, and so on down to 0. The candidate with the most total points wins.

Proposed by Jean-Charles de Borda in 1770. Still used today in some sports awards (NFL MVP, Heisman Trophy) and political elections in several small countries.
Point values: 1st = 2 pts, 2nd = 1 pt, 3rd = 0 pts

A Condorcet winner is a candidate who beats every other candidate in a head-to-head comparison using the preference data. The Condorcet criterion says: if a Condorcet winner exists, they should win the election.

Not all elections have a Condorcet winner (this is called a Condorcet paradox). And several common methods — including plurality and Borda — can fail to elect the Condorcet winner when one exists.
Head-to-head comparisons in our 9-voter example
A vs. B: 4 voters prefer A over B; 5 prefer B over A. → B wins.
A vs. C: 4 prefer A over C; 5 prefer C over A. → C wins.
B vs. C: 7 prefer B over C; 2 prefer C over B. → B wins.
B beats both A and C head-to-head — B is the Condorcet winner. Both IRV and Borda elected B in this example. Plurality did not.
Think about it: Can you construct a 3-candidate election where A beats B, B beats C, and C beats A? (This is called a Condorcet cycle — and it has no winner.)
How do we judge whether a voting method is "fair"? Mathematicians have proposed several criteria — reasonable properties any fair election should satisfy. The four main ones:
Majority Criterion
If a candidate has a majority of first-place votes, they should win.
Condorcet Criterion
If a Condorcet winner exists, they should win the election.
Monotonicity Criterion
Increasing support for a winning candidate should not cause them to lose.
Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives (IIA)
Removing a losing candidate should not change the winner.
Borda count fails the majority and Condorcet criteria. IRV fails monotonicity. Plurality fails Condorcet and IIA. Every common method fails at least one criterion. This brings us to the big question…
In 1951, economist Kenneth Arrow published his doctoral thesis and won the Nobel Prize for it. His result, now called Arrow's Impossibility Theorem, showed that no voting system can satisfy all four fairness criteria simultaneously — as long as there are three or more candidates.
Arrow's Impossibility Theorem (informal): There is no ranked-choice voting method that satisfies the following three conditions at once:

1. No dictator — the election outcome doesn't just reflect one voter's preferences.
2. Pareto efficiency — if every voter prefers A over B, the election result must rank A over B.
3. Independence of irrelevant alternatives — the ranking of A vs. B depends only on voters' opinions about A and B, not on how they feel about C.

Any method satisfying the first two must violate the third.
This is one of the most famous results in social choice theory. It doesn't say elections are hopeless — it says there's no perfect system. Every method involves a tradeoff. The question shifts from "which system is perfect?" to "which imperfection can we live with?"
Think about it: Arrow's theorem applies to ranked voting. Does approval voting (where you vote for as many candidates as you like) escape the theorem? Look it up after you've thought about it.
Approval voting changes the question entirely: instead of ranking candidates, voters approve or disapprove of each one. The candidate with the most approvals wins.

Because it's not a ranked system, it sidesteps Arrow's theorem — but it introduces different problems. The outcome depends heavily on how many candidates voters choose to approve, which itself can be strategic.

Used by the United Nations, IEEE, and several professional societies. Adopted for St. Louis municipal elections in 2020.
Real World — St. Louis: In 2020, St. Louis voters approved a ballot measure replacing plurality primaries with an approval voting system called nonpartisan approval voting. The top two vote-getters advance to a runoff. The first election under this system was held in 2021. Supporters argued it reduces spoiler effects and encourages more candidates to run. Researchers are still studying its long-term effects.
