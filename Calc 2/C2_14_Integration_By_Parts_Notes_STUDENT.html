Integration by Parts

Calc 2  ·  §14  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

U-substitution reverses the chain rule. What rule of differentiation does the formula $\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$ reverse? Can you derive it from the product rule?
The Formula

$$\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du$$

LIATE priority for $u$:
Logarithm, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trig, Exponential

Choose $u$ to be the type that appears earliest in LIATE.

Repeated / Tabular IBP

If $\int v\,du$ still needs IBP, apply it again — or use the tabular method.

Tabular: alternate signs, differentiate $u$ column, integrate $dv$ column; multiply diagonals.

Cyclic case: IBP twice may return the original integral — solve algebraically.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Basic
$\int x\,e^x\,dx$
$u=x$, $dv=e^x\,dx$; $v=e^x$.
$=xe^x-\int e^x\,dx=xe^x-e^x+C=(x-1)e^x+C$
Example 2  —  Log
$\int \ln x\,dx$
$u=\ln x$, $dv=dx$; $du=dx/x$, $v=x$.
$=x\ln x-\int 1\,dx=x\ln x-x+C$
Example 3  —  Tabular
$\int x^2 e^x\,dx$
Tabular: $(x^2,2x,2,0)$ vs $(e^x,e^x,e^x,e^x)$
$=(x^2-2x+2)e^x+C$
Example 4  —  Cyclic
$\int e^x\sin x\,dx$
IBP twice: $I=e^x\sin x - e^x\cos x - I$
$2I=e^x(\sin x-\cos x)\Rightarrow I=\dfrac{e^x(\sin x-\cos x)}{2}+C$
