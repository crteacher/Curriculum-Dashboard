U-Substitution

Calculus 2  ·  §1  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026

Core Ideas
Reversing the Chain Rule The Chain Rule says $\dfrac{d}{dx}f(g(x))=f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$. Reading it backward: $\displaystyle\int f'(g(x))\,g'(x)\,dx = f(g(x))+C$. Set $u=g(x)$ and $du=g'(x)\,dx$ to convert the integral to $\int f'(u)\,du$.
Choosing u Look for a function raised to a power — that function is usually $u$.   If you see $f'(x)/f(x)$, try $u=f(x)$ (gives a logarithm).   If something non-linear sits inside a trig or exponential, try that as $u$.
Definite Integrals Either (a) convert the bounds: replace $x$-bounds with $u$-bounds using $u=g(x)$, or (b) integrate in $u$, back-substitute to $x$, then evaluate at the original bounds.
