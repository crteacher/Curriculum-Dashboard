U-Substitution — Continued

Calc 2  ·  §2  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

Sometimes after substituting $u$ you still have an $x$ left over. What algebra trick could let you handle an integral like $\int x\sqrt{x+1}\,dx$?
Leftover Variable

If after setting $u=g(x)$ an $x$ remains, solve for $x$ from $u=g(x)$ and substitute.

Example: $u=x+1 \Rightarrow x=u-1$
$\int x\sqrt{x+1}\,dx = \int(u-1)\sqrt{u}\,du$

▸ Common Mistake

When a stray $x$ is left after substituting, students integrate a mixture of variables — writing $\int x\sqrt{u}\,du$ and treating the leftover $x$ as if it were a constant. You cannot integrate an expression that contains both $x$ and $u$; every $x$ must be rewritten in terms of $u$ first. From $u=x+1$, solve $x=u-1$, so the integrand becomes $\int(u-1)\sqrt{u}\,du$, entirely in $u$.

The same discipline applies to the differential: $dx$ has to be converted to $du$ before integrating, never carried along as $dx$. A correct $u$-integral has no $x$ and no $dx$ anywhere in it.

Multiply out and integrate term-by-term after solving for $x$ in terms of $u$.

More Patterns

Rational functions with radical:
If $\sqrt{ax+b}$ appears, let $u=ax+b$ (or $u=\sqrt{ax+b}$).

Completing the square can create a recognizable $u$-form for square-root or inverse-trig integrals.

Always simplify the $u$-integral as much as possible before integrating.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Leftover $x$
$\int x\sqrt{x+1}\,dx$
Let $u=x+1$, $x=u-1$, $dx=du$.
$=\int(u-1)u^{1/2}\,du=\int(u^{3/2}-u^{1/2})\,du$
$=\dfrac{2u^{5/2}}{5}-\dfrac{2u^{3/2}}{3}+C=\dfrac{2(x+1)^{5/2}}{5}-\dfrac{2(x+1)^{3/2}}{3}+C$
Example 2  —  Radical denominator
$\int \dfrac{x}{\sqrt{x-3}}\,dx$
Let $u=x-3$, $x=u+3$.
$=\int\dfrac{u+3}{\sqrt{u}}\,du=\int(u^{1/2}+3u^{-1/2})\,du$
$=\dfrac{2u^{3/2}}{3}+6u^{1/2}+C$, back-sub $u=x-3$.
Example 3  —  Nested composition
$\int\dfrac{e^x}{1+e^{2x}}\,dx$
Let $u=e^x$, $du=e^x\,dx$.
$=\int\dfrac{du}{1+u^2}=\arctan(e^x)+C$
Example 4  —  Definite with leftover
$\int_0^3 x\sqrt{9-x^2}\,dx$
Let $u=9-x^2$, $du=-2x\,dx$; limits: $u(0)=9$, $u(3)=0$.
$=-\frac{1}{2}\int_9^0\sqrt{u}\,du=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^9 u^{1/2}\,du=\left[\frac{u^{3/2}}{3}\right]_0^9=9$
