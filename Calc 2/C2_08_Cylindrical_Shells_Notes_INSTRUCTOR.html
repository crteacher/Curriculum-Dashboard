Rotational Solids — Cylindrical Shells

Calc 2  ·  §8  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

Slicing gives disks/washers. But what if you sliced the solid into thin cylindrical shells instead? What would the volume of one thin shell look like?
Shell Formula

Rotating about the $y$-axis:

$$V = 2\pi\int_a^b x\,f(x)\,dx$$

circumference × height × thickness = $2\pi r\cdot h\cdot dx$

The shell method integrates in the same variable as the function. No solving for the inverse is needed.

Proof Sketch

Why $V=2\pi\int_a^b x\,f(x)\,dx$?

Instead of slicing perpendicular to the axis, peel the solid into thin cylindrical shells.

  • Shell at radius $x$, thickness $dx$
  • Height of shell: $f(x)$
  • Circumference: $2\pi x$
  • Volume of thin shell: $dV = 2\pi x \cdot f(x)\,dx$
  • Unroll the shell — it becomes a flat rectangular slab: $2\pi x$ wide, $f(x)$ tall, $dx$ thick
  • Sum all shells: $V=\int_a^b 2\pi x\,f(x)\,dx$

Key insight: the shell method never requires solving for the inverse function.

Worked Example: $y=x^2$ on $[0,2]$ rotated about $y$-axis
Which Method?

Use Shells when: rotating about a vertical axis and function is $y=f(x)$, or when the washer method would require solving for $x$.

Shifted axis $x=k$: radius becomes $|x-k|$
$V=2\pi\int_a^b|x-k|f(x)\,dx$

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Basic shell
Rotate $y=x^2$ on $[0,2]$ about the $y$-axis.
$V=2\pi\int_0^2 x\cdot x^2\,dx=2\pi\int_0^2 x^3\,dx=2\pi\cdot 4=8\pi$
Example 2  —  Shell vs disk
Rotate $y=\sqrt{x}$ on $[0,1]$ about $y$-axis.
$V=2\pi\int_0^1 x\sqrt{x}\,dx=2\pi\int_0^1 x^{3/2}\,dx=2\pi\cdot\dfrac{2}{5}=\dfrac{4\pi}{5}$
Example 3  —  Shifted axis
Rotate $y=x(2-x)$ about $x=3$ on $[0,2]$.
$V=2\pi\int_0^2(3-x)\cdot x(2-x)\,dx=2\pi\int_0^2(6x-5x^2+x^3)\,dx=\dfrac{8\pi}{3}$
Example 4  —  Region between curves
Rotate region between $y=x$ and $y=x^2$ about $y$-axis.
$V=2\pi\int_0^1 x(x-x^2)\,dx=2\pi\int_0^1(x^2-x^3)\,dx=\dfrac{\pi}{6}$
