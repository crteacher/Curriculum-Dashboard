Improper Integrals

Calc 2  ·  §18  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

A definite integral requires a closed, finite interval. What if a bound is $\infty$, or the integrand blows up inside the interval? Can the integral still converge to a finite value?
Two Types

Type I (infinite limits):
$\int_a^\infty f\,dx=\lim_{t\to\infty}\int_a^t f\,dx$

Type II (discontinuity inside):
$\int_a^b f\,dx=\lim_{t\to a^+}\int_t^b f\,dx$ (if $f$ blows up at $a$)

â–¸ Common Mistake

Students plug the bounds straight into the antiderivative via FTC without noticing the integrand is undefined somewhere in $[a,b]$ â€” treating a Type II integral as an ordinary one. For $\int_0^1 x^{-2}\,dx$, blindly applying FTC gives $\left[-\dfrac{1}{x}\right]_0^1=-1-(-\infty)$, and it's tempting to just call this "$\infty$" or fudge an answer, when the real issue is that $x^{-2}$ isn't even continuous on $[0,1]$ (it blows up at $x=0$).

Any time the integrand has a vertical asymptote at or between the limits of integration, FTC doesn't apply directly â€” you must rewrite the integral as a limit approaching the bad point from the correct side, exactly as the Type II definition says, and check whether that limit exists.

$p$-Integral Test

$\displaystyle\int_1^\infty\frac{dx}{x^p}$ converges iff $p>1$.
Value when convergent: $\dfrac{1}{p-1}$

$\displaystyle\int_0^1\frac{dx}{x^p}$ converges iff $p<1$.
Value when convergent: $\dfrac{1}{1-p}$

â–¸ Show Proof

Both claims fall right out of integrating a power and watching the limit. Take $\displaystyle\int_1^\infty x^{-p}\,dx$ and assume $p\neq 1$:

$$\int_1^t x^{-p}\,dx=\left[\frac{x^{1-p}}{1-p}\right]_1^t=\frac{t^{1-p}-1}{1-p}.$$

Now let $t\to\infty$. Everything hinges on the sign of the exponent $1-p$. If $p>1$ then $1-p<0$, so $t^{1-p}\to 0$ and the integral converges to $\dfrac{0-1}{1-p}=\dfrac{1}{p-1}$. If $p<1$ then $1-p>0$, so $t^{1-p}\to\infty$ and it diverges. The borderline $p=1$ gives $\int_1^t x^{-1}\,dx=\ln t\to\infty$ â€” also divergent. So convergence happens exactly when $p>1$.

The interval $[0,1]$ is the mirror image: now the trouble is at $x=0$, where $x^{-p}$ blows up. The same antiderivative gives $\dfrac{1-t^{1-p}}{1-p}$ as $t\to 0^+$. This time $t^{1-p}\to 0$ precisely when $1-p>0$, i.e. $p<1$, leaving the finite value $\dfrac{1}{1-p}$. For $p\geq 1$ that term blows up and the integral diverges.

â–¸ Common Mistake

Students memorize "$p>1$ converges" as a blanket rule and apply it to both integrals â€” but the condition flips depending on where the trouble is. $\int_1^\infty x^{-p}\,dx$ converges for $p>1$ (function must shrink fast enough at infinity), while $\int_0^1 x^{-p}\,dx$ converges for $p<1$ (function must blow up slowly enough near $0$). Using the same inequality direction for both gives the wrong answer roughly half the time.

A quick way to keep them straight: at infinity, bigger $p$ makes $x^{-p}$ smaller faster, which helps convergence â€” so you want $p$ large. Near $0$, bigger $p$ makes $x^{-p}$ blow up faster, which hurts convergence â€” so you want $p$ small. The two rules point in opposite directions because the "danger zone" is in opposite places.

If a limit is $\pm\infty$ or DNE, the integral diverges.
