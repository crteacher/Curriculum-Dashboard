Hyperbolic Functions

Calc 2  ·  §13  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

You've seen $e^x$ and $e^{-x}$ separately. What happens when you add or subtract them? The combinations $\frac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}$ and $\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}$ define two new functions. What properties might they share with $\cos$ and $\sin$?
Definitions & Identities

$\cosh x=\dfrac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}$, $\quad\sinh x=\dfrac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}$

$\cosh^2 x - \sinh^2 x = 1$ (compare: $\cos^2+\sin^2=1$)

$\tanh x=\dfrac{\sinh x}{\cosh x}$

▸ Show Proof

Both functions are built from $e^x$ and $e^{-x}$, so just square the definitions and subtract. The handy fact is that $e^x\cdot e^{-x}=1$, so the cross terms become constants.

$$\cosh^2 x=\frac{(e^x+e^{-x})^2}{4}=\frac{e^{2x}+2+e^{-2x}}{4},\qquad \sinh^2 x=\frac{(e^x-e^{-x})^2}{4}=\frac{e^{2x}-2+e^{-2x}}{4}.$$

Subtract: the $e^{2x}$ and $e^{-2x}$ pieces cancel and only the constants survive:

$$\cosh^2 x-\sinh^2 x=\frac{(e^{2x}+2+e^{-2x})-(e^{2x}-2+e^{-2x})}{4}=\frac{4}{4}=1.$$

This is exactly why the point $(\cosh t,\sinh t)$ rides the hyperbola $x^2-y^2=1$ — which is where the name "hyperbolic" comes from.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

It's tempting to carry over the trig identity and write $\cosh^2 x+\sinh^2 x=1$, but the hyperbolic version has a minus sign: $\cosh^2 x-\sinh^2 x=1$.

The sign traces back to how the functions are built: squaring $\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}$ and $\frac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}$ and subtracting cancels the $e^{2x}$ and $e^{-2x}$ cross terms, leaving a plain constant. Adding instead leaves $\frac{2e^{2x}+2e^{-2x}}{4}$, which isn't a constant at all — a quick way to catch the error is noticing the wrong version still depends on $x$.

Derivatives & Integrals

$\dfrac{d}{dx}\sinh x=\cosh x$    $\dfrac{d}{dx}\cosh x=\sinh x$

$\dfrac{d}{dx}\tanh x=\text{sech}^2 x$

$\int\sinh x\,dx=\cosh x+C$    $\int\cosh x\,dx=\sinh x+C$

$\int\text{sech}^2 x\,dx=\tanh x+C$

▸ Show Proof

These come straight from the definitions and the one rule you already know: $\dfrac{d}{dx}e^x=e^x$, and $\dfrac{d}{dx}e^{-x}=-e^{-x}$ by the chain rule.

Differentiate $\sinh$:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\sinh x=\frac{d}{dx}\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}=\frac{e^x-(-e^{-x})}{2}=\frac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}=\cosh x.$$

Differentiate $\cosh$:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\cosh x=\frac{d}{dx}\frac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}=\frac{e^x+(-e^{-x})}{2}=\frac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}=\sinh x.$$

Notice there is no minus sign here, unlike $\dfrac{d}{dx}\cos x=-\sin x$. The integral formulas are just these read backwards: since differentiating $\cosh$ gives $\sinh$, we get $\int\sinh x\,dx=\cosh x+C$, and likewise for the others.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

When the argument isn't just $x$, it's easy to forget the chain-rule factor. $\int\sinh(3x)\,dx$ is not $\cosh(3x)+C$ — differentiating that back gives $3\sinh(3x)$, three times too big. The correct antiderivative needs the compensating $\frac{1}{3}$ out front: $\frac13\cosh(3x)+C$.

The same slip happens in reverse on the derivative side: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\cosh(5x)=5\sinh(5x)$, not just $\sinh(5x)$ — the inner function's own derivative always has to multiply through.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Evaluate
Find $\cosh(\ln 3)$.
$=\dfrac{e^{\ln 3}+e^{-\ln 3}}{2}=\dfrac{3+1/3}{2}=\dfrac{5}{3}$
Example 2  —  Differentiate
Find $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\cosh(x^2)]$.
$=\sinh(x^2)\cdot 2x=2x\sinh(x^2)$
Example 3  —  Integrate
$\int \sinh(3x)\,dx$
Let $u=3x$: $=\dfrac{\cosh(3x)}{3}+C$
Example 4  —  Identity use
Verify: $\cosh^2 x-\sinh^2 x=1$.
$\left(\dfrac{e^x+e^{-x}}{2}\right)^2-\left(\dfrac{e^x-e^{-x}}{2}\right)^2=\dfrac{4}{4}=1$ ✓
