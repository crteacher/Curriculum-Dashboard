Trig Substitution

Calculus 2  ·  §15  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026

Core Ideas
Three Substitution Forms For $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$: let $x=a\sin\theta$.   For $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$: let $x=a\tan\theta$.   For $\sqrt{x^2-a^2}$: let $x=a\sec\theta$. The goal is to convert a radical into a single trig function using a Pythagorean identity.
Simplified Radicals After $x=a\sin\theta$: $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}=a\cos\theta$.   After $x=a\tan\theta$: $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}=a\sec\theta$.   After $x=a\sec\theta$: $\sqrt{x^2-a^2}=a\tan\theta$.   (Valid for $0\leq\theta\leq\pi/2$.)
Standard Results $\displaystyle\int\frac{dx}{\sqrt{a^2-x^2}}=\arcsin\!\left(\frac{x}{a}\right)+C$.   $\displaystyle\int\frac{dx}{a^2+x^2}=\frac{1}{a}\arctan\!\left(\frac{x}{a}\right)+C$.
Practice Problems

For substitution answers write e.g. x=3sin(t). For simplified radicals write e.g. 3cos(t).
