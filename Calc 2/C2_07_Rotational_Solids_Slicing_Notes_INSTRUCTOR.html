Rotational Solids — Slicing Method

Calc 2  ·  §7  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

When you rotate a curve about an axis, each thin slice of the solid looks like a disk or washer. What changes when the axis of rotation is a line like $y=2$ instead of the $x$-axis?
Radius from a Shifted Axis

The radius is the distance from the curve to the axis.

Axis $y=k$: outer radius $R=|f(x)-k|$

Axis $x=k$: integrate with respect to $y$, $R=|g(y)-k|$

Sketch the region and the axis. Carefully write $R(x)$ and $r(x)$ as absolute distances from the axis to each boundary.

Proof Sketch

Why $V=\pi\int_a^b[R^2-r^2]\,dx$?

Slice the solid at position $x$ into a thin washer of thickness $dx$.

  • Outer radius $R(x)$: distance from axis to outer curve
  • Inner radius $r(x)$: distance from axis to inner curve (0 for a solid disk)
  • Area of washer face: $A(x)=\pi R^2 - \pi r^2$
  • Volume of thin slice: $dV = A(x)\,dx$
  • Sum infinitely many slices: $V=\int_a^b \pi[R(x)^2-r(x)^2]\,dx$

The formula is the area of an annulus integrated over the length of the solid.

▸ Show Proof

Here's why: slice the solid with cuts perpendicular to the axis of rotation, spaced a tiny distance $dx$ apart. Each cut produces a thin slab.

Look at one slab at position $x$. Its flat face is a washer — a big disk of radius $R(x)$ with a smaller disk of radius $r(x)$ punched out of the middle.

The area of that ring is the big circle minus the hole: $A(x)=\pi R(x)^2-\pi r(x)^2=\pi\,[R(x)^2-r(x)^2]$.

The slab has thickness $dx$, so its volume is its face area times its thickness: $dV=\pi\,[R(x)^2-r(x)^2]\,dx$.

Adding up every slab from $x=a$ to $x=b$ is exactly what an integral does, so

$$V=\pi\int_a^b\big[R(x)^2-r(x)^2\big]\,dx.$$

(For a solid with no hole, set $r=0$ and this collapses to the disk method $V=\pi\int_a^b R(x)^2\,dx$.)

∎

Worked Example: $y=x$ and $y=x^2$ rotated about $x$-axis
Washer Setup Steps

1. Sketch and identify inner/outer curves relative to the axis.

2. Write $R(x)$ and $r(x)$ (distances from axis).

3. $V=\pi\int_a^b[R(x)^2-r(x)^2]\,dx$

4. Evaluate and simplify.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Axis $y=1$
Rotate $y=x^2$ and $y=1$ about $y=1$ on the region they enclose.
$R=1-x^2$, $r=0$; intersections at $x=\pm1$.
$V=\pi\int_{-1}^1(1-x^2)^2\,dx=\dfrac{16\pi}{15}$
Example 2  —  Axis $y=-1$
Rotate $y=\sqrt{x}$ on $[0,4]$ about $y=-1$.
$R=\sqrt{x}+1$; $V=\pi\int_0^4(\sqrt{x}+1)^2\,dx=\pi\int_0^4(x+2\sqrt{x}+1)\,dx=\dfrac{68\pi}{3}$
Example 3  —  Axis $x=3$
Rotate $y=x^2$ and $y=x$ about $x=3$.
Integrate w.r.t. $y$; $R=3-y$, $r=3-\sqrt{y}$.
$V=\pi\int_0^1[(3-y)^2-(3-\sqrt{y})^2]\,dy=\dfrac{5\pi}{6}$
Example 4  —  Washer with two curves
Rotate region between $y=x$ and $y=x^2$ about $y=2$.
$R=2-x^2$, $r=2-x$; $[0,1]$.
$V=\pi\int_0^1[(2-x^2)^2-(2-x)^2]\,dx=\dfrac{8\pi}{15}$
