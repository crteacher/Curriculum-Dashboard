Direction Fields

Calc 2  ·  §20  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

We can write a differential equation like $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = y(2-y)$ without being able to solve it algebraically. Is there a way to visualize the behavior of its solutions anyway?
Direction Fields

Given a first-order DE $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = f(x,y)$, its direction field (slope field) is drawn by placing a short line segment at each point $(x,y)$ with slope $f(x,y)$.

Each segment is tangent to whatever solution curve passes through that point. Sketching many segments reveals the shape of the solution family.

Equilibrium Solutions & Stability

For an autonomous DE $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = f(y)$ (no $x$ in $f$), an equilibrium solution is a constant $y = c$ where $f(c) = 0$.

  • Stable: nearby solutions move toward $y = c$
  • Unstable: nearby solutions move away from $y = c$

Horizontal segments in the direction field indicate equilibria.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — $dy/dx = x$
Sketch the direction field. Describe the solutions.
Slopes depend only on $x$ — all points on a vertical line $x = c$ share the same slope $c$. The field fans left (negative slopes for $x<0$) and right (positive for $x>0$). Solutions are $y = \frac{1}{2}x^2 + C$ (upward parabolas).
Example 2 — $dy/dx = 1 - y$
Find any equilibria. Classify as stable or unstable.
Set $1-y=0$: equilibrium at $\mathbf{y=1}$. For $y<1$: $f>0$ (solutions rise toward 1). For $y>1$: $f<0$ (solutions fall toward 1). Stable equilibrium. All solution curves approach $y=1$ as $x\to+\infty$.
Example 3 — $dy/dx = y(2-y)$
Find all equilibria and classify each.
Set $y(2-y)=0$: equilibria at $\mathbf{y=0}$ and $\mathbf{y=2}$. For $0<y<2$: $f>0$ (rising). For $y>2$ or $y<0$: $f<0$ (falling). $y=2$ is stable; $y=0$ is unstable. Logistic growth pattern.
Example 4 — $dy/dx = xy$
Describe the slope pattern quadrant by quadrant. What are the zero-slope curves?
Zero slopes where $x=0$ or $y=0$ (the axes). QI & QIII: $xy>0$ → positive slopes. QII & QIV: $xy<0$ → negative slopes. Solutions are $y = Ce^{x^2/2}$ (grows rapidly for large $|x|$).
Practice Problems
1.
For $dy/dx = -y$: find any equilibria, classify them, and describe what happens to all solution curves as $x \to +\infty$.
Equilibrium at $y=0$. For $y>0$: $f=-y<0$ (decreasing); for $y<0$: $f>0$ (increasing). Stable. All solutions $\to 0$ as $x\to+\infty$.
2.
Find all equilibria of $dy/dx = y^2 - 1$ and classify each as stable or unstable.
$y^2-1=0 \Rightarrow y=\pm 1$. For $y\in(-1,1)$: $f<0$ (decreasing toward $-1$, away from $1$). So $y=1$ is unstable, $y=-1$ is stable.
3.
For $dy/dx = x - 1$: along what vertical line are all slopes zero? For $x > 1$, are solutions increasing or decreasing?
Slopes are zero on $x=1$. For $x>1$: $f=x-1>0$, so solutions are increasing.
4.
A direction field shows horizontal segments along $y = 3$ and $y = -1$, with solutions above $y=3$ increasing and solutions between the two lines also increasing. Which DE is most consistent: (A) $dy/dx = (y-3)(y+1)$ or (B) $dy/dx = (3-y)(y+1)$?
Choice (B) $dy/dx=(3-y)(y+1)$. For $y>3$: $(3-y)<0,(y+1)>0$ so $f<0$ — solutions decrease (correct). Between lines: both factors positive, $f>0$ — solutions increase (correct).
5.
For $dy/dx = \sin(y)$: identify two equilibrium values in $[0,2\pi]$ and determine which is stable.
$\sin(y)=0$ at $y=0$ and $y=\pi$ in $[0,2\pi]$. Near $y=0$: $\sin(y)>0$ for $0<y<\pi$ (solutions move away) → unstable. Near $y=\pi$: solutions approach from both sides → stable.
6.
A population model gives $dy/dx = 0.2y(1 - y/100)$ where $y$ is population. What are the equilibria? Which is stable, and what does it represent?
Equilibria: $y=0$ (unstable) and $y=100$ (stable). $y=100$ is the carrying capacity — the population the environment can sustain long-term.
