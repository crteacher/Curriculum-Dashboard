Area Between Curves

Calc 2  ·  §5  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

You know $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ gives the signed area under $f$. If you have two curves, how could you use integrals to find the area between them?
The Formula

If $f(x)\geq g(x)$ on $[a,b]$:

$$A = \int_a^b [f(x)-g(x)]\,dx$$

Top minus bottom.

▸ Show Proof

Picture the region as a stack of very thin vertical strips, each perpendicular to the $x$-axis. At position $x$ a strip runs from the lower curve $g(x)$ up to the upper curve $f(x)$, so its height is $f(x)-g(x)$.

Give the strip a tiny width $dx$. It is almost a rectangle, so its area is height times width:

$$dA=[f(x)-g(x)]\,dx.$$

Adding every strip from $x=a$ to $x=b$ and letting the widths shrink to zero turns the sum into an integral:

$$A=\int_a^b[f(x)-g(x)]\,dx.$$

Another way to see it: $\int_a^b f\,dx$ is the area under the top curve and $\int_a^b g\,dx$ the area under the bottom curve, so their difference is exactly the area trapped between them.

∎

Always sketch: identify which curve is on top. If they cross, split the integral at the intersection point.

Integrating with Respect to $y$

Sometimes it is easier to integrate horizontally:

$$A = \int_c^d [R(y)-L(y)]\,dy$$

where $R(y)$ = right curve, $L(y)$ = left curve.

▸ Show Proof

This is the same slicing idea turned on its side. Now cut the region into thin horizontal strips, each of thickness $dy$.

At height $y$ a strip stretches from the left boundary $L(y)$ to the right boundary $R(y)$, so its length is $R(y)-L(y)$ and its area is

$$dA=[R(y)-L(y)]\,dy.$$

Summing the strips from $y=c$ to $y=d$ gives $A=\int_c^d[R(y)-L(y)]\,dy$. We reach for this version when the region is easier to describe by its left and right edges than by its top and bottom.

∎

Use $dy$ when the region is better described by horizontal slices, or when the curves are given as $x = f(y)$.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Two parabolas
Find the area between $y=x^2$ and $y=x+2$.
Region between $y=x+2$ (top) and $y=x^2$ (bottom) on $[-1,2]$, meeting at $(-1,1)$ and $(2,4)$.
Intersections: $x^2=x+2\Rightarrow x=-1,2$.
$A=\int_{-1}^2[(x+2)-x^2]\,dx=\left[\dfrac{x^2}{2}+2x-\dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_{-1}^2=\dfrac{9}{2}$
Example 2  —  Line and parabola
Area between $y=4-x^2$ and $y=0$.
$A=\int_{-2}^2(4-x^2)\,dx=\left[4x-\dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_{-2}^2=\dfrac{32}{3}$
Example 3  —  Curves cross
Area between $y=\sin x$ and $y=\cos x$ on $[0,\pi/2]$.
They cross at $x=\pi/4$.
$A=\int_0^{\pi/4}(\cos x-\sin x)\,dx+\int_{\pi/4}^{\pi/2}(\sin x-\cos x)\,dx=2(\sqrt{2}-1)$
Example 4  —  Respect to $y$
Find the area between $x=y^2$ and $x=2-y$.
Intersections: $y^2=2-y\Rightarrow y=-2,1$.
$A=\int_{-2}^1[(2-y)-y^2]\,dy=\dfrac{9}{2}$
