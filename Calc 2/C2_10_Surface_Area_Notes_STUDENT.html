Surface Area of Revolution

Calc 2  ·  §10  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

Arc length measures the length of a curve. When you rotate that curve around an axis, you get a surface. How would you set up an integral to find its area?
Surface Area Formula

Rotating $y=f(x)$ on $[a,b]$ about the $x$-axis:

$$S = 2\pi\int_a^b f(x)\sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}\,dx$$

About the $y$-axis:

$$S = 2\pi\int_a^b x\sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}\,dx$$

The $2\pi r$ factor is the circumference of the ring at each point; $\sqrt{1+(f')^2}\,dx$ is the arc length element.

Key Notes

$f(x)$ must be $\geq 0$ on $[a,b]$ when rotating about the $x$-axis.

Always check whether the integrand simplifies to a perfect square under the radical.

The formula requires arc length setup — compute $f'$ first, then build $1+(f')^2$.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Line — cone check
Surface area of $y=3x$ on $[0,1]$ rotated about $x$-axis.
$f'=3$; $S=2\pi\int_0^1 3x\sqrt{10}\,dx=2\pi\cdot 3\sqrt{10}\cdot\frac{1}{2}=3\pi\sqrt{10}$
Example 2  —  Sphere check
Surface area of $y=\sqrt{r^2-x^2}$ from $-r$ to $r$.
$f'=\dfrac{-x}{\sqrt{r^2-x^2}}$; $\sqrt{1+(f')^2}=\dfrac{r}{\sqrt{r^2-x^2}}$
$S=2\pi\int_{-r}^r r\,dx=4\pi r^2$ ✓
Example 3  —  Curve rotation
Surface area of $y=x^3/3$ on $[0,1]$ about $x$-axis.
$f'=x^2$; $S=2\pi\int_0^1 \dfrac{x^3}{3}\sqrt{1+x^4}\,dx$
Let $u=1+x^4$: $S=\dfrac{\pi}{6}\left[(2^{3/2}-1)\right]=\dfrac{\pi(2\sqrt{2}-1)}{6}$
Example 4  —  About $y$-axis
Surface area of $y=x^2$ on $[0,2]$ about $y$-axis.
$S=2\pi\int_0^2 x\sqrt{1+4x^2}\,dx$; let $u=1+4x^2$:
$=\dfrac{\pi}{6}\left[17^{3/2}-1\right]$
