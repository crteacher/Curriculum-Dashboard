Arc Length

Calc 2  ·  §9  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

A straight line's length is easy to measure. But how would you measure the length of a curve like $y=x^2$ from $x=0$ to $x=2$? What small-scale approximation might help?
Arc Length Formula

For $y=f(x)$ on $[a,b]$:

$$L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}\,dx$$

For $x=g(y)$ on $[c,d]$:

$$L = \int_c^d \sqrt{1+[g'(y)]^2}\,dy$$

Derived from the Pythagorean theorem applied to infinitesimal segments: $dL=\sqrt{dx^2+dy^2}$.

Strategy

1. Differentiate $f(x)$ to get $f'(x)$.

2. Form $1+[f'(x)]^2$ and simplify — try to get a perfect square under the radical.

3. Integrate. Many arc length integrals require $u$-sub or are difficult to evaluate exactly.

A perfect square under the radical is the key trick — e.g., $1+(\frac{3}{2}x^{1/2})^2=1+\frac{9x}{4}$.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Simple case
Find the length of $y=\dfrac{2}{3}x^{3/2}$ on $[0,3]$.
$f'=x^{1/2}$; $1+(x^{1/2})^2=1+x$.
$L=\int_0^3\sqrt{1+x}\,dx=\left[\dfrac{2}{3}(1+x)^{3/2}\right]_0^3=\dfrac{14}{3}$
Example 2  —  Perfect square
Length of $y=\dfrac{x^3}{6}+\dfrac{1}{2x}$ on $[1,3]$.
$f'=\dfrac{x^2}{2}-\dfrac{1}{2x^2}$; $1+(f')^2=\left(\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\dfrac{1}{2x^2}\right)^2$
$L=\int_1^3\left(\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\dfrac{1}{2x^2}\right)dx=\dfrac{17}{6}+\ln 3$
Example 3  —  With respect to $y$
Length of $x=\dfrac{y^2}{2}$ from $y=0$ to $y=2$.
$g'=y$; $L=\int_0^2\sqrt{1+y^2}\,dy$
$=\left[\dfrac{y\sqrt{1+y^2}}{2}+\dfrac{\ln|y+\sqrt{1+y^2}|}{2}\right]_0^2=\sqrt{5}+\dfrac{\ln(2+\sqrt{5})}{2}$
Example 4  —  Parametric setup
Set up (do not evaluate) arc length of $y=\ln(\cos x)$ on $[0,\pi/4]$.
$f'=-\tan x$; $L=\int_0^{\pi/4}\sqrt{1+\tan^2 x}\,dx=\int_0^{\pi/4}\sec x\,dx=\ln(1+\sqrt{2})$
