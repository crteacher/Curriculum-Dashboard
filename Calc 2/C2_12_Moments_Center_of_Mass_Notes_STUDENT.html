Moments and Center of Mass

Calc 2  ·  §12  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

We know integration finds area. Can it also locate the balance point — the centroid — of a flat region?
Mass & Moments of a Lamina

For a uniform lamina over $a \le x \le b$ under $y=f(x)\ge 0$ with density $\rho$:

$m = \rho\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$

$M_y = \rho\displaystyle\int_a^b x\,f(x)\,dx$

$M_x = \dfrac{\rho}{2}\displaystyle\int_a^b [f(x)]^2\,dx$

▸ Show Proof

A moment measures turning power: a tiny piece of mass $dm$ sitting a distance $r$ from an axis contributes $r\,dm$. The total moment is the sum of all these, and the total mass is the sum of the $dm$'s. Slice the lamina into thin vertical strips at position $x$.

The strip from $x$ to $x+dx$ is almost a rectangle of height $f(x)$ and width $dx$, so its area is $f(x)\,dx$ and its mass is $dm=\rho f(x)\,dx$. Summing gives $m=\rho\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$.

The strip's distance from the $y$-axis is simply $x$, so its moment about that axis is $x\,dm=\rho x f(x)\,dx$. Summing gives $M_y=\rho\int_a^b x f(x)\,dx$.

For $M_x$ we need each strip's distance from the $x$-axis. The strip is tall, but a uniform strip balances at its midpoint, height $f(x)/2$. So its moment about the $x$-axis is $\dfrac{f(x)}{2}\,dm=\dfrac{f(x)}{2}\cdot\rho f(x)\,dx=\dfrac{\rho}{2}[f(x)]^2\,dx$. That midpoint is where the factor of one-half comes from. Summing:

$$M_x=\frac{\rho}{2}\int_a^b [f(x)]^2\,dx.$$

∎

Centroid (Center of Mass)

$\bar x = \dfrac{M_y}{m} = \dfrac{\displaystyle\int_a^b x\,f(x)\,dx}{\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx}$

$\bar y = \dfrac{M_x}{m} = \dfrac{\frac{1}{2}\displaystyle\int_a^b [f(x)]^2\,dx}{\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx}$

Density $\rho$ cancels for uniform laminas.

▸ Show Proof

The centroid is the balance point: support the lamina there and all the turning power cancels out. Think of the lever law — a mass $dm$ at position $x$, measured from the balance point $\bar x$, exerts a moment $(x-\bar x)\,dm$, and at balance these add to zero:

$$\int (x-\bar x)\,dm=0.$$

Split the integral: $\displaystyle\int x\,dm-\bar x\int dm=0$. The first piece is exactly the moment $M_y$, and $\int dm$ is the total mass $m$, so $M_y-\bar x\,m=0$.

Solving gives $\bar x=\dfrac{M_y}{m}$. Running the same argument about the $x$-axis gives $\bar y=\dfrac{M_x}{m}$.

For a uniform lamina every moment and the mass each carry one factor of $\rho$, so it cancels top and bottom — the centroid depends only on the shape, not the density.

∎

Region Between Two Curves

For the region between $f(x) \ge g(x)$ on $[a,b]$, area $A$:

$\bar x = \dfrac{1}{A}\displaystyle\int_a^b x[f(x)-g(x)]\,dx$

$\bar y = \dfrac{1}{2A}\displaystyle\int_a^b[f(x)^2 - g(x)^2]\,dx$

Example Region: $y = x^2$ on $[0,2]$

Practice Problems
1.
Find $\bar x$ for the region under $y=2x$ on $[0,3]$.
$A=9$; $\bar x=2$
2.
Find the centroid of the region under $y=e^x$ on $[0,1]$.
$\bar x=\frac{1}{e-1}\approx 0.582$; $\bar y=\frac{e+1}{4}\approx 0.930$
3.
Find $\bar y$ for the region between $y=x$ and $y=x^2$ on $[0,1]$.
$\bar y=3\int_0^1(x^2-x^4)\,dx=\frac{2}{5}$
4.
Explain without computing why $\bar x=0$ for any region symmetric about the $y$-axis.
$xf(x)$ is odd on $[-a,a]$, so $M_y=0$ and $\bar x=0$.
5.
Find the centroid of the triangle with vertices $(0,0)$, $(4,0)$, $(0,2)$.
Average of vertices: $\left(\frac{4}{3},\frac{2}{3}\right)$
6.
A lamina covers $1\le x\le 3$ under $y=1/x$. Find $\bar x$.
$\bar x=\frac{2}{\ln 3}\approx 1.82$
