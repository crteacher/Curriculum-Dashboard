Taylor Series

Calc 2  ·  §29  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

Power series let us represent $\frac{1}{1-x}$ as an infinite polynomial. Can we do the same for $\sin x$, $e^x$, or any differentiable function — and if so, how do we find the coefficients?
Taylor & Maclaurin Series

If $f$ has derivatives of all orders at $x=a$, its Taylor series centered at $a$ is:

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n = f(a)+f'(a)(x-a)+\frac{f''(a)}{2!}(x-a)^2+\cdots$$

When $a=0$ this is called a Maclaurin series.

The $n$th partial sum $T_n(x)$ is the degree-$n$ Taylor polynomial.

▸ Show Proof

Why those coefficients $\dfrac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}$? Suppose $f$ really does equal some power series centered at $a$:

$$f(x)=c_0+c_1(x-a)+c_2(x-a)^2+c_3(x-a)^3+\cdots$$

We just need to pin down each $c_n$, and two tools do it: plugging in $x=a$, and differentiating.

Set $x=a$: every term carrying a factor of $(x-a)$ dies, leaving $f(a)=c_0$.

Differentiate once: $f'(x)=c_1+2c_2(x-a)+3c_3(x-a)^2+\cdots$; setting $x=a$ gives $f'(a)=c_1$.

Differentiate again: $f''(x)=2c_2+(3\cdot 2)c_3(x-a)+\cdots$, so $f''(a)=2c_2$, i.e. $c_2=\dfrac{f''(a)}{2}$.

Each extra derivative pulls a factor down from the exponent, so after $n$ derivatives the constant term is $n!\,c_n$. Setting $x=a$ gives $f^{(n)}(a)=n!\,c_n$, hence

$$c_n=\frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}.$$

∎

▸ Common Mistake

Students often write the Taylor series formula with $x^n$ instead of $(x-a)^n$ when $a \neq 0$ — i.e. they forget the series is centered at $a$, not at $0$. Only a Maclaurin series ($a=0$) collapses $(x-a)^n$ down to $x^n$.

A second common error: treating the Taylor series as equal to $f(x)$ everywhere, when it's only guaranteed to equal $f(x)$ inside the radius of convergence $R$. Outside that interval the series may diverge or converge to something else entirely.

Key Maclaurin Series
  • $e^x = \displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{x^n}{n!}$   ($R=\infty$)
  • $\sin x = \displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}$   ($R=\infty$)
  • $\cos x = \displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!}$   ($R=\infty$)
  • $\dfrac{1}{1-x} = \displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}x^n$   ($R=1$)
  • $\ln(1+x) = \displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{(-1)^{n-1}x^n}{n}$   ($R=1$)
Worked Examples
Example 1 — Deriving $e^x$
Find the Maclaurin series for $f(x)=e^x$ by computing derivatives.
$f^{(n)}(x)=e^x$, so $f^{(n)}(0)=1$ for all $n$. Thus $e^x=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{x^n}{n!}$. Ratio test gives $R=\infty$.
Example 2 — Deriving $\sin x$
Find the Maclaurin series for $\sin x$.
Derivatives cycle: $\sin,\cos,-\sin,-\cos,\ldots$ Values at $0$: $0,1,0,-1,\ldots$ Only odd terms survive: $\sin x=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}$.
Example 3 — Taylor Polynomial
Write the degree-4 Taylor polynomial $T_4(x)$ for $e^x$ centered at $a=0$.
$T_4(x)=1+x+\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\dfrac{x^3}{6}+\dfrac{x^4}{24}$. Use it to approximate $e^{0.5}\approx T_4(0.5)\approx 1.6484$.
Example 4 — Substitution
Use a known series to write the Maclaurin series for $\cos(x^2)$.
Replace $x$ with $x^2$ in the $\cos$ series: $\cos(x^2)=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{(-1)^n x^{4n}}{(2n)!}=1-\dfrac{x^4}{2}+\dfrac{x^8}{24}-\cdots$
Practice Problems
1.
Write the first four nonzero terms of the Maclaurin series for $\cos x$ from the definition.
$1-\dfrac{x^2}{2!}+\dfrac{x^4}{4!}-\dfrac{x^6}{6!}$
2.
Find $T_3(x)$ for $f(x)=\ln(1+x)$ centered at $a=0$.
$x-\dfrac{x^2}{2}+\dfrac{x^3}{3}$
3.
Find the Maclaurin series for $e^{-x}$.
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{(-1)^n x^n}{n!}$
4.
Use the $e^x$ series to estimate $e^{0.1}$ — keep terms through $x^3$. Round to 4 decimal places.
$1+0.1+\frac{0.01}{2}+\frac{0.001}{6}\approx 1.1052$
5.
Use a known series to write the first three nonzero terms of the Maclaurin series for $\sin(3x)$.
$3x-\dfrac{27x^3}{6}+\dfrac{243x^5}{120}=3x-\dfrac{9x^3}{2}+\dfrac{81x^5}{40}$
6.
Find the Maclaurin series for $\dfrac{1}{1+x^2}$ using the geometric series formula.
Replace $x$ with $-x^2$: $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^n x^{2n}$, $R=1$
