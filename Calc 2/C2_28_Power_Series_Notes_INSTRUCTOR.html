Power Series

Calc 2  ·  §28  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

The geometric series $\dfrac{1}{1-x}=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}x^n$ is valid only for $|x|<1$. What if we replace $x$ by $(x-a)$ and ask: for which values of $x$ does the series converge?
Power Series Basics

A power series centered at $a$ is $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}c_n(x-a)^n$.

Every power series has a radius of convergence $R\ge 0$:

  • Converges absolutely for $|x-a|
  • Diverges for $|x-a|>R$
  • Endpoints $x=a\pm R$: check separately

Find $R$: $\dfrac{1}{R}=\lim\left|\dfrac{c_{n+1}}{c_n}\right|$

▸ Show Proof

Where does the formula $\dfrac{1}{R}=\lim\left|\dfrac{c_{n+1}}{c_n}\right|$ come from? Apply the ratio test to the power series $\sum c_n(x-a)^n$, treating the whole term $c_n(x-a)^n$ as the thing being summed:

$$\left|\frac{c_{n+1}(x-a)^{n+1}}{c_n(x-a)^n}\right|=\left|\frac{c_{n+1}}{c_n}\right|\,|x-a|\;\longrightarrow\;\ell\,|x-a|,$$

where $\ell=\lim\left|\dfrac{c_{n+1}}{c_n}\right|$ is the limit of the coefficient ratios. The ratio test promises absolute convergence whenever this stays below $1$:

$$\ell\,|x-a|<1\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad |x-a|<\frac{1}{\ell}.$$

So the series converges exactly for $|x-a|$ smaller than $\dfrac{1}{\ell}$ and diverges past it — which is precisely what it means for the radius of convergence to be $R=\dfrac{1}{\ell}$, i.e. $\dfrac{1}{R}=\ell$. (If $\ell=0$ it converges for all $x$, so $R=\infty$; if $\ell=\infty$, only at $x=a$.)

∎

Operations on Power Series

Within the interval of convergence, power series behave like polynomials:

Differentiation: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\displaystyle\sum c_n(x-a)^n=\sum nc_n(x-a)^{n-1}$

Integration: $\displaystyle\int\sum c_n(x-a)^n\,dx=\sum\dfrac{c_n(x-a)^{n+1}}{n+1}+C$

Key: $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}x^n=\dfrac{1}{1-x}$ for $|x|<1$.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Radius of Convergence
Find the radius of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{x^n}{n!}$.
$\left|\dfrac{x^{n+1}/(n+1)!}{x^n/n!}\right|=\dfrac{|x|}{n+1}\to 0$ for all $x$. $R=\infty$.
Example 2  —  Finite Radius
Find the interval of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\dfrac{x^n}{n}$.
Ratio test: $L=|x|$. Converges for $|x|<1$. Endpoints: $x=1$ diverges (harmonic); $x=-1$ converges (AST). $[-1,1)$.
Example 3  —  Differentiation
Use the geometric series to find a power series for $\dfrac{1}{(1-x)^2}$.
$\dfrac{d}{dx}\dfrac{1}{1-x}=\dfrac{1}{(1-x)^2}=\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}nx^{n-1}$, $|x|<1$.
Example 4  —  Integration
Find a power series for $\ln(1+x)$.
$\dfrac{1}{1+x}=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^n x^n$. Integrate: $\ln(1+x)=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{(-1)^n x^{n+1}}{n+1}$, $|x|\le 1$.
Practice Problems
1.
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{(x-2)^n}{3^n}$ — find $R$ and interval
$R=3$; interval $(-1,5)$
2.
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}n\,x^n$ — find $R$
$R=1$
3.
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(-1)^n\dfrac{x^{2n+1}}{2n+1}$ — what function?
$\arctan x$
4.
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{(3x)^n}{n!}$ — find $R$
$R=\infty$; equals $e^{3x}$
5.
Differentiate $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}$ to get a known series
$\sum x^n = \dfrac{1}{1-x}$
6.
$\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}n^2 x^n$ — converge or diverge at $x=1$?
Diverges ($a_n=n^2\to\infty$)
