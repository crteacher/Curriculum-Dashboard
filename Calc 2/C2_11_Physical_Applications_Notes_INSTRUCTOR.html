Physical Applications of Integration

Calc 2  ·  §11  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

Integrals give area and volume, but physics also uses integration. If force varies with position, how would you find the total work done moving an object?
Work

When force $F(x)$ varies: $W=\int_a^b F(x)\,dx$

Spring (Hooke's Law): $F=kx$, so $W=\int_0^d kx\,dx=\dfrac{kd^2}{2}$

$k$ = spring constant (N/m), $d$ = displacement from natural length

▸ Show Proof

Start from the one fact you already know: when a constant force $F$ pushes an object a distance $\Delta x$, the work is $W=F\cdot\Delta x$.

When the force changes along the way, cut the trip from $a$ to $b$ into many tiny steps of width $dx$. Over one tiny step the force barely changes, so it is essentially the constant $F(x)$, and the work for that step is $dW=F(x)\,dx$.

Adding all the steps and letting them shrink turns the sum into an integral:

$$W=\int_a^b F(x)\,dx.$$

For a spring, Hooke's Law says the force needed to hold it stretched a distance $x$ is $F=kx$. Substituting and integrating from $0$ to $d$:

$$W=\int_0^d kx\,dx=k\cdot\frac{x^2}{2}\Big|_0^d=\frac{kd^2}{2}.$$

∎

Hydrostatic Force & Mass

Hydrostatic Force: $F=\int_a^b \rho g\, w(x)\, d(x)\,dx$
where $w(x)$ = width at depth $x$, $d(x)$ = depth, $\rho g\approx 9800$ N/m³

Mass with density: $m=\int_a^b \rho(x)\,dx$

▸ Show Proof

This is the hydrostatic-force formula. The key physical fact is that water pressure grows with depth: at depth $d$ the pressure is $P=\rho g\,d$ — the weight of the column of water resting on each unit of area.

Pressure pushes on every part of a submerged surface, but because it changes with depth we slice the surface into thin horizontal strips. A strip at depth $x$ has width $w(x)$ and tiny height $dx$, so its area is $w(x)\,dx$.

Across one thin strip the depth barely changes, so the pressure there is essentially $\rho g\,d(x)$. Since force $=$ pressure $\times$ area,

$$dF=\rho g\,d(x)\,w(x)\,dx.$$

Summing every strip from $a$ to $b$ gives the total force:

$$F=\int_a^b \rho g\,w(x)\,d(x)\,dx.$$

∎

Set up coordinates carefully: depth is measured downward from the surface.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Spring work
A spring requires 40 J to stretch 0.2 m. Find $k$ and the work to stretch it 0.3 m.
$40=\frac{k(0.2)^2}{2}\Rightarrow k=2000$ N/m.
$W=\int_0^{0.3}2000x\,dx=90$ J
Example 2  —  Lifting a cable
A 10-m cable weighing 20 N/m hangs from a building. Find work to lift it to the top.
Slice at height $x$ from bottom: $W=\int_0^{10}20x\,dx=1000$ J
Example 3  —  Hydrostatic force
Find force on a vertical 2 m × 3 m rectangle submerged with top edge at surface.
$F=\rho g\int_0^3 2x\,dx=9800\cdot 9=88200$ N
Example 4  —  Mass density
A rod from $x=0$ to $x=4$ has density $\rho(x)=2+x$ kg/m. Find total mass.
$m=\int_0^4(2+x)\,dx=\left[2x+\dfrac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^4=16$ kg
