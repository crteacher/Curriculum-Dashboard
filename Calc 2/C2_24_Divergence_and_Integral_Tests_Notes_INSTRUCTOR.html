Divergence & Integral Tests

Calc 2  ·  §24  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

The $n$th-term test only detects divergence. To prove convergence, we need stronger tests. If the terms $a_n=f(n)$ for a decreasing positive function $f$, how does $\int_1^\infty f\,dx$ relate to $\sum a_n$?
Integral Test

If $f$ is continuous, positive, decreasing on $[1,\infty)$ and $a_n=f(n)$:

$\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n$ and $\int_1^\infty f(x)\,dx$ both converge or both diverge.

$p$-series: $\sum\dfrac{1}{n^p}$ converges iff $p>1$.

▸ Show Proof

Why do $\sum a_n$ and $\int_1^\infty f\,dx$ live or die together when $f$ is positive and decreasing with $a_n=f(n)$? Draw the curve $y=f(x)$ and build rectangles of width $1$ against it.

Rectangles under the curve. On each interval $[n,n+1]$ use a rectangle of height $f(n+1)$ — the value at the right end, which is the smallest height there because $f$ decreases. These sit below the curve, so their total area is at most the area under it:

$$a_2+a_3+a_4+\cdots\;\le\;\int_1^\infty f(x)\,dx.$$

If the integral is finite, the left-hand sum is trapped below a finite number — the series converges.

Rectangles over the curve. Now use height $f(n)$ — the value at the left end, the largest height. These cover the curve, so

$$\int_1^\infty f(x)\,dx\;\le\;a_1+a_2+a_3+\cdots.$$

If the integral is infinite, the series is even bigger and diverges. The two inequalities pin the sum and the integral to the same fate. Applying this to $f(x)=1/x^p$ — whose integral converges exactly when $p>1$ — hands us the $p$-series rule for free.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

When applying the $p$-series rule $\sum 1/n^p$, students often round $p=1$ into the "converges" bucket because it feels like the boundary case should be included, writing something like "converges for $p\geq 1$." It doesn't: $p=1$ is the harmonic series $\sum 1/n$, and it diverges (the corresponding integral $\int_1^\infty dx/x = \ln x\big|_1^\infty=\infty$ is infinite). The rule is strictly $p>1$ for convergence — the boundary belongs to the divergent side, not the convergent one.

This mistake usually comes from pattern-matching to other tests where boundary cases are ambiguous, rather than actually checking what the integral test says at $p=1$: run the improper integral yourself instead of guessing which side $p=1$ lands on.

Using the Tests

1. Verify $f$ is positive, continuous, decreasing.

2. Evaluate $\int_1^\infty f(x)\,dx$.

3. Conclude convergence/divergence of the series (not its value).

Note: The integral value $\neq$ the series sum.

▸ Common Mistake

After running the integral test and confirming convergence, students sometimes report the value of the integral as if it were the sum of the series — e.g., concluding $\sum_{n=1}^\infty 1/n^2$ "equals" $\int_1^\infty dx/x^2 = 1$. It doesn't: the integral test only tells you whether the series converges, never what it converges to. The actual sum $\sum 1/n^2 = \pi^2/6\approx1.645$ isn't even close to the integral's value of $1$.

The two quantities aren't supposed to match — the rectangles used to build the integral's bounds sit strictly above or below the curve, so the integral traces a rough boundary, not a running total of the series.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  $p$-series
Does $\sum_{n=1}^\infty\dfrac{1}{n^3}$ converge?
$p=3>1$ → converges ($p$-series test). (Actual value = $\pi^2/6$... wait, that's $p=2$. Sum $=\zeta(3)$.)
Example 2  —  Integral test
Test $\sum\dfrac{1}{n^2+1}$.
$\int_1^\infty\dfrac{dx}{x^2+1}=\arctan x\big|_1^\infty=\dfrac{\pi}{4}$ (finite) → converges
Example 3  —  $p=1$ harmonic
Does $\sum\dfrac{1}{n}$ converge?
$\int_1^\infty\frac{1}{x}\,dx=\ln x\big|_1^\infty=\infty$ → diverges
Example 4  —  Log series
Test $\sum_{n=2}^\infty\dfrac{1}{n\ln n}$.
$\int_2^\infty\dfrac{dx}{x\ln x}=\ln(\ln x)\big|_2^\infty=\infty$ → diverges
