Modeling with Linear Functions

Pre-Calculus  ·  §2.3  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

If demand for a product drops by 15 units for every \$1 price increase, what kind of function models demand vs. price? What does the slope mean in context?
Building Linear Models

A linear function has the form $f(x)=mx+b$.

$m$ = constant rate of change (slope)
$b$ = initial value ($y$-intercept, value when $x=0$)

Identifying linearity: if average rate of change is constant across all intervals, the function is linear.

▸ Show Proof

Here is why a constant rate of change forces the formula $f(x)=mx+b$. Suppose that between the starting input $x=0$ and any other input $x$, the average rate of change always equals the same number $m$:

$$\frac{f(x)-f(0)}{x-0}=m.$$

Multiply both sides by $x$:

$$f(x)-f(0)=mx.$$

Now solve for $f(x)$ and rename the starting value $f(0)$ as $b$:

$$f(x)=mx+b.$$

So “constant rate of change” and “looks like $mx+b$” say exactly the same thing — the rate $m$ becomes the slope, and the starting value $b$ becomes the $y$-intercept.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

Using a positive slope for a quantity that's decreasing. In $f(x)=mx+b$, $m$ is the signed rate of change, so "loses \$1{,}750 per year" or "drops 15 units per \$1" makes $m$ negative ($m=-1750$, $m=-15$). Students record the size of the rate and forget the sign, so their model rises when it should fall.

Keep $m$ and $b$ straight too: $b$ is the value at $x=0$ (the starting amount), not just "the other number in the problem." Decide which quantity is the per-unit rate (that's $m$) and which is the initial value (that's $b$).

Steps to build a model: identify what $x$ and $f(x)$ represent, find $m$ from context, find $b$ from an initial value.

Applications and Intersections

Supply/Demand models where slope is given:
Demand decreasing with price: negative slope.
Supply increasing with price: positive slope.

Finding intersection of $f(x)=mx+b$ and $g(x)=nx+c$:
Set equal: $mx+b=nx+c$, solve for $x$, then find $y$.

▸ Common Mistake

Stopping at $x$ when the question asks for the intersection point. Solving $mx+b=nx+c$ gives only the $x$-coordinate where the lines meet; the point is $(x,y)$, so substitute that $x$ back into either function to get $y$. Reporting just $x$ is half an answer.

Don't confuse this with setting one function to zero. $f(x)=g(x)$ finds where the two graphs cross each other; $f(x)=0$ finds where one graph crosses the $x$-axis — different questions.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Car depreciation
A car costs $\$35{,}000$ and loses $\$1{,}750$ per year. Write $V(t)$ and find when it is worth $\$14{,}000$.
$V(t)=35000-1750t$. Set $=14000$: $t=12$ years.
Example 2 — Widget cost
A factory costs $\$800$/day and \$2 per widget produced. Write $C(w)$ for daily cost.
$C(w)=800+2w$. E.g. $C(500)=\$1800$.
Example 3 — Demand model
Demand is 400 units at $\$10$ and drops 15 per $\$1$ increase. Write $D(p)$.
Slope $=-15$, point $(10,400)$: $D(p)=-15(p-10)+400=-15p+550$.
Example 4 — Intersection of two models
Hertz: $C_1(x)=50+0.15x$. Budget: $C_2(x)=85$. Find the break-even mileage $x$.
$50+0.15x=85\Rightarrow 0.15x=35\Rightarrow x\approx 233$ miles.
Practice Problems
1.
A phone plan costs $\$25$/month plus $\$0.10$ per text. Write $C(n)$ and find $C(200)$.
$C(n)=25+0.1n$; $C(200)=\$45$.
2.
Is the data linear? $x$: 0,1,2,3; $f(x)$: 4,7,10,13.
Differences are all 3 — yes, linear with slope 3.
3.
Demand is 500 at $\$14$ and 510 at $\$13$. Write $D(p)$.
$m=\dfrac{510-500}{13-14}=-10$; $D(p)=-10(p-14)+500=-10p+640$.
4.
Two lines: $y=3x+1$ and $y=-x+9$. Find their intersection.
$3x+1=-x+9\Rightarrow x=2, y=7$. Point $(2,7)$.
5.
A population of 29{,}000 grows by 1{,}700/yr. Write $P(t)$ and find $P$ after 5 yr.
$P(t)=29000+1700t$; $P(5)=37{,}500$.
6.
Average rate of change of $f(x)=4x+2$ from $x=1$ to $x=5$.
ARC $= m = 4$.
