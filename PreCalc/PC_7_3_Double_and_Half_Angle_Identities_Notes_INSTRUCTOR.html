$\sin(2x)$ and $2\sin(x)$ are not the same function. Can you derive the formula for $\sin(2x)$ from the sum formula you already know?
Double Angle Formulas

Derived from sum formulas with $A=B=\theta$:

$\sin(2\theta)=2\sin\theta\cos\theta$

$\cos(2\theta)=\cos^2\theta-\sin^2\theta$
$\phantom{\cos(2\theta)}=1-2\sin^2\theta$
$\phantom{\cos(2\theta)}=2\cos^2\theta-1$

$\tan(2\theta)=\dfrac{2\tan\theta}{1-\tan^2\theta}$

âẁ Show Proof

The double angle formulas are just the sum formulas with $B$ set equal to $A$ â nothing new to prove, just substitution.

Start from $\sin(A+B)=\sin A\cos B+\cos A\sin B$. Setting $A=B=\theta$ gives $$\sin(2\theta)=\sin\theta\cos\theta+\cos\theta\sin\theta=2\sin\theta\cos\theta.$$

Similarly, start from $\cos(A+B)=\cos A\cos B-\sin A\sin B$. Setting $A=B=\theta$ gives $$\cos(2\theta)=\cos^2\theta-\sin^2\theta.$$

The other two forms of $\cos(2\theta)$ come from substituting the Pythagorean identity $\sin^2\theta=1-\cos^2\theta$ (or $\cos^2\theta=1-\sin^2\theta$) into that result: $\cos^2\theta-(1-\cos^2\theta)=2\cos^2\theta-1$, and $(1-\sin^2\theta)-\sin^2\theta=1-2\sin^2\theta$.

For tangent, $\tan(2\theta)=\dfrac{\sin(2\theta)}{\cos(2\theta)}=\dfrac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{\cos^2\theta-\sin^2\theta}$. Divide top and bottom by $\cos^2\theta$ to get $$\tan(2\theta)=\dfrac{2\tan\theta}{1-\tan^2\theta}.$$

Half Angle Formulas

Solve $\cos(2\theta)=2\cos^2\theta-1$ for $\cos\theta$:

$\cos\dfrac{\theta}{2}=\pm\sqrt{\dfrac{1+\cos\theta}{2}}$

$\sin\dfrac{\theta}{2}=\pm\sqrt{\dfrac{1-\cos\theta}{2}}$

$\tan\dfrac{\theta}{2}=\dfrac{\sin\theta}{1+\cos\theta}=\dfrac{1-\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}$

Sign chosen by the quadrant of $\theta/2$.

âẁ Show Proof

The half-angle formulas come from solving a double-angle formula backwards.

Start from $\cos(2\theta)=2\cos^2\theta-1$. Replace $\theta$ with $\theta/2$ everywhere â a valid move, since the identity holds for every angle: $$\cos\theta=2\cos^2\dfrac{\theta}{2}-1.$$

Solve for $\cos^2(\theta/2)$: $\cos^2\dfrac{\theta}{2}=\dfrac{1+\cos\theta}{2}$, so $$\cos\dfrac{\theta}{2}=\pm\sqrt{\dfrac{1+\cos\theta}{2}}.$$ The $\pm$ shows up because squaring lost the sign â you pick whichever sign matches the quadrant $\theta/2$ actually lands in.

The same trick starting from $\cos(2\theta)=1-2\sin^2\theta$ gives $\sin^2(\theta/2)=\dfrac{1-\cos\theta}{2}$, so $$\sin\dfrac{\theta}{2}=\pm\sqrt{\dfrac{1-\cos\theta}{2}}.$$

Dividing the sine version by the cosine version gives $\tan(\theta/2)$ as a ratio of square roots. Multiplying top and bottom by matching factors (using $\sin\theta=2\sin(\theta/2)\cos(\theta/2)$ and $\sin^2\theta=1-\cos^2\theta$) clears the square roots, leaving the sign-free forms $\tan\dfrac{\theta}{2}=\dfrac{\sin\theta}{1+\cos\theta}=\dfrac{1-\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}$.
