Law of Cosines

Pre-Calculus  ·  §8.2  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

The Law of Sines needs at least one angle. What if you only know three sides (SSS) or two sides and the angle between them (SAS)? What new law handles those cases?
The Law of Cosines

$$a^2=b^2+c^2-2bc\cos A$$

$$b^2=a^2+c^2-2ac\cos B$$

$$c^2=a^2+b^2-2ab\cos C$$

Use when: SAS (two sides + included angle) or SSS (all three sides).

Note: if $A=90°$, reduces to Pythagorean theorem.

▸ Show Proof

Set up coordinates so angle $A$ sits at the origin, with side $b$ (which connects $A$ and $C$) lying along the positive $x$-axis. Then $A=(0,0)$ and $C=(b,0)$.

Vertex $B$ is a distance $c$ from $A$, at angle $A$ above the $x$-axis, so $B=(c\cos A,\,c\sin A)$.

Side $a$ is the distance from $B$ to $C$. By the distance formula: $$a^2=(c\cos A-b)^2+(c\sin A)^2$$

Expand the square: $a^2=c^2\cos^2A-2bc\cos A+b^2+c^2\sin^2A$.

Group the $c^2\cos^2A$ and $c^2\sin^2A$ terms together and use $\cos^2A+\sin^2A=1$: $$a^2=c^2(\cos^2A+\sin^2A)+b^2-2bc\cos A=b^2+c^2-2bc\cos A.$$

Placing the origin at a different vertex gives the other two versions of the formula by the identical argument. And when $A=90°$, $\cos A=0$, so the formula collapses to $a^2=b^2+c^2$ — the Pythagorean theorem is just the special case of a right angle.

∎

Finding Angles (SSS)

Rearrange to find an angle:

$$\cos A=\dfrac{b^2+c^2-a^2}{2bc}$$

Strategy for SSS: find the largest angle first (opposite the longest side) — this is least likely to cause ambiguity.

Then use Law of Sines (or cosines) for the remaining angles.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — SAS
In triangle: $b=5$, $c=8$, $A=60°$. Find $a$.
$a^2=25+64-80\cos60°=89-40=49\Rightarrow a=7$.
Example 2 — SSS — find an angle
Triangle with $a=8$, $b=11$, $c=6$. Find angle $B$.
$\cos B=\dfrac{64+36-121}{2(8)(6)}=\dfrac{-21}{96}\approx-0.219\Rightarrow B\approx102.6°$.
Example 3 — Navigation
Two ships leave port at the same time. Ship A travels 30 km at $N35°E$; Ship B travels 40 km at $S50°E$. Find their distance apart.
Angle between them $=35°+50°=85°$. $d^2=30^2+40^2-2(30)(40)\cos85°\approx2500-208.7\approx2291$; $d\approx47.9$ km.
Example 4 — Heron's formula and cosines
Find area of triangle with $a=9$, $b=12$, $c=15$.
Check: $9^2+12^2=225=15^2$ — right triangle! Area $=\frac{1}{2}(9)(12)=54$.
Practice Problems
1.
SAS: $a=5$, $c=7$, $B=45°$. Find $b$.
$b^2=25+49-70\cos45°\approx74-49.5=24.5$; $b\approx4.95$.
2.
SSS: $a=3$, $b=4$, $c=5$. Find angle $C$.
$\cos C=(9+16-25)/24=0\Rightarrow C=90°$.
3.
SSS: $a=10$, $b=8$, $c=6$. Find the largest angle.
$\cos A=(64+36-100)/96=0\Rightarrow A=90°$.
4.
SAS: two sides 6 and 8 with included angle $120°$. Find the third side.
$c^2=36+64-96\cos120°=100+48=148$; $c\approx12.17$.
5.
Two hikers start at the same point: one walks 5 km north, the other 7 km at $N40°E$. Find distance between them.
Angle $=40°$; $d^2=25+49-70\cos40°\approx74-53.6=20.4$; $d\approx4.52$ km.
6.
Find all angles of triangle $a=7$, $b=8$, $c=9$.
$\cos A=(64+81-49)/144=96/144=2/3$; $A\approx48.2°$. Then use Law of Sines for $B,C$.
