Polar Coordinates

Pre-Calculus  ·  §8.3  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

Rectangular coordinates $(x,y)$ describe a point by two perpendicular distances. Is there a more natural way to describe a point on a circle — using its distance from the center and the angle it makes?
Polar Coordinates

A point $P$ is described by $(r,\theta)$: $r$ = distance from origin, $\theta$ = angle from positive $x$-axis.

Conversion to rectangular:
$x=r\cos\theta$, $y=r\sin\theta$

Conversion to polar:
$r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$, $\theta=\arctan(y/x)$ (adjust quadrant)

Points have multiple representations: $(r,\theta)=(r,\theta+2\pi)=(-r,\theta+\pi)$.

Plotting in Polar

To plot $(r,\theta)$: rotate the ray $\theta$ from the positive $x$-axis, then go $r$ units out (or back if $r<0$).

Common polar curves:
$r=a$: circle of radius $a$
$\theta=\alpha$: ray at angle $\alpha$
$r=2a\cos\theta$: circle through origin
$r=a(1+\cos\theta)$: cardioid

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Plot polar points
Plot and convert to rectangular: $(3,\pi/3)$.
$x=3\cos(\pi/3)=3/2$; $y=3\sin(\pi/3)=3\sqrt3/2$. Rectangular: $(3/2, 3\sqrt3/2)$.
Example 2 — Rectangular to polar
Convert $(-1,-\sqrt3)$ to polar form.
$r=\sqrt{1+3}=2$; $\theta=\arctan(\sqrt3/1)=\pi/3$; but point in QIII: $\theta=\pi+\pi/3=4\pi/3$. Answer: $(2,4\pi/3)$.
Example 3 — Convert equation
Convert $x^2+y^2=4$ to polar.
$r^2=4\Rightarrow r=2$. (Circle of radius 2.)
Example 4 — Convert polar equation
Convert $r=4\cos\theta$ to rectangular.
$r^2=4r\cos\theta\Rightarrow x^2+y^2=4x\Rightarrow(x-2)^2+y^2=4$. (Circle center $(2,0)$, radius 2.)
Practice Problems
1.
Convert $(4,\pi/4)$ to rectangular.
$(2\sqrt2,2\sqrt2)$.
2.
Convert $(2,0)$ rectangular to polar.
$(2,0)$.
3.
Convert $(-3,0)$ rectangular to polar.
$(3,\pi)$.
4.
Convert $r=5$ to rectangular.
$x^2+y^2=25$.
5.
Convert $x=3$ to polar.
$r\cos\theta=3\Rightarrow r=3/\cos\theta=3\sec\theta$.
6.
Plot $\left(2,\dfrac{5\pi}{6}\right)$ — which quadrant?
$\theta=5\pi/6$ is in QII; $r=2>0$: point in QII.
