Function Combinations and Composition

Pre-Calculus  ·  §1.4  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

If $f(x)$ gives the miles per gallon of a car and $g(x)$ gives the price of gas per gallon, how would you combine $f$ and $g$ to find the cost per mile?
Arithmetic Combinations

Given functions $f$ and $g$:

$(f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)$
$(f-g)(x)=f(x)-g(x)$
$(fg)(x)=f(x)\cdot g(x)$
$(f/g)(x)=f(x)/g(x),\; g(x)\neq 0$

Domain: intersection of domains of $f$ and $g$ (exclude zeros of $g$ for division).

▸ Common Mistake

A combined function inherits the domain restrictions of both originals — you can't read the domain off the simplified formula. If $f(x)=\sqrt x$ and $g(x)=\sqrt x$, then $(f/g)(x)=1$ looks defined everywhere, but its domain is still $x>0$: both pieces need $x\ge0$, and $g\ne0$ rules out $x=0$.

Find the domain from the original $f$ and $g$ (and exclude zeros of $g$ for a quotient) before simplifying.

Composition of Functions

Composition: $(f\circ g)(x)=f(g(x))$ — apply $g$ first, then $f$.

Domain of $f\circ g$: all $x$ in domain of $g$ such that $g(x)$ is in domain of $f$.

Order matters: $f\circ g\neq g\circ f$ in general.

$(f\circ g)(2)=f(g(2))$ — evaluate $g(2)$ first, then plug into $f$.

▸ Common Mistake

$(f\circ g)(x)=f(g(x))$ means do $g$ first, then $f$ — the inside function acts first, even though $f$ is written on the left. Reading left-to-right and computing $g(f(x))$ is the usual slip, and order matters: $f\circ g\ne g\circ f$ in general.

Don't confuse composition with the product either: $(fg)(x)=f(x)\cdot g(x)$, but $f(g(x))$ is not multiplication.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Arithmetic combination
Let $f(x)=x^2+1$ and $g(x)=2x-3$. Find $(f+g)(x)$, $(f-g)(x)$, and $(fg)(x)$.
$(f+g)(x)=x^2+2x-2$; $(f-g)(x)=x^2-2x+4$; $(fg)(x)=(x^2+1)(2x-3)=2x^3-3x^2+2x-3$.
Example 2 — Composition
Let $f(x)=\sqrt{x}$ and $g(x)=x^2+1$. Find $(f\circ g)(x)$ and $(g\circ f)(x)$.
$(f\circ g)(x)=\sqrt{x^2+1}$; $(g\circ f)(x)=(\sqrt{x})^2+1=x+1$ for $x\geq 0$.
Example 3 — Evaluate composed functions
Let $f(x)=2x+1$ and $g(x)=x^2$. Find $(f\circ g)(3)$ and $(g\circ f)(-1)$.
$(f\circ g)(3)=f(9)=19$; $(g\circ f)(-1)=g(-1)=(-1)^2=1$.
Example 4 — Domain of quotient
Let $f(x)=x+4$ and $g(x)=x^2-16$. Find $(f/g)(x)$ and its domain.
$(f/g)(x)=\dfrac{x+4}{(x-4)(x+4)}=\dfrac{1}{x-4}$ for $x\neq\pm4$. Domain: all reals except $\pm4$.
Practice Problems
1.
Let $f(x)=x+2$, $g(x)=x-5$. Find $(f+g)(x)$ and $(fg)(x)$.
$(f+g)(x)=2x-3$; $(fg)(x)=x^2-3x-10$.
2.
Let $f(x)=3x$ and $g(x)=x^2-1$. Find $(f\circ g)(2)$.
$g(2)=3$; $f(3)=9$.
3.
Let $f(x)=x+1$ and $g(x)=x^2$. Find $(g\circ f)(x)$ and simplify.
$(g\circ f)(x)=(x+1)^2=x^2+2x+1$.
4.
Let $f(x)=\sqrt{x-1}$ and $g(x)=x^2+1$. Find the domain of $f\circ g$.
$g(x)=x^2+1\geq 2>1$ for all $x$, so domain of $f\circ g$ is all reals $(-\infty,\infty)$.
5.
Let $f(x)=2x-1$ and $g(x)=\dfrac{x+1}{2}$. Compute $(f\circ g)(x)$.
$(f\circ g)(x)=2\cdot\dfrac{x+1}{2}-1=x$. (These are inverses of each other.)
6.
Find $f$ and $g$ such that $h(x)=(3x+2)^5=(f\circ g)(x)$.
One choice: $g(x)=3x+2$, $f(x)=x^5$.
