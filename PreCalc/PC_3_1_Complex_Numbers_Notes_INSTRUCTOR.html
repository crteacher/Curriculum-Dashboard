Complex Numbers

Pre-Calculus  ·  §3.1  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

The equation $x^2+1=0$ has no real solution — but what if we invent a number $i$ such that $i^2=-1$? What kind of algebra becomes possible?
Complex Number Basics

Imaginary unit: $i=\sqrt{-1}$, so $i^2=-1$.

Standard form: $a+bi$ where $a,b\in\mathbb{R}$.
$a$ = real part; $b$ = imaginary part.

Powers of $i$:
$i^1=i,\; i^2=-1,\; i^3=-i,\; i^4=1$, then repeats.

Conjugate of $a+bi$: $\overline{a+bi}=a-bi$.

▸ Show Proof

Why do the powers of $i$ repeat in a cycle of four? Everything follows from the single rule $i^2=-1$. Build the powers one step at a time, each time multiplying the previous one by $i$:

$$i^1=i,\qquad i^2=-1,\qquad i^3=i^2\cdot i=-i,\qquad i^4=i^2\cdot i^2=(-1)(-1)=1.$$

Now $i^4=1$, and multiplying by $1$ changes nothing — so $i^5=i^4\cdot i=i$ lands right back at the start. From here the list $i,\,-1,\,-i,\,1$ repeats forever.

That is the shortcut for any power: divide the exponent by $4$ and keep only the remainder. For instance $17=4\cdot4+1$ has remainder $1$, so $i^{17}=i$.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

Students simplify $\sqrt{-4}\cdot\sqrt{-9}$ by combining the radicals first: $\sqrt{(-4)(-9)}=\sqrt{36}=6$. That's wrong — the rule $\sqrt{a}\cdot\sqrt{b}=\sqrt{ab}$ only holds when $a$ and $b$ are non-negative. Once you're under a negative radical you're already in imaginary territory, and the combination rule doesn't carry over.

The correct approach converts to $i$ first: $\sqrt{-4}\cdot\sqrt{-9}=(2i)(3i)=6i^2=-6$. The sign flips because $i^2=-1$ — so the true answer is $-6$, not $6$. Whenever both radicands are negative, simplify each $\sqrt{-k}$ as $i\sqrt{k}$ before multiplying.

Arithmetic with Complex Numbers

Add/Subtract: combine real parts, combine imaginary parts.
$(3+2i)+(1-5i)=4-3i$

Multiply: FOIL, replace $i^2=-1$.
$(2+3i)(1-4i)=2-8i+3i-12i^2=14-5i$

Divide: multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator.
$\dfrac{2+i}{3-i}=\dfrac{(2+i)(3+i)}{10}=\dfrac{5+5i}{10}=\dfrac{1}{2}+\dfrac{1}{2}i$

▸ Show Proof

Why does multiplying by the conjugate let you divide complex numbers? The aim is to turn the denominator into a plain real number, and the conjugate is exactly the tool that does it. Multiply any $a+bi$ by its conjugate $a-bi$:

$$(a+bi)(a-bi)=a^2-(bi)^2=a^2-b^2i^2=a^2+b^2.$$

Because $i^2=-1$, the minus sign flips to a plus and the imaginary parts cancel — the result $a^2+b^2$ is a real (and positive) number.

So to compute $\dfrac{z}{a+bi}$, multiply top and bottom by $a-bi$. The denominator becomes the real number $a^2+b^2$, and dividing by a real number is easy: just split into real and imaginary parts.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

To compute $\dfrac{2+i}{3-i}$, students multiply top and bottom by the conjugate of the numerator, $2-i$, instead of the denominator's conjugate, $3+i$. The result still looks like a legitimate complex-number computation, but it leaves an imaginary part sitting in the denominator — exactly the thing conjugate multiplication is supposed to eliminate.

The rule targets whichever expression you're dividing by: multiplying $a+bi$ by its own conjugate $a-bi$ gives the real number $a^2+b^2$, so it's the denominator's conjugate that clears the imaginary part below the fraction bar. Conjugating the numerator instead changes the value of the fraction.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Add and subtract
Compute $(5+7i)-(7-3i)$.
$(5-7)+(7+3)i=-2+10i$.
Example 2 — Multiply
Compute $(5+3i)(2+7i)$.
$10+35i+6i+21i^2=10+41i-21=-11+41i$.
Example 3 — Divide
Compute $\dfrac{2-3i}{4-3i}$.
$\dfrac{(2-3i)(4+3i)}{(4-3i)(4+3i)}=\dfrac{8+6i-12i-9i^2}{25}=\dfrac{17-6i}{25}=\dfrac{17}{25}-\dfrac{6}{25}i$.
Example 4 — Quadratic with complex solutions
Solve $x^2+8=0$.
$x^2=-8\Rightarrow x=\pm\sqrt{-8}=\pm 2i\sqrt{2}$.
Practice Problems
1.
Compute $(3+4i)+(1-2i)$.
$4+2i$.
2.
Compute $(1+i)(1-i)$.
$1-i^2=1+1=2$.
3.
Find $i^{17}$.
$17=4\cdot 4+1$, so $i^{17}=i^1=i$.
4.
Compute $\dfrac{1}{i}$.
$\dfrac{1}{i}\cdot\dfrac{-i}{-i}=\dfrac{-i}{1}=-i$.
5.
Solve $2x^2+18=0$.
$x^2=-9\Rightarrow x=\pm 3i$.
6.
State the conjugate of $4-5i$ and compute their product.
Conjugate $=4+5i$; product $=16+25=41$.
