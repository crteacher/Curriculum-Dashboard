Conic Sections

Pre-Calculus  ·  §8.5  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

Slice a cone at different angles and you get a circle, an ellipse, a parabola, or a hyperbola. What equation unifies all four shapes?
Parabolas and Ellipses

Parabola (vertex at origin):
$x^2=4py$ (opens up/down, focus at $(0,p)$)
$y^2=4px$ (opens left/right, focus at $(p,0)$)

Ellipse (center at origin):
$\dfrac{x^2}{a^2}+\dfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$; $a>b>0$
Foci at $(\pm c,0)$ where $c^2=a^2-b^2$

Major axis along $x$ if $a>b$; along $y$ if $b>a$.

▸ Show Proof

An ellipse is defined as the set of points where the sum of the distances to two fixed foci is constant, say $2a$. Put the foci at $(\pm c,0)$ and let $(x,y)$ be any point on the ellipse. The definition says $$\sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2}+\sqrt{(x+c)^2+y^2}=2a.$$

Move one radical to the other side and square both sides. After the $x^2,y^2,c^2$ terms cancel, this simplifies to $a\sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2}=a^2-cx$ (a standard trick — squaring once clears one radical).

Square again to clear the last radical, and collect like terms. Everything reduces to $$(a^2-c^2)x^2+a^2y^2=a^2(a^2-c^2).$$

Since $a>c$ for an ellipse, $a^2-c^2$ is positive, so define $b^2=a^2-c^2$. Dividing both sides by $a^2b^2$ gives exactly $$\dfrac{x^2}{a^2}+\dfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1,$$ and the foci relation $c^2=a^2-b^2$ falls right out of how we defined $b^2$.

The parabola formula comes from a similar distance-based definition — every point is equidistant from a focus and a line (the directrix) — and the same style of algebra (set the two distances equal, square, simplify) produces $x^2=4py$.

∎

Hyperbolas

Hyperbola (center at origin):
$\dfrac{x^2}{a^2}-\dfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1$ — opens left/right
$\dfrac{y^2}{a^2}-\dfrac{x^2}{b^2}=1$ — opens up/down

Foci: $c^2=a^2+b^2$
Asymptotes: $y=\pm\dfrac{b}{a}x$ (horizontal opening)

General form: $Ax^2+Cy^2+Dx+Ey+F=0$. Complete the square to identify the conic.

▸ Show Proof

A hyperbola uses the same setup as the ellipse, but with a difference of distances instead of a sum: $$\left|\sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2}-\sqrt{(x+c)^2+y^2}\right|=2a.$$

Running through the identical squaring-and-simplifying steps used for the ellipse leads to $(c^2-a^2)x^2-a^2y^2=a^2(c^2-a^2)$ — the same equation as before, but with the sign on the $c^2-a^2$ term flipped because we squared a difference instead of a sum.

This time $c>a$ (the foci are farther out than the vertices), so $c^2-a^2$ is positive. Define $b^2=c^2-a^2$ — note this is the opposite sign convention from the ellipse — and dividing through gives $$\dfrac{x^2}{a^2}-\dfrac{y^2}{b^2}=1,\qquad c^2=a^2+b^2.$$

For the asymptotes, solve the equation for $y$: $y^2=b^2\left(\dfrac{x^2}{a^2}-1\right)$. As $x$ gets very large, the $-1$ becomes negligible compared to $x^2/a^2$, so $y^2\approx b^2x^2/a^2$, giving $y\approx\pm\dfrac{b}{a}x$ — the two lines the hyperbola hugs but never touches.

∎

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Identify and graph ellipse
Graph $\dfrac{x^2}{25}+\dfrac{y^2}{9}=1$. Find foci.
$a=5$, $b=3$; $c=\sqrt{25-9}=4$. Foci $=(\pm4,0)$. Vertices: $(\pm5,0)$, $(0,\pm3)$.
Example 2 — Find parabola equation
A parabola has vertex at origin, opens upward, and passes through $(2,1)$.
$x^2=4py$: $4=4p\cdot1\Rightarrow p=1$. Equation: $x^2=4y$.
Example 3 — Hyperbola
Find foci and asymptotes of $\dfrac{x^2}{16}-\dfrac{y^2}{9}=1$.
$a=4$, $b=3$; $c=5$. Foci $(\pm5,0)$. Asymptotes $y=\pm\dfrac{3}{4}x$.
Example 4 — Complete the square
Identify $4x^2+9y^2-16x+36y+16=0$.
$4(x-2)^2+9(y+2)^2=36\Rightarrow\dfrac{(x-2)^2}{9}+\dfrac{(y+2)^2}{4}=1$. Ellipse centered at $(2,-2)$.

$y^2=12x$.
4.

$y=\pm\dfrac{2}{3}x$.
5.
Identify $x^2+y^2=16$.
Circle, radius 4.
6.
Complete the square: $x^2-6x+y^2+4y=3$.
$(x-3)^2+(y+2)^2=16$ — circle center $(3,-2)$, radius 4.
