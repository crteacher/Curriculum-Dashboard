Quadratic Functions — Completing the Square

Pre-Calculus  ·  §3.2 Pt 2  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

The standard form $ax^2+bx+c$ hides the vertex. Is there an algebraic procedure to rewrite any quadratic in vertex form without graphing?
Completing the Square

Key identity: $x^2+bx=\left(x+\dfrac{b}{2}\right)^2-\left(\dfrac{b}{2}\right)^2$

Steps to vertex form:

  1. Factor $a$ from the $x$-terms.
  2. Add and subtract $\left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2$ inside.
  3. Write as $a(x-h)^2+k$.
▸ Show Proof

The key idea is to recognize a perfect-square pattern. Recall that squaring a binomial gives $\left(x+\tfrac{b}{2}\right)^2 = x^2 + 2\cdot x\cdot\tfrac{b}{2} + \left(\tfrac{b}{2}\right)^2$.

The middle term simplifies, since $2\cdot\tfrac{b}{2}=b$:

$$\left(x+\tfrac{b}{2}\right)^2 = x^2 + bx + \left(\tfrac{b}{2}\right)^2.$$

So a perfect square is exactly $x^2+bx$ plus the extra constant $\left(\tfrac{b}{2}\right)^2$. To recover $x^2+bx$ on its own, just subtract that extra constant back off:

$$x^2+bx = \left(x+\tfrac{b}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\tfrac{b}{2}\right)^2.$$

That is the identity — taking half of the $x$-coefficient, squaring it, then adding and removing it is what lets you fold $x^2+bx$ into a single squared term.

∎

Quadratic Formula and Discriminant

For $ax^2+bx+c=0$:

$$x=\dfrac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$$

Discriminant $D=b^2-4ac$:
$D>0$: two real zeros
$D=0$: one real zero (double root)
$D<0$: two complex conjugate zeros

▸ Show Proof

Here's where the quadratic formula comes from: just complete the square on the general equation. Start with $ax^2+bx+c=0$ and divide through by $a$ (allowed since $a\neq 0$):

$$x^2 + \tfrac{b}{a}x + \tfrac{c}{a} = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x^2 + \tfrac{b}{a}x = -\tfrac{c}{a}.$$

Now complete the square on the left: take half of $\tfrac{b}{a}$, square it to get $\left(\tfrac{b}{2a}\right)^2$, and add it to both sides:

$$\left(x+\tfrac{b}{2a}\right)^2 = \tfrac{b^2}{4a^2} - \tfrac{c}{a} = \frac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}.$$

Take the square root of both sides (the $\pm$ appears because both a positive and negative root work):

$$x+\tfrac{b}{2a} = \pm\frac{\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}.$$

Finally subtract $\tfrac{b}{2a}$ and combine over the common denominator $2a$:

$$x = \frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}.$$

Notice the quantity under the root is exactly the discriminant $b^2-4ac$ — that is why its sign decides whether the roots are real or complex.

∎

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Complete the square
Write $f(x)=x^2-6x+2$ in vertex form.
$x^2-6x+9-7=(x-3)^2-7$. Vertex $(3,-7)$.
Example 2 — Leading coefficient
Write $f(x)=2x^2+12x+5$ in vertex form.
$2(x^2+6x)+5=2(x^2+6x+9-9)+5=2(x+3)^2-18+5=2(x+3)^2-13$. Vertex $(-3,-13)$.
Example 3 — Quadratic formula
Solve $3x^2-5x+1=0$.
$x=\dfrac{5\pm\sqrt{25-12}}{6}=\dfrac{5\pm\sqrt{13}}{6}$.
Example 4 — Applied maximum
A ball's height is $h(t)=-16t^2+64t+5$. Find the maximum height and when it occurs.
Complete square or use $t=-\tfrac{b}{2a}=2$ s. $h(2)=-64+128+5=69$ ft.
Practice Problems
1.
Write $x^2+8x+3$ in vertex form.
$(x+4)^2-13$.
2.
Use the quadratic formula to solve $x^2+4x+5=0$.
$D=16-20=-4<0$; $x=\dfrac{-4\pm 2i}{2}=-2\pm i$.
3.
How many real solutions does $2x^2-4x+2=0$ have?
$D=16-16=0$; one real solution (double root).
4.
Solve $x^2-5x+6=0$ by factoring.
$(x-2)(x-3)=0\Rightarrow x=2,3$.
5.
Find the vertex of $f(x)=x^2+4x-1$ by completing the square.
$(x+2)^2-5$; vertex $(-2,-5)$.
6.
A quadratic has roots $x=3\pm 2i$. State the discriminant.
$D<0$ (complex roots come from $D=-16$).
