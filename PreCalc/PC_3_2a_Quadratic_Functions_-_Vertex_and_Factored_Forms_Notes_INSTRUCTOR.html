Quadratic Functions — Vertex & Factored Forms

Pre-Calculus  ·  §3.2 Pt 1  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

A ball is thrown upward — its height is quadratic in time. How can you quickly read the maximum height and when it hits the ground from the equation?
Standard and Vertex Forms

Standard form: $f(x)=ax^2+bx+c$, $a\neq 0$.

Vertex form: $f(x)=a(x-h)^2+k$
Vertex at $(h,k)$; opens up if $a>0$, down if $a<0$.

Axis of symmetry: $x=h$.

Vertex form makes the maximum/minimum immediately visible: $k$ is the max (if $a<0$) or min (if $a>0$).

▸ Show Proof

Why is the vertex of $f(x)=a(x-h)^2+k$ exactly the point $(h,k)$? Look at the squared term $(x-h)^2$. A square is never negative, and it equals $0$ only when $x=h$.

Take $a>0$. Then $a(x-h)^2\ge 0$ for every $x$, so

$$f(x)=a(x-h)^2+k\ge k,$$

with equality precisely at $x=h$. The output never dips below $k$, so $k$ is the minimum and it occurs at $x=h$ — the vertex is $(h,k)$.

If $a<0$ the reasoning flips: $a(x-h)^2\le 0$, so $f(x)\le k$ and $k$ becomes the maximum. Either way the turning point sits at $(h,k)$, with the curve mirrored across the line $x=h$.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

Given $f(x)=2(x-3)^2-8$, students read the vertex straight off the sign in front of the number and say $(-3,-8)$ instead of $(3,-8)$. The minus sign inside the parentheses gets carried into the vertex instead of canceled out of it.

The form is $a(x-h)^2+k$, so the vertex is at $x=h$ — the value that makes $(x-h)$ equal zero. If the expression is $(x-3)^2$, that means $h=3$ (since $x-3=0$ at $x=3$), not $h=-3$. Only when the form already shows a plus sign, like $(x+3)^2=(x-(-3))^2$, does the vertex's $x$-coordinate become negative.

Factored Form and Zeros

Factored form: $f(x)=a(x-r_1)(x-r_2)$
Zeros (x-intercepts) at $x=r_1$ and $x=r_2$.

Vertex x-coordinate = midpoint of the zeros:
$h=\dfrac{r_1+r_2}{2}$

y-intercept: set $x=0$: $f(0)=ar_1 r_2$.

▸ Show Proof

Why does the vertex sit exactly halfway between the two zeros? A parabola is symmetric about its vertical axis — the left half mirrors the right half. The two $x$-intercepts $r_1$ and $r_2$ are points on the curve at the same height ($y=0$), so by that mirror symmetry they must be the same distance from the axis, one on each side.

The value the same distance from both $r_1$ and $r_2$ is simply their average:

$$h=\frac{r_1+r_2}{2}.$$

The algebra agrees: $f(x)=a(x-r_1)(x-r_2)$ is unchanged if you swap $r_1$ and $r_2$, so its graph is symmetric about the midpoint of the roots. That midpoint is the axis of symmetry $x=h$, and the vertex lives on it.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

For $f(x)=a(x-r_1)(x-r_2)$, students read the zeros off with the wrong sign — given $f(x)=-(x+1)(x-5)$, they say the zeros are $x=1$ and $x=-5$ instead of $x=-1$ and $x=5$. The sign written next to the number gets used directly, instead of the sign that actually makes each factor zero.

Each zero is the value of $x$ that makes its own factor vanish: $(x+1)=0$ requires $x=-1$, not $x=1$. Read $(x+1)$ as $(x-(-1))$ to match the $(x-r)$ template — the root is always the opposite of the constant sitting inside the parentheses.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Graph from vertex form
Sketch $f(x)=2(x-3)^2-8$. State vertex, axis, direction.
Vertex $(3,-8)$, axis $x=3$, opens upward. $y$-int: $f(0)=18-8=10$.
Example 2 — Write in vertex form
Write $f(x)=x^2-6x+5$ in vertex form.
$x^2-6x+9-4=(x-3)^2-4$. Vertex $(3,-4)$.
Example 3 — Graph from factored form
Sketch $f(x)=-(x+1)(x-5)$. Find vertex, zeros, $y$-int.
Zeros $x=-1,5$; vertex $x=2$, $f(2)=-(3)(-3)=9$, vertex $(2,9)$; $y$-int $f(0)=5$.
Example 4 — Write factored form
Write a quadratic with $a=2$, zeros at $x=1$ and $x=-3$.
$f(x)=2(x-1)(x+3)=2x^2+4x-6$.
Practice Problems
1.
Find the vertex of $f(x)=(x-4)^2+1$.
$(4,1)$.
2.
Write $f(x)=x^2-4x+3$ in factored form.
$f(x)=(x-1)(x-3)$; zeros at $x=1,3$.
3.
Find zeros of $f(x)=3(x+2)(x-4)$.
$x=-2$ and $x=4$.
4.
State the vertex of $f(x)=-2(x+1)^2+5$. Does it open up or down?
Vertex $(-1,5)$, opens down.
5.
A quadratic has vertex $(2,8)$ and opens downward. Write it in vertex form (use $a=-1$).
$f(x)=-(x-2)^2+8$.
6.
Find the $y$-intercept of $f(x)=3(x-2)(x+1)$.
$f(0)=3(-2)(1)=-6$.
