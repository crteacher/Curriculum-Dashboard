Unit Circle: Sine and Cosine

Pre-Calculus  ·  §5.2  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

A point moves around a circle of radius 1. As the angle increases, what do the $x$- and $y$-coordinates of the point do? Can you use them to define sine and cosine?
Sine and Cosine on the Unit Circle

Unit circle: $x^2+y^2=1$, centered at origin.

For angle $\theta$ (measured from positive $x$-axis): the terminal point is $(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$.

Key values:

$\theta$$\cos\theta$$\sin\theta$
$0$$1$$0$
$\pi/6$$\sqrt{3}/2$$1/2$
$\pi/4$$\sqrt{2}/2$$\sqrt{2}/2$
$\pi/3$$1/2$$\sqrt{3}/2$
$\pi/2$$0$$1$
Properties of Sine and Cosine

Pythagorean identity: $\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta=1$

Signs by quadrant (ASTC):
QI: All positive; QII: Sin+; QIII: Tan+; QIV: Cos+.

Periodicity: $\sin(\theta+2\pi)=\sin\theta$; $\cos(\theta+2\pi)=\cos\theta$.
Period $= 2\pi$.

Even/Odd: $\cos(-\theta)=\cos\theta$ (even); $\sin(-\theta)=-\sin\theta$ (odd).

▸ Show Proof

Why these properties hold. By definition, the point on the unit circle at angle $\theta$ is $(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)$. Every point $(x,y)$ on the unit circle sits exactly distance $1$ from the origin, so it satisfies $x^2+y^2=1$.

Substituting $x=\cos\theta$ and $y=\sin\theta$ gives the Pythagorean identity directly:

$$\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta=1.$$

Even/odd: the angle $-\theta$ is the reflection of $\theta$ across the $x$-axis. Reflecting keeps the $x$-coordinate the same but flips the sign of the $y$-coordinate, so $\cos(-\theta)=\cos\theta$ and $\sin(-\theta)=-\sin\theta$.

Periodicity: adding $2\pi$ to $\theta$ is one full trip around the circle, landing on the exact same point. Same point means same coordinates, so $\cos(\theta+2\pi)=\cos\theta$ and $\sin(\theta+2\pi)=\sin\theta$.

∎

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Evaluate on unit circle
Find $\sin\dfrac{5\pi}{6}$ and $\cos\dfrac{5\pi}{6}$.
$\dfrac{5\pi}{6}$ is in QII; reference angle $\dfrac{\pi}{6}$. $\sin=\dfrac{1}{2}$; $\cos=-\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}$.
Example 2 — Use Pythagorean identity
If $\sin\theta=\dfrac{3}{5}$ and $\theta$ is in QII, find $\cos\theta$.
$\cos^2\theta=1-\dfrac{9}{25}=\dfrac{16}{25}$. QII: $\cos\theta=-\dfrac{4}{5}$.
Example 3 — Evaluate using symmetry
Find $\cos\left(-\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)$ and $\sin\left(-\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)$.
$\cos$ is even: $\cos\dfrac{\pi}{3}=\dfrac{1}{2}$. $\sin$ is odd: $-\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}$.
Example 4 — Signs in a quadrant
In which quadrant is $\theta$ if $\sin\theta<0$ and $\cos\theta>0$?
QIV ($x>0$, $y<0$).
Practice Problems
1.
Find $\sin\dfrac{\pi}{4}$ and $\cos\dfrac{3\pi}{4}$.
$\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ and $-\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$.
2.
Evaluate $\cos\pi$ and $\sin\dfrac{3\pi}{2}$.
$-1$ and $-1$.
3.
If $\cos\theta=\dfrac{5}{13}$ and $\theta$ in QIV, find $\sin\theta$.
$-\dfrac{12}{13}$.
4.
Find $\sin\dfrac{7\pi}{6}$.
Reference angle $\dfrac{\pi}{6}$ in QIII: $\sin=-\dfrac{1}{2}$.
5.
Verify: $\sin^2\dfrac{\pi}{3}+\cos^2\dfrac{\pi}{3}=1$.
$\dfrac{3}{4}+\dfrac{1}{4}=1$ ✓.
6.
In which quadrants is $\cos\theta<0$?
Quadrants II and III.
