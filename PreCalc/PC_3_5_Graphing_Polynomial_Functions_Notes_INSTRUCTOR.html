Graphing Polynomial Functions

Pre-Calculus  ·  §3.5  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

Given $f(x)=(x+2)^2(x-3)$, can you sketch its graph without a calculator? What features do you need to find first?
Graphing Strategy

Steps to graph a polynomial:

  1. Find zeros and their multiplicities.
  2. Find the $y$-intercept ($x=0$).
  3. Determine end behavior.
  4. Sketch: cross at odd-mult zeros, bounce at even-mult zeros.
  5. Check sign between zeros.
Increasing, Decreasing, Local Extrema

At local maxima/minima the graph changes direction.

A degree-$n$ polynomial has at most $n-1$ turning points.

Sign chart: pick test values between zeros; the sign of $f$ at each test point tells you if the graph is above or below the $x$-axis on that interval.

▸ Geometric Argument

Why can a degree-$n$ polynomial have at most $n-1$ turning points? A full calculus proof uses derivatives, but here is the geometric idea using only a fact you already know: the equation $f(x)=c$ has at most $n$ solutions, because it is a degree-$n$ polynomial equation.

A turning point is a spot where the graph switches between rising and falling. So if a graph has $k$ turning points, it is made of $k+1$ alternating "arcs" — up, down, up, down, and so on.

Now slide a horizontal line $y=c$ to a height that cuts through every one of those arcs. Each arc, being either entirely rising or entirely falling across its stretch, can be crossed once, so the line meets the graph in $k+1$ separate points:

$$\underbrace{k+1}_{\text{intersections}} \;\le\; \underbrace{n}_{\text{max solutions of } f(x)=c}.$$

Rearranging $k+1\le n$ gives $k\le n-1$. So no matter how the curve wiggles, it cannot turn more than $n-1$ times.

∎

Combine all features (zeros, $y$-int, end behavior, sign chart) to get a complete rough sketch.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Full graph sketch
Sketch $f(x)=(2x+1)(x-3)^2$. Label zeros, $y$-int, end behavior.
Zeros: $x=-\tfrac{1}{2}$ (mult 1, crosses), $x=3$ (mult 2, bounces). $y$-int: $f(0)=(1)(9)=9$. End: $a_n=2>0$, degree 3 → $\downarrow\uparrow$.
Example 2 — Sketch with bounce
Sketch $g(x)=-2(x+2)(x-2)^2$.
Zeros: $x=-2$ (crosses), $x=2$ (bounces). $y$-int: $g(0)=-2(2)(-2)^2=-2(2)(4)=-16$. End: $a_n=-2<0$, degree 3 → $\uparrow\downarrow$.
Example 3 — Find the polynomial from a graph
A graph crosses $x=-1$ (once) and bounces off $x=2$ (once), with $a_n>0$, degree 3. Write a possible equation.
$f(x)=a(x+1)(x-2)^2$, $a>0$. Use a $y$-intercept to find $a$ if given.
Example 4 — Reading increasing/decreasing
For $f(x)=x^4+2x^3+x^2$, factor and identify all zeros and multiplicities.
$f(x)=x^2(x+1)^2$. Zeros $x=0$ (mult 2) and $x=-1$ (mult 2) — both bounce.
Practice Problems
1.
Find zeros and multiplicities: $f(x)=(x-1)^3(x+2)$.
$x=1$ (mult 3, crosses), $x=-2$ (mult 1, crosses).
2.
Find the $y$-intercept of $f(x)=(x+1)^2(x-2)$.
$f(0)=(1)(-2)=-2$.
3.
Describe end behavior of $f(x)=-x^4+3x^2$.
$a_n=-1<0$, even degree: both ends go to $-\infty$.
4.
Sketch $f(x)=x(x-2)(x+1)$. Identify crossing points.
Zeros at $x=-1, 0, 2$, all odd mult — all crossings.
5.
How many local extrema can $f(x)=(x^2-1)^2$ have?
Degree 4: at most 3 turning points. (It has 3: a local max and two local min.)
6.
Write a degree-4 poly with zeros $0$ (mult 2) and $3$ (mult 2).
$f(x)=x^2(x-3)^2$.
