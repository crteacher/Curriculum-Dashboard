Inverse Functions

Pre-Calculus  ·  §1.7  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

If a function encodes a message, what kind of function lets you decode it? What property must it have to guarantee you can always recover the original input?
One-to-One and Inverse Functions

$f^{-1}$ (read "$f$ inverse") reverses $f$:

$f^{-1}(f(x))=x$ for all $x$ in domain of $f$
$f(f^{-1}(y))=y$ for all $y$ in range of $f$

Key fact: $f$ has an inverse if and only if $f$ is one-to-one (passes HLT).

Domain of $f^{-1}$ = Range of $f$
Range of $f^{-1}$ = Domain of $f$

▸ Common Mistake

The $-1$ in $f^{-1}$ is not an exponent: $f^{-1}(x)\neq\dfrac{1}{f(x)}$. For $f(x)=2x+3$, the inverse is $f^{-1}(x)=\dfrac{x-3}{2}$, while the reciprocal $\dfrac{1}{f(x)}=\dfrac{1}{2x+3}$ is a completely different function.

The superscript here means "the function that reverses $f$," reusing reciprocal notation for an unrelated idea. If you want $\dfrac{1}{f(x)}$, write $[f(x)]^{-1}$ — never $f^{-1}(x)$.

Finding and Graphing Inverses

Steps to find $f^{-1}$:

  1. Write $y=f(x)$.
  2. Swap $x$ and $y$.
  3. Solve for $y$.
  4. Let $y=f^{-1}(x)$.

Graph: the graph of $f^{-1}$ is the reflection of the graph of $f$ over the line $y=x$.

▸ Show Proof

Why is the graph of $f^{-1}$ exactly the mirror image of $f$ across the line $y=x$? It comes down to what the inverse does to coordinates.

A point $(a,b)$ lies on the graph of $f$ exactly when $b=f(a)$. Apply $f^{-1}$ to both sides: $f^{-1}(b)=a$, which says the point $(b,a)$ lies on the graph of $f^{-1}$. So the two graphs carry the same information with the coordinates swapped:

$$(a,b)\ \text{on}\ f\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad (b,a)\ \text{on}\ f^{-1}.$$

And swapping a point's coordinates, $(a,b)\mapsto(b,a)$, is reflection across the line $y=x$: that line is exactly the set of points left unchanged by the swap, and it sits halfway between any pair $(a,b)$ and $(b,a)$. Reflecting every point of $f$'s graph therefore lands precisely on $f^{-1}$'s graph.

This is also why the "swap $x$ and $y$, then solve" recipe works — swapping the variables is the algebraic version of reflecting across $y=x$.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Verify an inverse
Verify that $f(x)=2x+3$ and $g(x)=\dfrac{x-3}{2}$ are inverses.
$f(g(x))=2\cdot\dfrac{x-3}{2}+3=x$ ✓; $g(f(x))=\dfrac{(2x+3)-3}{2}=x$ ✓.
Example 2 — Find the inverse
Find $f^{-1}(x)$ for $f(x)=\dfrac{3x-1}{x+2}$.
Swap: $x=\dfrac{3y-1}{y+2}$. Solve: $x(y+2)=3y-1\Rightarrow xy+2x=3y-1\Rightarrow y(x-3)=-2x-1\Rightarrow y=\dfrac{-2x-1}{x-3}=\dfrac{2x+1}{3-x}$.
Example 3 — Restricted domain
Find $f^{-1}(x)$ for $f(x)=x^2$, $x\geq 0$.
Swap: $x=y^2$, $y\geq 0$. Solve: $y=\sqrt{x}$. So $f^{-1}(x)=\sqrt{x}$, domain $[0,\infty)$.
Example 4 — Graph interpretation
The point $(3,7)$ is on the graph of $f$. What point is on the graph of $f^{-1}$?
$(7,3)$ — the coordinates swap.
Practice Problems
1.
Find $f^{-1}(x)$ for $f(x)=4x-8$.
$y=4x-8\Rightarrow x=4y-8\Rightarrow y=\dfrac{x+8}{4}$.
2.
Verify: $f(x)=x^3$ and $g(x)=x^{1/3}$ are inverses.
$f(g(x))=(x^{1/3})^3=x$ ✓; $g(f(x))=(x^3)^{1/3}=x$ ✓.
3.
Is $f(x)=x^2-1$ (domain: all reals) one-to-one? Does it have an inverse?
No — it fails HLT. No inverse without restricting the domain.
4.
Find $g^{-1}(x)$ for $g(x)=\sqrt{x-3}$.
Swap: $x=\sqrt{y-3}\Rightarrow x^2=y-3\Rightarrow y=x^2+3$, for $x\geq 0$.
5.
The graph of $f$ passes through $(0,2),(1,4),(2,6)$. List three points on $f^{-1}$.
$(2,0),(4,1),(6,2)$.
6.
Find $f^{-1}(5)$ if $f(x)=2x+1$.
$f(2)=5$, so $f^{-1}(5)=2$.
