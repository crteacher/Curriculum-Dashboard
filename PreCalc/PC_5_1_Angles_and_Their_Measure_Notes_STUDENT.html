Angles and Their Measure

Pre-Calculus  ·  §5.1  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

A clock's minute hand sweeps a full circle in 60 minutes. If you measure the angle in "radians" instead of degrees, what does one full rotation equal?
Degrees and Radians

Degree: one full rotation = $360°$.

Radian: angle subtended by arc length equal to the radius.
One full rotation $= 2\pi$ rad.

Conversion:
Degrees $\to$ radians: multiply by $\dfrac{\pi}{180}$
Radians $\to$ degrees: multiply by $\dfrac{180}{\pi}$

$180°=\pi\text{ rad}$; $90°=\dfrac{\pi}{2}$; $60°=\dfrac{\pi}{3}$; $45°=\dfrac{\pi}{4}$; $30°=\dfrac{\pi}{6}$.

▸ Show Proof

Where do the numbers $\dfrac{\pi}{180}$ and $\dfrac{180}{\pi}$ actually come from? They follow from a single fact connecting the two ways of measuring a full circle.

A full rotation is $360°$. Going around the whole circle, the arc you trace is the entire circumference, $2\pi r$. Since a radian is the angle whose arc equals one radius $r$, a full circle holds $\dfrac{2\pi r}{r} = 2\pi$ radians. So the same turn is both $360°$ and $2\pi$ rad:

$$360° = 2\pi\text{ rad} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad 180° = \pi\text{ rad}.$$

Now just solve for one unit. Divide $180° = \pi$ rad by $180$: $\;1° = \dfrac{\pi}{180}\text{ rad}$ — so turning degrees into radians means multiplying by $\dfrac{\pi}{180}$.

Divide the same equation by $\pi$ instead: $\;1\text{ rad} = \dfrac{180}{\pi}{}°$ — so radians become degrees by multiplying by $\dfrac{180}{\pi}$.

∎

Arc Length, Coterminal Angles

Arc length: $s=r\theta$ ($\theta$ in radians).

Angular speed: $\omega=\dfrac{\theta}{t}$; Linear speed: $v=r\omega$.

Coterminal angles: same initial and terminal sides. Add or subtract $360°$ (or $2\pi$ rad) to find them.

E.g. $410°$ and $50°$ are coterminal; $-\dfrac{\pi}{4}$ and $\dfrac{7\pi}{4}$ are coterminal.

▸ Show Proof

The formula $s=r\theta$ comes straight from what a radian means. By definition, an angle of $1$ radian cuts off an arc exactly as long as the radius, $r$.

Arc length grows in direct proportion to the angle — double the angle, double the arc. So an angle of $\theta$ radians cuts off an arc $\theta$ times as long as the one-radian arc:

$$s = r\theta.$$

Quick check: a full turn is $\theta = 2\pi$, which gives $s = 2\pi r$ — the whole circumference, exactly as it should be.

Linear speed follows by dividing distance by time. If a point on the rim sweeps at angular speed $\omega = \dfrac{\theta}{t}$, then in time $t$ it travels the arc $s = r\theta$, so its speed along the circle is

$$v = \frac{s}{t} = \frac{r\theta}{t} = r\,\frac{\theta}{t} = r\omega.$$

∎

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Convert degrees to radians
Convert $150°$, $270°$, and $-45°$ to radians.
$\dfrac{5\pi}{6}$; $\dfrac{3\pi}{2}$; $-\dfrac{\pi}{4}$.
Example 2 — Convert radians to degrees
Convert $\dfrac{5\pi}{3}$, $\dfrac{7\pi}{4}$, and $3$ rad to degrees.
$300°$; $315°$; $\dfrac{540}{\pi}\approx171.9°$.
Example 3 — Arc length
A circle has radius 6 cm. Find the arc length subtended by an angle of $\dfrac{2\pi}{3}$ rad.
$s=6\cdot\dfrac{2\pi}{3}=4\pi\approx12.6$ cm.
Example 4 — Coterminal angles
Find two angles coterminal with $\dfrac{5\pi}{6}$.
$\dfrac{5\pi}{6}+2\pi=\dfrac{17\pi}{6}$ and $\dfrac{5\pi}{6}-2\pi=-\dfrac{7\pi}{6}$.
