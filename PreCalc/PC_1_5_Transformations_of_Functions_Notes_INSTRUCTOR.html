Transformations of Functions

Pre-Calculus  ·  §1.5  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

If you know the graph of $y=x^2$, how could you get the graph of $y=(x-3)^2+1$ without making a new table of values?
Shifts and Reflections

Starting from $y=f(x)$:

  • Vertical shift up $k$: $y=f(x)+k$
  • Vertical shift down $k$: $y=f(x)-k$
  • Horizontal shift right $h$: $y=f(x-h)$
  • Horizontal shift left $h$: $y=f(x+h)$
  • Reflect over $x$-axis: $y=-f(x)$
  • Reflect over $y$-axis: $y=f(-x)$

Inside the function $\leftrightarrow$ horizontal (and opposite). Outside $\leftrightarrow$ vertical.

▸ Show Proof

The vertical rules are intuitive — adding $k$ after the function lifts every output by $k$. The tricky ones are the horizontal shifts, which feel backwards: why does $y=f(x-h)$ move the graph to the right when we subtracted?

Here's the key idea. A point sits on the new graph at input $x$ with height $f(x-h)$. To land the same height the original graph had at some input $u$ — namely $f(u)$ — we need

$$x-h=u\quad\Longrightarrow\quad x=u+h.$$

So whatever feature lived at $x=u$ on the old graph now appears at $x=u+h$ — shifted $h$ units to the right. Subtracting inside the function pushes the graph the opposite way it "reads," because the input now has to be $h$ larger to reach the same stage.

The same swap explains $y=f(-x)$: the height at $x$ equals the old height at $-x$, so left and right inputs trade places — a reflection across the $y$-axis. Anything done inside $f$ acts on the input, so it changes $x$ and runs counter to intuition; anything done outside acts on the output and behaves normally.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

Keep the two reflections straight: $y=-f(x)$ flips over the $x$-axis (the output is negated — vertical), while $y=f(-x)$ flips over the $y$-axis (the input is negated — horizontal). Students swap them and reflect the wrong way.

Rule of thumb: a negative outside $f$ acts on outputs (vertical flip); a negative inside acts on inputs (horizontal flip).

Stretches and Combined Transformations

For $y=af(b(x-h))+k$:

  • $|a|>1$: vertical stretch; $0<|a|<1$: vertical shrink; $a<0$: flip over $x$-axis
  • $|b|>1$: horizontal shrink; $0<|b|<1$: horizontal stretch; $b<0$: flip over $y$-axis
  • $h$: horizontal shift right; $k$: vertical shift up

Order for graphing: horizontal shift → stretch/shrink/flip → vertical shift.

▸ Common Mistake

Horizontal changes run backwards and must be factored before you read them. $y=f(x-3)$ shifts right 3 (not left), and with a coefficient you must factor $b$ out first: $f(2x-6)=f\big(2(x-3)\big)$ shifts right $3$, not $6$.

Same reversal for scaling: $|b|>1$ shrinks horizontally rather than stretching. Anything inside $f$ acts on the input and behaves opposite to its sign.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Identify transformations
Describe the transformations that produce $y=-(x+2)^2+3$ from $y=x^2$.
Left 2, reflect over $x$-axis, up 3.
Example 2 — Apply transformations
Graph $y=\sqrt{x-1}+2$ starting from $y=\sqrt{x}$.
Shift right 1 unit, then up 2 units.
Example 3 — Vertical stretch + shift
Describe how $y=3|x|-1$ relates to $y=|x|$.
Vertical stretch by factor 3, then shift down 1.
Example 4 — Write transformed equation
The graph of $y=x^3$ is shifted left 4, reflected over the $x$-axis, and shifted up 2. Write the equation.
$y=-(x+4)^3+2$.
Practice Problems
1.
Describe the transformation from $y=x^2$ to $y=(x-5)^2$.
Horizontal shift right 5.
2.
Graph $y=|x+3|-1$ from $y=|x|$.
Shift left 3, then down 1.
3.
Write the equation of $y=\sqrt{x}$ shifted right 2 and up 4.
$y=\sqrt{x-2}+4$.
4.
How does $y=-2x^2$ relate to $y=x^2$?
Vertical stretch by 2, then reflect over $x$-axis.
5.
Describe $y=f(x+1)-3$ relative to $y=f(x)$.
Shift left 1, shift down 3.
6.
What transformation produces $y=f(-x)$ from $y=f(x)$?
Reflection over the $y$-axis.
