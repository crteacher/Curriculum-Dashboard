Lines

Pre-Calculus  ·  §2.1  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

A car's value drops at a constant rate of $1{,}750 per year. If it is worth $22{,}000 today, how would you model its value over time — and what does "constant rate" tell you about the shape of the graph?
Slope and Point-Slope Form

Slope of the line through $(x_1,y_1)$ and $(x_2,y_2)$:

$$m=\dfrac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}$$

$m>0$: rising; $m<0$: falling; $m=0$: horizontal; undefined: vertical.

Point-Slope Form: $y-y_1=m(x-x_1)$

▸ Show Proof

First, why does a line have just one slope? Pick any two points on it and draw the little right triangle whose legs are the horizontal "run" and vertical "rise." Because a straight line keeps the same direction everywhere, any second triangle you draw on it has the same angles, so the two triangles are similar. Similar triangles have proportional sides, so $\dfrac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}}$ comes out the same no matter which two points you pick. That single ratio is the slope $m$.

Now use that fact. Let $(x_1,y_1)$ be a known point on the line and let $(x,y)$ be any other point on it. The slope between them must equal $m$:

$$\frac{y-y_1}{x-x_1}=m.$$

Multiply both sides by $(x-x_1)$:

$$y-y_1=m(x-x_1).$$

That is point-slope form — it simply says every point on the line keeps the same slope back to $(x_1,y_1)$.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

Subtracting the coordinates in a different order on top and bottom. Slope is $m=\dfrac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}$ — both differences must run the same way. Students write $\dfrac{y_2-y_1}{x_1-x_2}$, flipping only the denominator, which negates the slope: an uphill line comes out with a downhill sign.

Pick a "first" point and a "second" point and keep that order in both the numerator and the denominator. It doesn't matter which point is first — $\dfrac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}=\dfrac{y_1-y_2}{x_1-x_2}$ — only that you're consistent.

A line has constant rate of change equal to its slope.

Slope-Intercept & Parallel/Perpendicular

Slope-Intercept Form: $y=mx+b$

$m$=slope, $b$=$y$-intercept (value when $x=0$).

Parallel lines: equal slopes ($m_1=m_2$).

Perpendicular lines: slopes are opposite reciprocals ($m_1\cdot m_2=-1$).

▸ Show Proof

Slope-intercept form is just point-slope form anchored at the $y$-axis. Use the known point $(0,b)$:

$$y-b=m(x-0)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad y=mx+b.$$

Now the perpendicular rule. Picture a line’s direction as “go right $b$, then up $a$,” so its slope is $m_1=\dfrac{a}{b}$. To turn that arrow a quarter-turn ($90^\circ$), swap the two legs and flip one sign — the arrow becomes the direction $(-a,\,b)$.

The slope of this rotated, perpendicular direction is $m_2=\dfrac{b}{-a}=-\dfrac{b}{a}$. Multiplying the two slopes:

$$m_1\cdot m_2=\frac{a}{b}\cdot\left(-\frac{b}{a}\right)=-1.$$

So perpendicular slopes are always negative reciprocals. (Parallel lines point the same direction, so they trivially share the same slope.)

∎

▸ Common Mistake

Getting the perpendicular slope half-right. Perpendicular means the negative reciprocal — flip and change sign. For slope $2$, the perpendicular slope is $-\dfrac{1}{2}$, not $-2$ (only negated) and not $\dfrac{1}{2}$ (only flipped). The check is $m_1\cdot m_2=-1$: confirm $2\cdot(-\tfrac12)=-1$.

The mirror mistake is swapping the two rules — using negative reciprocals for parallel lines. Parallel lines have equal slopes ($m_1=m_2$); the negative-reciprocal rule is for perpendicular lines only.

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Calculate slope
Find the slope of the line through $(1,2)$ and $(5,-3)$.
$m=\dfrac{-3-2}{5-1}=\dfrac{-5}{4}$.
Example 2 — Point-slope form
Find the equation of the line through $(2,3)$ and $(-4,5)$.
$m=\dfrac{5-3}{-4-2}=\dfrac{-1}{3}$. Using $(2,3)$: $y-3=-\dfrac{1}{3}(x-2)\Rightarrow y=-\dfrac{x}{3}+\dfrac{11}{3}$.
Example 3 — Parallel line
Find the line through $(2,-3)$ parallel to $2x+y=6$.
Slope of $2x+y=6$ is $m=-2$. Parallel: $y+3=-2(x-2)\Rightarrow y=-2x+1$.
Example 4 — Perpendicular line
Find the line through $(1,-2)$ perpendicular to $x+3y=6$.
Slope of given line: $-\tfrac{1}{3}$. Perpendicular slope: $3$. Line: $y+2=3(x-1)\Rightarrow y=3x-5$.
Practice Problems
1.
Find the slope of the line through $(-2,4)$ and $(3,-1)$.
$m=\dfrac{-1-4}{3-(-2)}=-1$.
2.
Write the slope-intercept form of the line through $(0,5)$ with slope $-2$.
$y=-2x+5$.
3.
Write the equation of the line through $(3,1)$ with slope $\dfrac{2}{3}$.
$y-1=\dfrac{2}{3}(x-3)\Rightarrow y=\dfrac{2}{3}x-1$.
4.
Find the equation of the line through $(1,4)$ and $(4,4)$.
Slope $=0$; horizontal line $y=4$.
5.
A line has slope $-3$ and passes through $(2,5)$. Find the $y$-intercept.
$5=-3(2)+b\Rightarrow b=11$.
6.
Are $y=2x+3$ and $y=-\tfrac{1}{2}x+1$ perpendicular?
Slopes are $2$ and $-\tfrac{1}{2}$; product $=-1$. Yes, perpendicular.
