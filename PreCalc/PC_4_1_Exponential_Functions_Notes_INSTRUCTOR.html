Exponential Functions

Pre-Calculus  ·  §4.1  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

A colony of bacteria doubles every hour. If you start with 100, how many are there after $t$ hours? What kind of function models this — and why is it fundamentally different from a polynomial?
Exponential Functions

Exponential function: $f(x)=a\cdot b^x$, $b>0$, $b\neq1$, $a>0$.

$a$ = initial value; $b$ = base (growth factor).

  • $b>1$: growth (increasing)
  • $0decay (decreasing)

Key features: continuous, passes through $(0,a)$, horizontal asymptote at $y=0$, always positive.

Laws of Exponents & the Number $e$

Laws: $b^x\cdot b^y=b^{x+y}$; $\dfrac{b^x}{b^y}=b^{x-y}$; $(b^x)^y=b^{xy}$.

Natural base $e\approx2.71828$:
$f(x)=e^x$ — the most natural exponential.

Compound interest: $A=P\left(1+\dfrac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}$

Continuous compounding: $A=Pe^{rt}$

Worked Examples
Example 1 — Identify growth vs. decay
Which are growth? (a) $f(x)=3^x$ (b) $g(x)=(0.5)^x$ (c) $h(x)=e^{-x}$
(a) Growth ($b=3>1$). (b) Decay ($b=0.5<1$). (c) Decay (negative exponent: $e^{-x}=(1/e)^x$, $1/e<1$).
Example 2 — Evaluate
If $f(x)=2\cdot3^x$, find $f(0)$, $f(2)$, $f(-1)$.
$f(0)=2$; $f(2)=18$; $f(-1)=\tfrac{2}{3}$.
Example 3 — Compound interest
\$5{,}000 invested at 4% compounded monthly for 3 years. Find $A$.
$A=5000\left(1+\dfrac{0.04}{12}\right)^{36}\approx\$5{,}637$.
Example 4 — Continuous compounding
\$1{,}000 at 5% continuously for 10 years.
$A=1000e^{0.5}\approx\$1{,}649$.
Practice Problems
1.
Evaluate $f(x)=4\cdot(0.5)^x$ at $x=0,2,4$.
$4, 1, 0.25$.
2.
Is $f(x)=5\cdot1.08^x$ growth or decay?
Growth ($b=1.08>1$).
3.
Simplify $\dfrac{e^{3x}}{e^x}$.
$e^{2x}$.
4.
Find $A$: \$2000 at 3% compounded quarterly for 5 years.
$A=2000(1.0075)^{20}\approx\$2322$.
5.
Bacteria doubles hourly. Starting from 200, write $P(t)$.
$P(t)=200\cdot2^t$.
6.
Sketch the general shape of $f(x)=e^{-x}$ and state its HA.
Decreasing, passes through $(0,1)$, HA: $y=0$.
