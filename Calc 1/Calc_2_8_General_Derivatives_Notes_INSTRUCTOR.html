General Derivatives

Calculus I  ·  §2.8  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

We now have Product Rule, Quotient Rule, and Chain Rule. Real functions combine all three. How do you decide which rule to apply first, and how do you handle nested combinations?
Strategy: Read the Structure

Before differentiating, ask: what is the outermost operation?

  • Product at top level → Product Rule first
  • Quotient at top level → Quotient Rule first
  • Composite (one function inside another) → Chain Rule first

Then apply chain rule to any composite factors inside.

Work outermost to innermost, one layer at a time.

▸ Common Mistake

The most common error here isn't misreading the structure — it's abandoning Product Rule once it's identified. Students see $f(x)=x^2e^x$, correctly call it a product, then differentiate each factor separately and multiply the results: $f'(x)=2x\cdot e^x$. That's not the product rule; that's just "derivative of each piece times each other," and it's wrong. The correct rule keeps both terms: $f'(x)=(x^2)'e^x+x^2(e^x)'=2xe^x+x^2e^x$.

This mistake is easy to catch with a sanity check: if you only wrote down one term after applying Product or Quotient Rule, you dropped half the rule. Product Rule always produces two additive terms; Quotient Rule always produces a single fraction with a subtraction in the numerator — never a lone product of derivatives.

Key Values for Evaluation

Evaluating at $x=0$ or $x=1$ simplifies most mixed-rule expressions:

$e^0=1$   $\ln 1=0$   $\sin 0=0$   $\cos 0=1$

Example: for $f(x)=x^2 e^x$,
$f'(x)=2xe^x+x^2e^x$, so $f'(0)=0+0=0$.

▸ Common Mistake

When asked to evaluate a derivative at a point, students sometimes plug the number into the original function instead of the derivative — computing $f(0)$ when the question asks for $f'(0)$. For $f(x)=x^2e^x$, $f(0)=0$ too in this particular example, which can mask the error; but for most functions $f(a)\neq f'(a)$, and skipping the differentiation step entirely gives a meaningless answer.

Always differentiate first, simplify symbolically if it helps, and only then substitute the number. Writing $f'(x)=\dots$ explicitly before plugging in $x=0$ makes it obvious which function you're evaluating.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Product + Chain
Differentiate $f(x)=x^2\sin(3x)$.
Product rule: $(x^2)'\sin(3x)+x^2(\sin(3x))'$
$=2x\sin(3x)+x^2\cdot 3\cos(3x)$
$=2x\sin(3x)+3x^2\cos(3x)$
Example 2  —  Quotient + Chain
Differentiate $g(x)=\dfrac{e^{2x}}{x^2+1}$.
$\dfrac{2e^{2x}(x^2+1)-e^{2x}\cdot 2x}{(x^2+1)^2}=\dfrac{2e^{2x}(x^2-x+1)}{(x^2+1)^2}$
