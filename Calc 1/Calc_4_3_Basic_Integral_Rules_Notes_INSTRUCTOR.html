Basic Integration Rules

Calculus I  ·  §4.3  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

Riemann sums let us approximate $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$, but computing thousands of rectangles by hand is impractical. Can we reverse the differentiation rules to evaluate integrals exactly? Every derivative rule has a mirror — what does the Power Rule look like going backwards?
Integration Rules

Power Rule $(n\neq -1)$:

$$\int x^n\,dx=\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+C$$

Constant Rule: $\displaystyle\int k\,dx=kx+C$

Constant Multiple: $\displaystyle\int c\,f(x)\,dx=c\int f(x)\,dx$

Sum / Difference:

$\displaystyle\int[f(x)\pm g(x)]\,dx=\int f\,dx\pm\int g\,dx$

▸ Show Proof

The key idea: we want a function whose derivative is $x^n$. We run the Power Rule for derivatives backwards.

From differentiation, we know: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left[x^{n+1}\right] = (n+1)x^n$.

That's almost what we want, but we have an extra factor of $(n+1)$. To cancel it, divide both sides by $(n+1)$ (which is fine as long as $n \neq -1$, since we can't divide by zero):

$$\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}\right] = x^n$$

So $\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}$ is an antiderivative of $x^n$. But it's not the only one — any constant $C$ has derivative zero, so adding $C$ gives another antiderivative. In fact, every antiderivative of $x^n$ has this form. Therefore:

$$\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C$$

You can always verify an integration result by differentiating the right side — you should get the integrand back.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

Students often write $\displaystyle\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^n}{n}+C$ instead of $\displaystyle\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+C$ — dividing by the original exponent $n$ rather than the new one after raising the power. For example, $\int x^3\,dx$ becomes $\frac{x^4}{4}+C$, not $\frac{x^3}{3}+C$. The slip comes from over-focusing on the exponent that was already there and forgetting the exponent changes first — only then does the new exponent become the divisor.

Also common: dropping the $+C$. Since $\int x^n\,dx$ asks for an entire family of antiderivatives, not just the one you'd get by reversing the derivative in your head, omitting $C$ loses that generality — and it typically costs a point on a test even when every other step is right.

$+C$ is the constant of integration — every antiderivative family has infinitely many members differing by a constant. For definite integrals, $C$ cancels and can be dropped.

Common Antiderivatives
  • $\displaystyle\int e^x\,dx = e^x+C$
  • $\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x|+C$
  • $\displaystyle\int \sin x\,dx = -\cos x+C$
  • $\displaystyle\int \cos x\,dx = \sin x+C$

Area below the $x$-axis is negative.
For a definite integral, any region where $f(x)<0$ subtracts from the total. Signed area $\neq$ total area.

▸ Common Mistake

Students treat $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ as if it always returns a positive quantity — "the area under the curve" — and get thrown off (or "fix" their answer) when the result comes out negative. If $f(x)<0$ somewhere on $[a,b]$, the definite integral is signed area: regions below the $x$-axis subtract from the total instead of adding to it.

For instance, $\int_{-2}^{0}x^3\,dx = \left[\frac{x^4}{4}\right]_{-2}^0 = 0-4=-4$, and $-4$ is the correct final answer — not $4$. To get the total (unsigned) area between the curve and the axis, you'd need to split the interval at the roots of $f$ and integrate $|f|$ piece by piece; the plain definite integral won't do that for you automatically.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Power Rule (indefinite)
Find $\displaystyle\int (3x^2+4x)\,dx$.
$\dfrac{3x^3}{3}+\dfrac{4x^2}{2}+C = x^3+2x^2+C$
Example 2  —  Polynomial antiderivative
Find $\displaystyle\int (5x^4-6x+2)\,dx$.
$x^5-3x^2+2x+C$
Example 3  —  Definite integral
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^3 x^2\,dx$.
$\left[\dfrac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^3=\dfrac{27}{3}-0=9$
Example 4  —  Mixed antiderivatives
Find $\displaystyle\int (4e^x+3\cos x)\,dx$.
$4e^x+3\sin x+C$
Practice Problems
1.
Find $\displaystyle\int x^5\,dx$.
$\dfrac{x^6}{6}+C$
2.
Find $\displaystyle\int (6x^2+4x-3)\,dx$.
$2x^3+2x^2-3x+C$
3.
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^2 4x^3\,dx$.
$\left[x^4\right]_0^2=16$
4.
Find $\displaystyle\int (2e^x-\sin x)\,dx$.
$2e^x+\cos x+C$
5.
Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_1^4 \sqrt{x}\,dx$.
$\left[\dfrac{2x^{3/2}}{3}\right]_1^4=\dfrac{16}{3}-\dfrac{2}{3}=\dfrac{14}{3}$
6.
Is $\displaystyle\int_{-2}^{0} x^3\,dx$ positive or negative? Why?
Negative — $x^3<0$ on $(-2,0)$, so the signed area is below the $x$-axis.
