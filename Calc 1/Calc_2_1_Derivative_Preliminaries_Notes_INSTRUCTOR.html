Derivative Preliminaries

Calculus I  ·  §2.1  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

Before we can define the derivative, we need two ideas: average rate of change (secant slope) and what happens as the interval shrinks to zero. What does it mean geometrically when that interval vanishes?
Average Rate of Change

The average rate of change of $f$ over $[a,b]$:

$$\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$$

This is the slope of the secant line through $(a,f(a))$ and $(b,f(b))$.

Units: change in output per unit change in input. For position $s(t)$, this is average velocity.

▸ Geometric Argument

Picture two points sitting on the graph of $f$: $(a, f(a))$ and $(b, f(b))$. The straight line connecting them is called a secant line, and slope is always "rise over run" — how much the output changes divided by how much the input changes.

The rise is the change in output, $f(b)-f(a)$. The run is the change in input, $b-a$. So the slope of that secant line is $$\text{slope} = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$$

That ratio is exactly the average rate of change. It's an average because it only looks at the two endpoints — it has no idea whether $f$ wiggled up and down in between. Whatever happened along the way, the net change over the whole interval is summarized by this one slope.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

Average rate of change is the secant slope over the whole interval — not the slope at $a$ or at $b$. It's an average: the curve can rise and fall in between and still have, say, ARC $=0$ on $[a,b]$ when the endpoints are equal.

Don't report it as "the rate at $a$." That's the instantaneous rate — the derivative $f'(a)$ — which generally differs from the average.

Difference quotient form (used to build the derivative): $\dfrac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}$ where $h=b-a$. As $h\to 0$, the secant slope approaches the tangent slope.

The Derivative: Preview

$f'(a)$ = slope of the tangent line at $x=a$

$$f'(a)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}$$

Derivative = instantaneous rate of change = slope.

If $f'(a)>0$: $f$ is increasing at $a$.
If $f'(a)<0$: $f$ is decreasing at $a$.
If $f'(a)=0$: $f$ has a horizontal tangent at $a$.

▸ Geometric Argument

Here's where this definition comes from. Start with the secant line through $(a, f(a))$ and a nearby point $(a+h, f(a+h))$. Its slope is the difference quotient $$\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}$$ which is just the average rate of change over the short interval from $a$ to $a+h$ — a reasonable first guess at how steep $f$ is right around $x=a$.

The problem: that guess still depends on $h$, the distance to the second point. If $h$ is large, the secant line might cut across the curve and miss what's really happening at $a$. The fix is to shrink $h$ toward $0$ — slide the second point back along the curve until it's right on top of the first.

As that happens, the secant line pivots and settles into a limiting position: the tangent line, the line that just grazes the curve at $x=a$ and matches its direction there exactly. So we define $$f'(a)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}$$ to be whatever number the secant slopes are homing in on. That limiting slope — the slope of the tangent line — is what we mean by the instantaneous rate of change at $a$.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

You can't evaluate $\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}$ by setting $h=0$ — that gives $\frac00$. Expand $f(a+h)$, simplify the numerator, and cancel the $h$ in the denominator first, then let $h\to0$.

Watch the algebra while expanding: $(a+h)^2=a^2+2ah+h^2$, not $a^2+h^2$. Dropping the $2ah$ cross term is the usual slip that wrecks the limit.

As $Q \to P$, the secant slope approaches the tangent slope $f'(1)=2$
Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Average rate of change
Find the average rate of change of $f(x)=x^2$ on $[1,3]$.
$\dfrac{f(3)-f(1)}{3-1}=\dfrac{9-1}{2}=\dfrac{8}{2}=4$
Example 2  —  Secant slope with $h$
Find the slope of the secant line for $f(x)=x^2$ from $x=2$ to $x=2+h$.
$\dfrac{(2+h)^2-4}{h}=\dfrac{4+4h+h^2-4}{h}=\dfrac{4h+h^2}{h}=4+h$
Example 3  —  Limit of secant slope
Using the result above, find $f'(2)$ for $f(x)=x^2$.
$f'(2)=\displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}(4+h)=4$
Tangent slope at $x=2$ is $4$.
Example 4  —  Average velocity
Position $s(t)=t^2+2t$ (meters). Find average velocity on $[1,3]$.
$\dfrac{s(3)-s(1)}{3-1}=\dfrac{15-3}{2}=\dfrac{12}{2}=6$ m/s
Practice Problems
1.
Find the average rate of change of $f(x)=x^2+1$ on $[0,2]$.
$\dfrac{5-1}{2}=2$
2.
Find the slope of the secant line for $f(x)=3x^2$ from $x=1$ to $x=1+h$.
$\dfrac{3(1+h)^2-3}{h}=\dfrac{6h+3h^2}{h}=6+3h$
3.
Using the result above, find $f'(1)$ for $f(x)=3x^2$.
$\lim_{h\to 0}(6+3h)=6$
4.
Position $s(t)=2t^2$. Find average velocity on $[2,5]$.
$\dfrac{50-8}{3}=14$ units/s
5.
Find the average rate of change of $f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x}$ on $[1,2]$.
$\dfrac{1/2-1}{1}=-\dfrac{1}{2}$
6.
For $f(x)=x^2$, as $h\to 0$, the secant slope $4+h$ approaches what value?
$4$ — this is $f'(2)$
