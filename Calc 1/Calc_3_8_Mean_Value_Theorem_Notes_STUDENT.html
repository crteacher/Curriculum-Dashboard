Mean Value Theorem

Calculus I  ·  §3.8  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

If you drive 120 miles in 2 hours, your average speed is 60 mph. The Mean Value Theorem says: at some moment, your speedometer must have read exactly 60 mph. Can we always find that exact moment?
Mean Value Theorem

If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, then

$$\exists\,c\in(a,b)\text{ such that }f'(c)=\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$$

The instantaneous rate of change equals the average rate of change at some interior point.

Geometrically: there exists a point where the tangent line is parallel to the secant line through $(a,f(a))$ and $(b,f(b))$.

Finding $c$ & Rolle's Theorem

To find $c$:

  1. Compute the average rate: $m=\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$
  2. Solve $f'(x)=m$
  3. Keep solutions in $(a,b)$

Rolle's Theorem (special case):
If $f(a)=f(b)$, then $m=0$, so $\exists\,c$ with $f'(c)=0$.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Average rate of change
Find the average rate of change of $f(x)=x^2$ on $[1,3]$.
Example 2  —  Finding $c$
Find $c$ guaranteed by MVT for $f(x)=x^2$ on $[1,3]$.
Example 3  —  Rolle's Theorem
Apply Rolle's Theorem to $f(x)=x^2-4x$ on $[0,4]$.
Example 4  —  MVT application
For $f(x)=x^3-x$ on $[0,2]$, find $c$ guaranteed by MVT.
