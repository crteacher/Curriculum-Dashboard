Critical Points & EVT

Calculus I  ·  §3.5  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

Where does a function reach its highest or lowest value on an interval? The answer must occur at special points — places where the derivative is zero, undefined, or at the boundary. Can we find all candidates systematically?
Critical Numbers

$c$ is a critical number of $f$ if $c$ is in the domain and:

  • $f'(c)=0$,  OR
  • $f'(c)$ does not exist

Absolute extrema can only occur at critical numbers or at the endpoints of a closed interval.

▸ Common Mistake

Students only look for where $f'(c)=0$ and stop there, forgetting the "OR $f'(c)$ does not exist" half of the definition. That misses critical numbers at sharp corners or cusps — places where the function is defined but not differentiable.

Classic example: $f(x)=x^{2/3}$ has $f'(x)=\tfrac{2}{3}x^{-1/3}$, which is never zero. Solve $f'(x)=0$ and you'll conclude (wrongly) that there are no critical numbers at all. But $f'(0)$ is undefined (division by zero), and $x=0$ is in the domain — so $x=0$ is a critical number, and in fact the location of a sharp minimum. Always check both conditions, not just the first.

A critical number is a candidate for an extremum — not every critical number gives one. We need more tests (first or second derivative test) to classify.

Extreme Value Theorem & Closed Interval Method

EVT: If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, then $f$ attains both an absolute max and an absolute min on $[a,b]$.

Closed Interval Method:

  1. Find all critical numbers of $f$ in $(a,b)$
  2. Evaluate $f$ at each critical number and at $a$, $b$
  3. Largest value = absolute max; smallest = absolute min
▸ Common Mistake

Students find the critical numbers, evaluate $f$ there, and stop — forgetting to also evaluate $f$ at the two endpoints $a$ and $b$. The absolute max or min very often lives at an endpoint, not at a critical number, especially when the function is monotonic between critical points.

The Closed Interval Method is a fixed checklist for a reason: compute $f$ at every critical number in $(a,b)$ and at both endpoints, then compare all of those values together. Skipping the endpoints silently throws away two of the candidates before the comparison even starts. (And remember EVT itself requires a closed, bounded interval and continuity — on an open interval like $(0,\pi/2)$ for $\tan x$, there may be no absolute max at all.)
