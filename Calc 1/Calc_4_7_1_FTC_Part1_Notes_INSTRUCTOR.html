FTC Part 1 — The Accumulation Function

Calculus I  ·  §4.7.1  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

Define $F(x)=\displaystyle\int_a^x f(t)\,dt$. This accumulation function grows as $x$ increases. What is its derivative? And what does the behavior of $f$ tell us about the shape of $F$?
FTC Part 1 — Accumulation Function

If $F(x)=\displaystyle\int_a^x f(t)\,dt$, then:

$$F'(x)=f(x)$$

The derivative of the accumulation function equals the integrand evaluated at the upper limit.

Reading $F$ from $f$:
$f(x)>0$ → $F$ is increasing
$f(x)<0$ → $F$ is decreasing
$f(x)=0$ → $F$ has a horizontal tangent

▸ Show Proof

Think of $F(x)$ as a running total of area from $a$ up to wherever $x$ currently sits. We want to know how fast that total is growing — i.e., $F'(x)$.

Use the limit definition: $F'(x) = \displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{F(x+h)-F(x)}{h}$.

The difference $F(x+h) - F(x)$ is the area accumulated between $x$ and $x+h$:

$$F(x+h)-F(x) = \int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt$$

For very small $h$, this thin strip of area is nearly a rectangle: height $\approx f(x)$, width $= h$. So $F(x+h)-F(x) \approx f(x)\cdot h$.

Divide by $h$ and take the limit:

$$F'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x)\cdot h}{h} = f(x)$$

The rate at which area accumulates is exactly the height of the curve at that point — which makes intuitive sense.

∎

Chain Rule Version

When the upper limit is a function $g(x)$:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^{g(x)}f(t)\,dt=f(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$$

Steps:

  1. Identify the upper limit: $g(x)$
  2. Evaluate the integrand at $g(x)$: $f(g(x))$
  3. Multiply by $g'(x)$

Example: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\displaystyle\int_0^{x^3}\cos t\,dt=\cos(x^3)\cdot 3x^2$

▸ Show Proof

This follows directly from combining FTC Part 1 with the Chain Rule.

Define the accumulation function $A(u) = \displaystyle\int_a^u f(t)\,dt$. By FTC Part 1, $A'(u) = f(u)$.

The integral in question is just $A$ evaluated at $g(x)$:

$$\int_a^{g(x)} f(t)\,dt = A(g(x))$$

Differentiate with respect to $x$ using the Chain Rule:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\bigl[A(g(x))\bigr] = A'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$$

Substitute $A'(u) = f(u)$ with $u = g(x)$:

$$= f(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$$

The key idea: FTC Part 1 handles the integral, and the Chain Rule handles the composed upper limit. They multiply together.

∎

To find $F(c)$ numerically: use FTC Part 2 to evaluate $\displaystyle\int_a^c f(t)\,dt$ directly.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Basic derivative
Let $F(x)=\displaystyle\int_0^x t^3\,dt$. Find $F'(x)$.
By FTC Part 1: $F'(x)=x^3$
Example 2  —  Chain rule version
Let $G(x)=\displaystyle\int_1^{x^2}\sin t\,dt$. Find $G'(x)$.
$G'(x)=\sin(x^2)\cdot 2x=2x\sin(x^2)$
Example 3  —  Find $F(c)$ numerically
Let $F(x)=\displaystyle\int_0^x(t^2+1)\,dt$. Find $F(2)$.
$F(2)=\displaystyle\int_0^2(t^2+1)\,dt=\left[\dfrac{t^3}{3}+t\right]_0^2=\dfrac{8}{3}+2=\dfrac{14}{3}$
Example 4  —  Increasing/decreasing
Let $F(x)=\displaystyle\int_0^x (t^2-4)\,dt$. Where is $F$ increasing?
$F'(x)=x^2-4>0$ when $|x|>2$
$F$ is increasing on $(-\infty,-2)$ and $(2,\infty)$
Practice Problems
1.
Let $F(x)=\displaystyle\int_0^x e^t\,dt$. Find $F'(x)$.
$F'(x)=e^x$
2.
Let $G(x)=\displaystyle\int_2^{x^3}\cos t\,dt$. Find $G'(x)$.
$G'(x)=\cos(x^3)\cdot 3x^2$
3.
Let $F(x)=\displaystyle\int_0^x(2t-6)\,dt$. Find $F(4)$.
$F(4)=\left[t^2-6t\right]_0^4=16-24=-8$
4.
Let $F(x)=\displaystyle\int_0^x(2t-6)\,dt$. Where is $F$ increasing?
$F'(x)=2x-6>0 \Rightarrow x>3$
5.
Let $H(x)=\displaystyle\int_x^0 t^2\,dt$. Find $H'(x)$.
Flip limits: $H(x)=-\displaystyle\int_0^x t^2\,dt$ → $H'(x)=-x^2$
6.
Let $F(x)=\displaystyle\int_0^{\sqrt{x}} t\,dt$. Find $F'(x)$.
$F'(x)=\sqrt{x}\cdot\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}=\dfrac{1}{2}$
