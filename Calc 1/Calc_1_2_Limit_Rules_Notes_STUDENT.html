Limit Rules & Algebraic Techniques

Calculus I  ·  §1.2  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

We know limits exist, but how do we actually compute them? Can we always just plug in? And when we get $\frac{0}{0}$, what does that tell us — and what do we do next?
Limit Laws

If $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)$ and $\lim_{x\to a}g(x)$ both exist:

  • $\lim[f \pm g] = \lim f \pm \lim g$
  • $\lim[f \cdot g] = (\lim f)(\lim g)$
  • $\lim\dfrac{f}{g} = \dfrac{\lim f}{\lim g}$  (when $\lim g \neq 0$)
  • $\lim[cf] = c\lim f$  (constant multiple)
  • $\lim[f^n] = (\lim f)^n$

Direct substitution: for polynomials and continuous functions, $\lim_{x\to a}f(x)=f(a)$.

▸ Show Proof

Here's why $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}[f(x)+g(x)] = \lim_{x\to a}f(x) + \lim_{x\to a}g(x)$ has to be true.

Suppose $f(x)\to L$ and $g(x)\to M$ as $x\to a$. "Gets arbitrarily close to $L$" just means that for $x$ near $a$, we can write $f(x) = L + (\text{a tiny leftover error})$, and likewise $g(x) = M + (\text{a tiny leftover error})$ — errors that shrink to $0$ as $x\to a$.

Add the two functions: $$f(x)+g(x) = (L+M) + \big[(\text{tiny error}_1) + (\text{tiny error}_2)\big]$$

The bracket is a sum of two quantities that are each shrinking to $0$, so the bracket itself shrinks to $0$. That means $f(x)+g(x)$ is getting arbitrarily close to the single number $L+M$.

But "getting arbitrarily close to $L+M$ as $x\to a$" is exactly the definition of $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to a}[f(x)+g(x)] = L+M$. The same idea (errors combining into a smaller error) is what makes the product, quotient, and constant-multiple rules work too.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

The quotient law $\lim\dfrac{f}{g}=\dfrac{\lim f}{\lim g}$ carries a condition that's easy to skip: it applies only when $\lim g\neq 0$. Students use it blindly, land on $\frac{0}{0}$, and write "DNE" — but $\frac{0}{0}$ doesn't mean the limit failed, it means the law doesn't apply yet and you must simplify (factor or rationalize) first.

Every limit law assumes the individual limits exist. Confirm the pieces behave before splitting a limit into separate pieces.

The 0/0 Indeterminate Form

If direct substitution gives $\dfrac{0}{0}$, the limit may still exist — factor and cancel:

Factor: $\dfrac{x^2-a^2}{x-a}=\dfrac{(x-a)(x+a)}{x-a}=x+a$

Rationalize: multiply by $\dfrac{\sqrt{x}+a}{\sqrt{x}+a}$ to clear a radical.

Expand: for difference quotients $\dfrac{(x+h)^n - x^n}{h}$, expand and cancel $h$.

▸ Show Proof

These factoring identities aren't assumptions — they're just multiplication facts you can check by expanding the right-hand side.

Difference of squares: multiply out $(x-a)(x+a)$ using FOIL: $$ (x-a)(x+a) = x\cdot x + x\cdot a - a\cdot x - a\cdot a = x^2 + ax - ax - a^2 = x^2 - a^2 $$ The two middle terms $ax$ and $-ax$ cancel, leaving exactly $x^2-a^2$. So the identity checks out in both directions.

Difference of cubes: the same cancellation trick, just with one more term. Multiply $(x-a)(x^2+ax+a^2)$ term by term: $$ x^3 + ax^2 + a^2x \;-\; ax^2 - a^2x - a^3 $$ Look closely: $ax^2$ cancels with $-ax^2$, and $a^2x$ cancels with $-a^2x$. All that survives is $x^3 - a^3$.

Why this matters for limits: when direct substitution gives $\frac{0}{0}$ at $x=a$, it means both the top and bottom of the fraction equal zero when $x=a$ — which means $(x-a)$ divides evenly into both. These identities tell you exactly how to peel that shared factor off, cancel it, and reveal the limit hiding underneath.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

$\frac{0}{0}$ is an indeterminate form, not an answer. Students reach it and conclude the limit is $0$ (or $1$, or "undefined/DNE"). It means none of those yet — it's a signal that the top and bottom share a factor of $(x-a)$ you haven't cancelled.

After factoring or rationalizing, the limit could be any number, $\infty$, or genuinely DNE. Getting $\frac{0}{0}$ is the start of the problem, not the end.

Difference of cubes: $x^3-a^3=(x-a)(x^2+ax+a^2)$ — useful when the numerator is a cubic.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Direct substitution
Find $\lim_{x\to 3}(2x^2-4x+1)$.
Example 2  —  Factor and cancel
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 1}\dfrac{x^2-1}{x-1}$.
Example 3  —  Rationalize
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 4}\dfrac{\sqrt{x}-2}{x-4}$.
Example 4  —  Difference quotient
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\dfrac{(5+h)^2-25}{h}$.
