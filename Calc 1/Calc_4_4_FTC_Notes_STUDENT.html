Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Calculus I  ·  §4.4  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

We've been computing areas with rectangles. The Fundamental Theorem gives us an exact method: antidifferentiate, then plug in endpoints. Why does the connection between antiderivatives and areas work?
FTC Part 2 — Evaluation Theorem

If $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$ on $[a,b]$:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b)-F(a)$$

Notation: $\Big[F(x)\Big]_a^b = F(b)-F(a)$

Procedure:

  1. Find an antiderivative $F$ (ignore $+C$)
  2. Evaluate $F(b)$
  3. Evaluate $F(a)$
  4. Subtract: $F(b)-F(a)$
▸ Show Proof

Here's the key idea: define the accumulation function $A(x) = \displaystyle\int_a^x f(t)\,dt$, which measures the signed area from $a$ up to $x$.

By FTC Part 1 (proved below), $A'(x) = f(x)$. So $A$ is an antiderivative of $f$.

Now let $F$ be any antiderivative of $f$. Since two antiderivatives of the same function can only differ by a constant, we have $F(x) = A(x) + C$ for some constant $C$.

Compute $F(b) - F(a)$:

$$F(b)-F(a) = \bigl[A(b)+C\bigr] - \bigl[A(a)+C\bigr] = A(b) - A(a)$$

Notice: $A(b) = \displaystyle\int_a^b f(t)\,dt$ (the total area from $a$ to $b$), and $A(a) = \displaystyle\int_a^a f(t)\,dt = 0$ (no width, no area). So:

$$F(b)-F(a) = \int_a^b f(t)\,dt$$

The constant $C$ cancels — that's why it doesn't matter which antiderivative you pick.

∎

FTC Part 1 — Derivative of an Integral

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t)\,dt=f(x)$$

The derivative of the accumulation function is the integrand, evaluated at the upper limit.

With chain rule:
$\dfrac{d}{dx}\displaystyle\int_a^{g(x)}f(t)\,dt=f(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$

▸ Show Proof

Let $A(x) = \displaystyle\int_a^x f(t)\,dt$. We want to find $A'(x)$ using the limit definition of the derivative:

$$A'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{A(x+h)-A(x)}{h}$$

The numerator is the area from $a$ to $x+h$, minus the area from $a$ to $x$. That difference is just the thin strip of area from $x$ to $x+h$:

$$A(x+h)-A(x) = \int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt$$

For small $h$, this strip is nearly a rectangle with height $f(x)$ and width $h$, so its area is approximately $f(x)\cdot h$. (We're assuming $f$ is continuous here, which holds for every function in this course.)

Dividing by $h$ and taking the limit:

$$A'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x)\cdot h}{h} = f(x)$$

The derivative of the area-accumulation function is exactly the function you're integrating, evaluated at the moving upper limit.

∎

Key antiderivative formulas:
$\int x^n\,dx=\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+C$   $(n\neq-1)$
$\int e^x\,dx=e^x+C$   $\int\cos x\,dx=\sin x+C$
$\int\sin x\,dx=-\cos x+C$   $\int\dfrac{1}{x}\,dx=\ln|x|+C$
