Concavity & 2nd Derivative Test

Calculus I  ·  §3.6.5  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

The first derivative tells us where $f$ goes up or down. The second derivative tells us whether the graph curves up like a cup or down like a hill. Can we use $f''$ to classify critical numbers more quickly?
Concavity & Inflection Points

$f''(x)>0$ on interval → $f$ is concave up ∪
$f''(x)<0$ on interval → $f$ is concave down ∩

An inflection point is where $f''$ changes sign.
To find: set $f''(x)=0$, check that the sign actually changes.

▸ Common Mistake

Students conflate concavity with monotonicity — reading "concave up" as "the function is increasing" and "concave down" as "the function is decreasing." These are answers to two completely different questions. Increasing/decreasing is about $f'$ (which way the graph is heading); concave up/down is about $f''$ (which way the graph is bending, cup vs. hill).

A function can be increasing while concave down (climbing, but the rate of climb is slowing — like the top half of $\sqrt{x}$), or decreasing while concave up (falling, but leveling off). Always check which derivative a question is actually asking about before answering.

Concave up means the tangent line lies below the curve. Concave down means it lies above. An inflection point is NOT just where $f''=0$ — the sign must change.

Concave up (cup): tangent lies below; concave down (hill): tangent lies above
Second Derivative Test

If $f'(c)=0$:

  • $f''(c)>0$ → concave up at $c$ → local min
  • $f''(c)<0$ → concave down at $c$ → local max
  • $f''(c)=0$ → inconclusive (use FDT instead)

Memory: concave up = cup shape = min at bottom.

▸ Common Mistake

When $f''(c)=0$, students treat it like a third case with its own conclusion — often guessing "neither max nor min" or "saddle point" — instead of recognizing that $f''(c)=0$ means the test simply gives no information. The point could still be a local max, a local min, or neither; the Second Derivative Test just can't tell.

$f(x)=x^4$ at $x=0$ is the standard warning example: $f''(0)=0$, yet $x=0$ is very much a local (and global) minimum. Whenever $f''(c)=0$, you must fall back to the First Derivative Test — checking the sign of $f'$ on either side of $c$ — to actually classify the point.

The 2nd Derivative Test is often faster than FDT when $f''$ is easy to compute. But if $f''(c)=0$, you must fall back to the First Derivative Test.
