Differentiability

Calculus I  ·  §2.2.6  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

We defined $f'(c)$ as a limit. But limits don't always exist. What does the graph of $f$ look like at a point where that limit fails?
Differentiability

$f$ is differentiable at $c$ if $f'(c)$ exists, i.e.

$\displaystyle\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(c+h)-f(c)}{h}$   exists (is finite).

Three ways a function can fail to be differentiable:

  • Discontinuity — limit cannot exist if $f$ jumps
  • Corner — left and right slopes exist but differ
     Example: $f(x)=|x|$ at $x=0$
  • Vertical tangent — slope blows up to $\infty$
     Example: $f(x)=x^{1/3}$ at $x=0$

Checking piecewise functions at join point $x=c$:
(1) Continuity: left and right limits of $f$ must agree.
(2) Equal slopes: left and right derivatives must agree.
Both must hold. A function can be continuous but still fail (2) — that gives a corner.

Differentiability Implies Continuity

Theorem: If $f$ is differentiable at $c$, then $f$ is continuous at $c$.

Proof sketch:

$\displaystyle\lim_{x\to c}[f(x)-f(c)]=\lim_{x\to c}\frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c}\cdot(x-c)=f'(c)\cdot 0=0$

So $\lim_{x\to c}f(x)=f(c)$ — which is continuity.

The converse is FALSE: $f(x)=|x|$ is continuous at $0$ but not differentiable.

Summary:
Differentiable  $\Rightarrow$  Continuous  (always true)
Continuous  $\Rightarrow$  Differentiable  (NOT always true)
Not continuous  $\Rightarrow$  Not differentiable  (always true)

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Absolute value — a corner
Is $f(x)=|x|$ differentiable at $x=0$?
Right derivative: $\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{|h|}{h}=1$
Left derivative:  $\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{|h|}{h}=-1$
$1\neq -1 \Rightarrow$ not differentiable (corner).
Note: $f$ is still continuous at $0$.
Example 2  —  Piecewise — slope mismatch
$f(x)=\begin{cases}2x+1 & x<1 \\ 3x & x\geq 1\end{cases}$   Differentiable at $x=1$?
Continuity: $2(1)+1=3$, $3(1)=3$ ✓
Left slope $=2$, right slope $=3$
$2\neq 3 \Rightarrow$ not differentiable (corner).
Example 3  —  Piecewise — both conditions satisfied
$f(x)=\begin{cases}x^2 & x\leq 2 \\ 4x-4 & x>2\end{cases}$   Differentiable at $x=2$?
Continuity: $f(2^-)=4$, $f(2^+)=4$ ✓
Left slope $=2x\big|_{x=2}=4$, right slope $=4$ ✓
Differentiable at $x=2$.
Example 4  —  Find the parameter
$f(x)=\begin{cases}ax-4 & x\leq 2 \\ x^2 & x>2\end{cases}$   Find $a$ so $f$ is differentiable at $x=2$.
Right slope: $f'(2^+)=2x\big|_{x=2}=4$, so need left slope $a=4$.
Check continuity: $a(2)-4=4$ ⇒ $2a=8$ ⇒ $a=4$ ✓
$a=4$
Practice Problems
1.
Is $f(x)=|x-3|$ differentiable at $x=3$?
No — corner. Left slope $=-1$, right slope $=1$.
2.
Is $f(x)=x^3$ differentiable at $x=0$?
Yes — polynomial, $f'(0)=0$.
3.
$f(x)=\begin{cases}2x+1 & x<1\\ 3x & x\geq 1\end{cases}$   Differentiable at $x=1$?
No — continuous (both $=3$), but slopes $2\neq 3$.
4.
$f(x)=\begin{cases}x^2 & x\leq 2\\ 4x-4 & x>2\end{cases}$   Differentiable at $x=2$?
Yes — continuous (both $=4$) and slopes both $=4$.
5.
If $f$ is differentiable at $x=5$, is $f$ continuous there?
Yes — differentiability implies continuity.
6.
$f(x)=\begin{cases}ax-4 & x\leq 2\\ x^2 & x>2\end{cases}$   Find $a$ for differentiability.
$a=4$  (slopes and values must both match at $x=2$)
