The Derivative at a Point

Calculus I  ·  §2.2  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

We defined the derivative as a limit of secant slopes. Now we calculate it — and use it to write tangent line equations. What's the difference between the derivative value $f'(c)$ and the tangent line at $x=c$?
The Derivative at a Point

Definition ($h$-form):

$$f'(c)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(c+h)-f(c)}{h}$$

Procedure:

  1. Compute $f(c+h)$ by substituting $c+h$ everywhere
  2. Subtract $f(c)$
  3. Divide by $h$ and simplify (cancel $h$)
  4. Take the limit as $h\to 0$
▸ Geometric Argument

The key idea: instantaneous slope is the limit of average slopes. Here's where this formula comes from.

Draw a secant line through the points $(c,\,f(c))$ and $(c+h,\,f(c+h))$. Its slope is plain rise-over-run:

$$m_{\text{sec}} = \frac{f(c+h)-f(c)}{(c+h)-c} = \frac{f(c+h)-f(c)}{h}$$

Now let $h\to 0$. The second point slides toward $(c,\,f(c))$ along the curve, and the secant line rotates until it becomes the tangent line. The slope of that tangent line is the derivative.

We can't plug in $h=0$ directly (that gives $\tfrac{0}{0}$), so we simplify the fraction algebraically — canceling $h$ — and then take the limit. That four-step procedure above is exactly this process.

∎

Tangent Line

Tangent line at $x=c$:

$$y=f'(c)(x-c)+f(c)$$

The slope is $f'(c)$; the point is $(c,\,f(c))$.

$f'(c)>0$: tangent slopes upward (increasing)
$f'(c)<0$: tangent slopes downward (decreasing)
$f'(c)=0$: horizontal tangent

▸ Show Proof

We know two things about the tangent line at $x=c$:

1. Its slope is $f'(c)$.

2. It passes through the point $(c,\,f(c))$ on the curve.

Any line with slope $m$ passing through $(x_1,\,y_1)$ can be written in point-slope form:

$$y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$$

Substitute $m = f'(c)$ and $(x_1,\,y_1) = (c,\,f(c))$:

$$y - f(c) = f'(c)(x - c)$$

Solve for $y$ by adding $f(c)$ to both sides:

$$y = f'(c)(x-c)+f(c)$$

That's the formula — it's point-slope form with the derivative supplying the slope and the curve supplying the point.

∎

Normal line (perpendicular to tangent): slope $= -1/f'(c)$, same point $(c, f(c))$. The normal line equation is $y = -\dfrac{1}{f'(c)}(x-c)+f(c)$.
