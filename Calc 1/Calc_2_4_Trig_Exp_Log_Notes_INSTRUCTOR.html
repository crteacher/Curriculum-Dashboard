Derivatives of sin, cos, eˣ, ln(x)

Calculus I  ·  §2.4  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

The Power Rule handles polynomial-type functions. But what about $\sin x$, $e^x$, and $\ln x$? These have their own derivative rules — and unlike the chain rule versions, these formulas apply only when the argument is plain $x$.
The Four Core Formulas

$$\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x]=\cos x \qquad \frac{d}{dx}[\cos x]=-\sin x$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}[e^x]=e^x \qquad \frac{d}{dx}[\ln x]=\frac{1}{x}$$

Combine with constant multiples and sums/differences just like the Power Rule.

Important: these formulas apply only when the argument is plain $x$ (not $2x$, $x^2$, etc. — that needs the Chain Rule).

▸ Show Proof

Why $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\sin x]=\cos x$: Start from the limit definition and apply the angle addition formula $\sin(x+h)=\sin x\cos h+\cos x\sin h$:

$$\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x]=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sin(x+h)-\sin x}{h}=\lim_{h\to 0}\!\left[\sin x\cdot\frac{\cos h-1}{h}+\cos x\cdot\frac{\sin h}{h}\right]$$

Two special limits — $\lim_{h\to 0}\dfrac{\sin h}{h}=1$ and $\lim_{h\to 0}\dfrac{\cos h-1}{h}=0$ — can be verified geometrically using the unit circle. Substituting gives $\sin x\cdot 0+\cos x\cdot 1=\cos x$. An identical argument with $\cos(x+h)=\cos x\cos h-\sin x\sin h$ gives $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\cos x]=-\sin x$.

Why $\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^x]=e^x$: Factor $e^x$ out of the limit:

$$\frac{d}{dx}[e^x]=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{e^{x+h}-e^x}{h}=e^x\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{e^h-1}{h}$$

The number $e\approx 2.718$ is defined precisely so that this last limit equals $1$, making $e^x$ the unique exponential whose derivative equals itself.

Why $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln x]=\dfrac{1}{x}$: Use log properties to rewrite the difference quotient, then substitute $u=h/x$:

$$\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x]=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{1}{h}\ln\!\left(1+\frac{h}{x}\right)=\frac{1}{x}\lim_{u\to 0}\frac{\ln(1+u)}{u}=\frac{1}{x}\cdot 1=\frac{1}{x}$$

The limit $\lim_{u\to 0}\frac{\ln(1+u)}{u}=1$ is another consequence of how $e$ is defined.

∎

Key Values to Know

$\sin(0)=0$   $\cos(0)=1$   $e^0=1$   $\ln(1)=0$

$\sin\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right)=1$   $\cos\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right)=0$

Use these to evaluate derivatives at $x=0$, $x=1$, $x=\pi/2$.

Memory trick for trig: derivative of sine is cosine (positive); derivative of cosine is negative sine. The negative sign moves from cosine to its derivative, not sine.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Mixed function
Differentiate $f(x)=3\sin x - 2e^x + 5x^2$.
$f'(x)=3\cos x - 2e^x + 10x$
Example 2  —  Tangent line
Find the tangent line to $f(x)=e^x$ at $x=0$.
$f'(0)=e^0=1$, $f(0)=1$
$y=1(x-0)+1=x+1$
Example 3  —  Trig tangent line
Find the tangent line to $f(x)=\sin x$ at $x=\pi/2$.
$f'(\pi/2)=\cos(\pi/2)=0$, $f(\pi/2)=1$
$y=0(x-\pi/2)+1=1$   (horizontal tangent)
Example 4  —  Natural log derivative
Differentiate $g(x)=4\ln x - x^3 + \cos x$.
$g'(x)=\dfrac{4}{x}-3x^2-\sin x$
Practice Problems
1.
Differentiate $f(x)=5\cos x + 2x^3$.
$-5\sin x + 6x^2$
2.
Differentiate $g(x)=e^x - \ln x + 7$.
$e^x - \dfrac{1}{x}$
3.
Find $f'(0)$ for $f(x)=\sin x + e^x$.
$f'(x)=\cos x+e^x$; $f'(0)=1+1=2$
4.
Find the tangent line to $f(x)=\ln x$ at $x=1$.
$f'(1)=1$, $f(1)=0$. Line: $y=x-1$
5.
Find all $x\in[0,2\pi]$ where $f(x)=\sin x$ has horizontal tangents.
$\cos x=0 \Rightarrow x=\pi/2,\,3\pi/2$
6.
Differentiate $h(x)=3\sin x - 4\cos x + e^x - 2\ln x$.
$3\cos x+4\sin x+e^x-\dfrac{2}{x}$
