Implicit Differentiation

Calculus I  ·  §3.1  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

Some curves — like circles and ellipses — can't be written as $y=f(x)$ neatly. But we can still find slopes. How do we differentiate an equation that mixes $x$ and $y$ on the same side?
Implicit Differentiation

Procedure:

  1. Differentiate both sides w.r.t. $x$
  2. Every $y$-term picks up a $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ factor (chain rule)
  3. Collect all $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ terms on one side
  4. Factor out and solve for $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$

Example: $x^2+y^2=r^2$
$2x+2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{x}{y}$

▸ Common Mistake

Students differentiate $y$-terms as if $y$ were just another constant with respect to $x$, writing $\dfrac{d}{dx}[y^2]=2y$ instead of $2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}$. But $y$ is secretly a function of $x$ here — that's the whole point of "implicit" — so every time you differentiate something involving $y$, the chain rule demands a $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ factor, exactly the way $\dfrac{d}{dx}[u^2]=2u\cdot u'$ works for any function $u$.

A quick check: if your derivative has zero occurrences of $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ anywhere, you almost certainly forgot to differentiate a $y$-term at all. Every implicit differentiation problem with $y$ in the original equation should produce at least one $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ in the result.

Product Rule on Mixed Terms

When $x$ and $y$ are multiplied, use the Product Rule:

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[xy]=y+x\dfrac{dy}{dx}$

$\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^2y]=2xy+x^2\dfrac{dy}{dx}$

Finding slope at a point:
Solve for $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ as a formula in $x$ and $y$, then substitute the coordinates of the point.

▸ Common Mistake

On mixed terms like $xy$, students apply the chain rule to the $y$ but forget the product rule needs two terms — writing $\dfrac{d}{dx}[xy]=x\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ alone (only differentiating the $y$ factor) instead of the full $y+x\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ (differentiate $x$ times $y$, plus $x$ times the derivative of $y$). The $x$ factor still needs its own product-rule term even though it's the "easy" one.

The same slip happens with $x^2y$: the correct derivative is $2xy+x^2\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ — one term from differentiating $x^2$ (treating $y$ as along for the ride) and one term from differentiating $y$ (which needs the $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ chain factor). Dropping either term is a product-rule error, not an implicit-differentiation error, so the fix is the same: always write both pieces before simplifying.</
