Limits at Infinity & Unbounded Limits

Calculus I  ·  §1.4  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

Consider $f(x)=1/x$. What happens as $x$ gets very large? What happens as $x$ approaches $0$ from the right? These look similar but are two completely different questions — one is about end behavior, one is about blowing up near a point.
Two Distinct Ideas

Limits at infinity ($x\to\pm\infty$): end behavior as $x$ grows without bound.
These describe horizontal asymptotes.

Limits to infinity ($x\to a$, $f\to\pm\infty$): the function grows without bound near $x=a$.
These describe vertical asymptotes.

Technique for $x\to\infty$: divide every term by the highest power of $x$. Each $c/x^n\to 0$.

▸ Common Mistake

When $x\to-\infty$, $\sqrt{x^2}=|x|=-x$, not $x$. Students pull a power out of a radical and write $\sqrt{9x^6}=3x^3$ even for negative $x$, getting the sign of the whole limit wrong. For $x\to-\infty$, $\sqrt{x^6}=|x^3|=-x^3$.

Always check which direction $x$ is heading before taking a variable out of a square root — the absolute value is the whole point.

Radicals: factor the highest even power out from under the root. Watch the sign when $x\to -\infty$ — $\sqrt{x^2}=|x|=-x$ when $x<0$.

Rational Function Rules ($x\to\infty$)

deg(num) $>$ deg(den):
 limit is $\pm\infty$  (no horizontal asymptote)

deg(num) $<$ deg(den):
 limit is $0$  (horizontal asymptote $y=0$)

deg(num) $=$ deg(den):
 limit $=$ ratio of leading coefficients

▸ Common Mistake

In the equal-degree case the limit is the ratio of the leading coefficients — not the constant terms, and not a plug-in. For $\frac{3x^2-x}{x^2+x}$ the answer is $\frac{3}{1}=3$, from the $x^2$ coefficients only; students grab the constants and get it wrong.

Don't mix up the three cases either: a bigger top degree blows up to $\pm\infty$, a bigger bottom degree gives $0$, and only equal degrees give a finite nonzero ratio.

Vertical asymptotes: set the denominator $=0$. For $1/x$ near $x=0$: left side $\to -\infty$, right side $\to +\infty$, so the two-sided limit is DNE. For $1/x^2$: both sides $\to +\infty$, so the limit is $+\infty$.

$f(x)=\tfrac{1}{x}$ — as $x\to\pm\infty$, $f\to 0$ (HA $y=0$); as $x\to 0$, $f\to\pm\infty$ (VA $x=0$)

$f(x)=\tfrac{1}{x-2}$ — same shape, shifted right by 2; VA moves to $x=2$, HA stays $y=0$

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Equal degrees
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{3x^2-x}{x^2+x}$.
Divide by $x^2$: $\dfrac{3-1/x}{1+1/x}\to\dfrac{3}{1}=3$
Example 2  —  Numerator degree lower
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{x}{x^2+x}$.
Divide by $x^2$: $\dfrac{1/x}{1+1/x}\to\dfrac{0}{1}=0$
Horizontal asymptote at $y=0$.
Example 3  —  Vertical asymptote
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0^+}\dfrac{1}{x^2}$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0}\dfrac{1}{x}$.
$1/x^2$: both sides $\to +\infty$, limit $= +\infty$.
$1/x$: left $\to -\infty$, right $\to +\infty$ → DNE.
Example 4  —  Radical at infinity
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to+\infty}\dfrac{\sqrt{9x^6+1}}{x^3}$.
Factor $x^6$ from radical ($x^3>0$ for $x>0$):
$\dfrac{x^3\sqrt{9+1/x^6}}{x^3}=\sqrt{9+1/x^6}\to 3$
Practice Problems
1.
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{5x^3-2}{x^3+1}$.
$5$
2.
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{4x^2+1}{x^3-2}$.
$0$
3.
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0^-}\dfrac{1}{x}$.
$-\infty$
4.
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{\sqrt{16x^4+3}}{2x^2}$.
$2$
5.
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{7x+3}{2x-1}$.
$\dfrac{7}{2}$
6.
Find $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{x^2-4}{3x^2+x}$.
$\dfrac{1}{3}$
