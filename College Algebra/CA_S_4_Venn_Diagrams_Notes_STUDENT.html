Imagine $A$ = cat lovers and $B$ = dog lovers in a class. A Venn diagram has four regions: only $A$, only $B$, both, and neither. What would each region represent in this scenario?
Two-set Venn diagram: four labeled regions
Reading a Venn Diagram

Four regions in a two-set Venn diagram:

  • Only $A$: in $A$ but not $B$
  • $A \cap B$: in both $A$ and $B$
  • Only $B$: in $B$ but not $A$
  • Neither: in $U$ but outside $A \cup B$

Cardinality formulas:
$|A| = $ (only $A$) $+ |A \cap B|$
$|A \cup B| = |A| + |B| - |A \cap B|$
$|U| = |A \cup B| + $ neither

▸ Show Proof

This formula corrects for double-counting, and the four-region breakdown above is exactly what makes that visible.

From the regions: $|A|=(\text{only }A)+|A\cap B|$ and $|B|=(\text{only }B)+|A\cap B|$. Adding these two equations: $$|A|+|B|=(\text{only }A)+(\text{only }B)+2|A\cap B|.$$

But $A\cup B$ is made up of exactly three regions — only $A$, only $B$, and $A\cap B$ — each counted once: $$|A\cup B|=(\text{only }A)+(\text{only }B)+|A\cap B|.$$

Comparing the two equations, the right side of the first is the right side of the second plus one extra copy of $|A\cap B|$. So $$|A|+|B|=|A\cup B|+|A\cap B|.$$

Solving for $|A\cup B|$: $$|A\cup B|=|A|+|B|-|A\cap B|.$$ In words: adding $|A|$ and $|B|$ counts the overlap region twice, so subtracting $|A\cap B|$ once brings it back down to being counted exactly once, matching the true total.

∎

De Morgan's Laws

$(A \cup B)' = A' \cap B'$
Not in $A$ or $B$ → not in $A$ AND not in $B$
(shaded region = neither)

$(A \cap B)' = A' \cup B'$
Not in both → not in $A$ OR not in $B$
(shaded region = everything except the center)

These laws let you distribute complements over union/intersection — and flip the operation.

▸ Show Proof

To prove two sets are equal, show that an arbitrary element $x$ belongs to one exactly when it belongs to the other.

$(A\cup B)'=A'\cap B'$: saying $x\in(A\cup B)'$ means $x\notin A\cup B$, i.e. it is not true that ($x\in A$ or $x\in B$). By the everyday logic of "not (this or that)" — think "not (raining or snowing)" means "not raining and not snowing" — this is the same as saying $x\notin A$ and $x\notin B$.

That's exactly $x\in A'$ and $x\in B'$, i.e. $x\in A'\cap B'$. Every step above was reversible ("if and only if"), so $x\in(A\cup B)'$ precisely when $x\in A'\cap B'$ — meaning the two sets contain exactly the same elements: $(A\cup B)'=A'\cap B'$.

$(A\cap B)'=A'\cup B'$: the same idea with "and"/"or" swapped. $x\in(A\cap B)'$ means not ($x\in A$ and $x\in B$), which — think "not (raining and cold)" means "not raining or not cold" — is the same as $x\notin A$ or $x\notin B$, i.e. $x\in A'$ or $x\in B'$, i.e. $x\in A'\cup B'$. So $(A\cap B)'=A'\cup B'$.

∎

Only $A = 5$, $A \cap B = 3$, only $B = 4$, neither $= 2$. Find $|A|$, $|B|$, $|A \cup B|$, and $|U|$.
$|A|=8$   $|B|=7$   $|A \cup B|=12$   $|U|=14$

$|A'| = |U| - |A| = 14 - 8 = 6$
$|(A \cup B)'| = $ neither $= 2$
