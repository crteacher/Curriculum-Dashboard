Exponent Rules

College Algebra  ·  §6.1  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

$2^3 \cdot 2^4 = ?$   Why does multiplying powers of the same base mean you add the exponents? Can you explain it by writing out the full multiplication?
The Five Core Rules

Product rule: $a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}$

Quotient rule: $\dfrac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}$

Power of a power: $(a^m)^n = a^{mn}$

Power of a product: $(ab)^n = a^n b^n$

Power of a quotient: $\left(\dfrac{a}{b}\right)^n = \dfrac{a^n}{b^n}$

▸ Show Proof

The product rule answers the opening question: why does $a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}$?

Write out what each power means: $a^m$ is $a$ multiplied by itself $m$ times, and $a^n$ is $a$ multiplied $n$ times. Putting them together gives $m+n$ copies of $a$:

$$a^m \cdot a^n = \underbrace{(a \cdot a \cdots a)}_{m\text{ copies}} \cdot \underbrace{(a \cdot a \cdots a)}_{n\text{ copies}} = \underbrace{a \cdot a \cdots a}_{m+n\text{ copies}} = a^{m+n}$$

Example: $2^3 \cdot 2^4 = (2\cdot2\cdot2)(2\cdot2\cdot2\cdot2) = 2^7 = 128$.

The other four rules follow the same idea: the power-of-power rule stacks repeated multiplication, and the product/quotient rules for powers distribute the exponent across factors.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

The most common error with these rules is swapping add and multiply: students see $(x^2)^3$ and write $x^{2+3}=x^5$ instead of $x^{2\cdot 3}=x^6$, or they see $x^2\cdot x^3$ and multiply the exponents to get $x^6$ instead of adding to get $x^5$.

When you're multiplying two powers of the same base, the exponents add ($a^m\cdot a^n=a^{m+n}$) because you're combining two separate stacks of factors into one longer stack. When you're raising a power to a power, the exponents multiply ($(a^m)^n=a^{mn}$) because you're repeating an already-repeated multiplication. Before you touch the exponents, say out loud which operation is sitting between the two powers — "times" or "to the power of" — and let that decide add vs. multiply.

Zero, Negative & Fractional Exponents

Zero exponent: $a^0 = 1$   ($a \neq 0$)

Negative exponent: $a^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{a^n}$

Fractional exponent: $a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}$   and   $a^{m/n} = \left(\sqrt[n]{a}\right)^m$
e.g. $27^{2/3} = (\sqrt[3]{27})^2 = 3^2 = 9$

▸ Show Proof

Each extended rule is forced by demanding consistency with the product rule $a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}$.

Zero exponent: $a^m \cdot a^0 = a^{m+0} = a^m$. Dividing both sides by $a^m$ gives $a^0 = 1$ (for $a\neq 0$).

Negative exponent: $a^n \cdot a^{-n} = a^{n+(-n)} = a^0 = 1$. So $a^{-n}$ is the reciprocal of $a^n$: $a^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{a^n}$.

Fractional exponent: $(a^{1/n})^n = a^{(1/n)\cdot n} = a^1 = a$ by the power-of-power rule. So $a^{1/n}$ is the number whose $n$th power is $a$ — that is exactly $\sqrt[n]{a}$ by definition. Then $a^{m/n} = (a^{1/n})^m = (\sqrt[n]{a})^m$.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

Students often distribute the negative sign onto the base instead of flipping to a reciprocal, writing $a^{-n} = -a^n$ instead of the correct $a^{-n}=\dfrac{1}{a^n}$. For example, $2^{-3}$ gets simplified to "$-8$" instead of $\dfrac{1}{8}$.

The negative sign in the exponent is not a sign on the value — it's an instruction to take the reciprocal. Nothing about $a^{-n}$ is negative when $a>0$: $2^{-3}=\dfrac{1}{2^3}=\dfrac{1}{8}$, a positive number less than 1. Do the reciprocal step first, then evaluate; don't let the minus sign leak onto the final answer.

Final answers should have no negative exponents and no parentheses.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Product and quotient rules
Simplify $\dfrac{x^5 \cdot x^3}{x^4}$.
$x^{5+3-4} = x^4$
Example 2  —  Power of a power
Simplify $(x^2)^5$.
$x^{2 \cdot 5} = x^{10}$
Example 3  —  Fractional exponent
Evaluate $27^{2/3}$.
$\left(\sqrt[3]{27}\right)^2 = 3^2 = 9$
Example 4  —  Combining rules
Simplify $\dfrac{a^6 b^9}{a^4 \cdot b^{-2}}$.
$a^{6-4} \cdot b^{9-(-2)} = a^2 b^{11}$
