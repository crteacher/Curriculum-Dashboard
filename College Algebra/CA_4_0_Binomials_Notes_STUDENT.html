Binomials & Polynomial Multiplication

College Algebra  ·  §4.0  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

FOIL is a memory device for applying the distributive property twice. When you multiply $(a+b)(c+d)$, how many individual products do you get, and which letter of FOIL produces the constant term?
FOIL — General Binomials

$(a+b)(c+d) = ac + ad + bc + bd$

First  ·  Outer  ·  Inner  ·  Last

e.g. $(x+2)(x+7)$:
F: $x \cdot x = x^2$
O: $x \cdot 7 = 7x$
I: $2 \cdot x = 2x$
L: $2 \cdot 7 = 14$
$= x^2 + 9x + 14$

▸ Common Mistake

Students multiply only First and Last, skipping Outer and Inner entirely — turning $(x+2)(x+7)$ into $x^2+14$ instead of $x^2+9x+14$. The middle term isn't optional decoration; it's the sum of the two "cross" products ($O+I=7x+2x=9x$) that comes from the distributive property applied twice. Every binomial times binomial produces four products before combining like terms, not two.

A quick check: if your expanded answer has no middle ($x$) term at all, and the original wasn't a difference-of-squares pattern like $(x+a)(x-a)$, you almost certainly dropped the Outer and Inner steps.

Special Patterns

Difference of squares:
$(a+b)(a-b) = a^2 - b^2$
e.g. $(x+6)(x-6) = x^2 - 36$

Perfect square trinomial:
$(a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$
$(a-b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2$
e.g. $(x+5)^2 = x^2 + 10x + 25$

Tip: recognizing these patterns saves time and prevents sign errors.

▸ Common Mistake

The single most common algebra error of all: students "distribute" the exponent over the sum and write $(a+b)^2=a^2+b^2$, dropping the middle term entirely. But $(a+b)^2$ means $(a+b)(a+b)$, and FOILing that out gives $a^2+ab+ba+b^2=a^2+2ab+b^2$ — the cross terms don't vanish just because both factors are the same. For $(x+5)^2$, that missing middle term is the difference between the correct $x^2+10x+25$ and the wrong $x^2+25$.

You can catch this instantly by testing a number: if $a=1,b=1$, then $(a+b)^2=4$, but $a^2+b^2=2$ — they're not equal, so the shortcut can't be right. The rule "exponents distribute over addition" is true for multiplication ($(ab)^2=a^2b^2$) but never for addition.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Basic FOIL
Expand $(x+2)(x+7)$.
$x^2 + 7x + 2x + 14 = x^2 + 9x + 14$
Example 2  —  Mixed signs
Expand $(x-3)(x+8)$.
$x^2 + 8x - 3x - 24 = x^2 + 5x - 24$
Example 3  —  Difference of squares
Expand $(x+6)(x-6)$.
$(x+6)(x-6) = x^2 - 36$
Example 4  —  Perfect square
Expand $(x+5)^2$.
$(x+5)^2 = x^2 + 10x + 25$
