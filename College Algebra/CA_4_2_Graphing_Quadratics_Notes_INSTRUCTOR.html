Graphing Quadratics

College Algebra  ·  §4.2  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

A football thrown in the air, an arch on a bridge, the cables of a suspension bridge — all parabolas. The equation $f(x)=ax^2+bx+c$ controls everything: which way it opens, how wide, and where the turning point sits. Before graphing anything, what single number in the equation tells you whether the parabola opens up or down?
Standard Form $f(x)=ax^2+bx+c$

$a>0$ → opens up (vertex is a minimum)
$a<0$ → opens down (vertex is a maximum)

Axis of symmetry: $x=-\dfrac{b}{2a}$

Vertex:
  $x$-coordinate: $h=-\dfrac{b}{2a}$
  $y$-coordinate: $k=f(h)$

$y$-intercept: always at $(0,c)$

Example: $f(x)=x^2+4x+1$:
$h=-4/2=-2$, $k=4-8+1=-3$
Vertex: $(-2,-3)$   Axis: $x=-2$

▸ Show Proof

The vertex formula comes from rewriting $f(x)=ax^2+bx+c$ so the squared term is isolated — a technique called completing the square.

Factor $a$ out of the first two terms: $f(x)=a\left(x^2+\dfrac{b}{a}x\right)+c$.

Inside the parentheses, add and subtract $\left(\dfrac{b}{2a}\right)^2$ to build a perfect square without changing the value: $$f(x)=a\left(x^2+\dfrac{b}{a}x+\dfrac{b^2}{4a^2}-\dfrac{b^2}{4a^2}\right)+c=a\left(x+\dfrac{b}{2a}\right)^2-\dfrac{b^2}{4a}+c.$$

This is now $f(x)=a(x-h)^2+k$ with $h=-\dfrac{b}{2a}$ and $k=c-\dfrac{b^2}{4a}$.

Since $\left(x+\dfrac{b}{2a}\right)^2\geq0$ always, and equals $0$ exactly when $x=-\dfrac{b}{2a}$, the term $a\left(x+\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2$ is as small as possible (if $a>0$) or as large as possible (if $a<0$) precisely at $x=h$. That's why $h=-\dfrac{b}{2a}$ locates the vertex, and $k=f(h)$ follows just by plugging $h$ back in.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

Students drop the negative sign and compute $h=\dfrac{b}{2a}$ instead of $h=-\dfrac{b}{2a}$. For $f(x)=x^2+4x+1$ that gives $h=2$ instead of the correct $h=-2$ — the vertex lands on the wrong side of the $y$-axis entirely.

The minus sign isn't optional decoration; it falls out of completing the square, where the boxed term is $\left(x+\dfrac{b}{2a}\right)^2$, meaning $x-h$ with $h=-\dfrac{b}{2a}$. A quick sanity check: if $b$ and $a$ have the same sign, the vertex's $x$-coordinate should be negative (for $a>0$) — plug in and see the formula confirms this only when the minus sign is kept.
