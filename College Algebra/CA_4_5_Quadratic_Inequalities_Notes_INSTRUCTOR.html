Quadratic Inequalities

College Algebra  ·  §4.5  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

A parabola that opens upward is negative between its roots and positive outside them. What two pieces of information do you need before you can build a sign chart for a quadratic inequality?
The Sign Chart Method

5 steps:

  1. Move everything to one side: $f(x) \lessgtr 0$
  2. Find the roots by factoring or quadratic formula
  3. Plot roots on a number line — these are the boundary points
  4. Test a value from each region
  5. Select regions where $f(x)$ has the correct sign

Note: use $( )$ for strict inequalities ($<$, $>$) and $[ ]$ for $\leq$, $\geq$.

▸ Common Mistake

Students often skip "test a value from each region" once they have the roots, assuming the sign just alternates $+,-,+,-,\dots$ as you cross each boundary point — left to right, flip every time. That pattern holds for distinct roots, but it breaks down for a repeated root.

For $x^2-6x+9<0$, i.e. $(x-3)^2<0$: the sign chart has only one boundary at $x=3$, but $(x-3)^2$ is $\geq0$ on both sides — it never goes negative, so there's no solution at all. Always test a point in each region rather than trusting an alternating pattern.

Quick Patterns (Opens Up)

For $ax^2 + bx + c$ with $a > 0$ and roots $r_1 < r_2$:

$f(x) < 0$: between the roots → $(r_1, r_2)$

$f(x) > 0$: outside the roots → $(-\infty, r_1) \cup (r_2, \infty)$

e.g. $x^2 - 6x + 8 < 0$: roots $x=2, x=4$
Parabola opens up → negative between roots → $(2, 4)$

▸ Show Proof

Write the quadratic in factored form: $f(x) = a(x-r_1)(x-r_2)$ with $a > 0$ and $r_1 < r_2$.

The sign of $f(x)$ depends on the signs of the two factors $(x-r_1)$ and $(x-r_2)$:

Outside left ($x < r_1$): both $(x-r_1) < 0$ and $(x-r_2) < 0$. Product of two negatives is positive. $f(x) > 0$. ✓

Between ($r_1 < x < r_2$): $(x-r_1) > 0$ but $(x-r_2) < 0$. Product of opposite signs is negative. $f(x) < 0$. ✓

Outside right ($x > r_2$): both factors positive. $f(x) > 0$. ✓

For $a < 0$, multiply through by $-1$: all signs flip, so the pattern reverses.

∎

▸ Common Mistake

For something like $x^2-9\geq0$, students often take a "square root" shortcut straight to $x\geq3$, the same instinct that works for equations like $x^2=9$. But an inequality has a whole outside region, not just one branch.

Here $x^2-9\geq0$ factors as roots $x=\pm3$, and since the pattern is "outside the roots" for $f(x)>0$ with $a>0$, the actual solution is $(-\infty,-3]\cup[3,\infty)$ — both branches, not just the positive one. Whenever you see $x^2 > k$ (or $\geq$), remember it opens outward in both directions.

For $a < 0$ (opens down), reverse the pattern.

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Less than zero (between roots)
Solve $x^2 - 6x + 8 < 0$.
Roots: $(x-2)(x-4)=0 \Rightarrow x=2,4$
Opens up → negative between roots → $(2, 4)$
Example 2  —  Greater than zero (outside roots)
Solve $x^2 - 4x - 5 > 0$.
Roots: $(x-5)(x+1)=0 \Rightarrow x=-1,5$
Positive outside roots → $(-\infty,-1) \cup (5,\infty)$
Example 3  —  Sign chart with test value
Solve $x^2 - x - 6 > 0$.
Roots: $(x-3)(x+2)=0 \Rightarrow x=-2,3$
Test $x=0$: $-6 < 0$ → middle region is negative
$f>0$ outside: $(-\infty,-2) \cup (3,\infty)$
Example 4  —  Less than or equal to (closed brackets)
Solve $x^2 - 2x - 3 \leq 0$.
Roots: $(x-3)(x+1)=0 \Rightarrow x=-1,3$
Negative between roots (include endpoints) → $[-1, 3]$
