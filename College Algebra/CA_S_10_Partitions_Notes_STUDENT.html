Partitions

College Algebra  ·  §S.10  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

A class of 10 splits into two field trip groups of 5 — one going to Washington DC, the other to Chicago. How many ways can this happen? Now suppose the groups have no labels — they are just “Group 1” and “Group 2” with no destination attached. Does that change the count? Why?
Ordered Partitions — Groups Have Labels

Splitting $n$ objects into labeled groups of sizes $n_1, n_2, \ldots$:

$$\frac{n!}{n_1! \cdot n_2! \cdots} = C(n,n_1) \times C(n-n_1, n_2) \times \cdots$$

e.g. 10 people → DC (5) and Chicago (5):
$C(10,5) = 252$

e.g. 9 people → Team A (2), Team B (3), Team C (4):
$C(9,2) \times C(7,3) \times C(4,4) = 36 \times 35 \times 1 = 1260$

“Labeled” means swapping groups creates a new outcome.

▸ Show Proof

This directly answers the first half of the opening question. Build the partition one labeled group at a time.

First, choose which $n_1$ of the $n$ objects go into the first group: there are $C(n,n_1)$ ways to do this.

That leaves $n-n_1$ objects. Choose which $n_2$ of those go into the second group: $C(n-n_1,n_2)$ ways. Continue the same way for every remaining group, always choosing from whatever objects are left.

Each of these choices is made after (and independently of) the ones before it, so the multiplication principle says the total number of ways to build the whole partition is the product of every step's count: $C(n,n_1)\times C(n-n_1,n_2)\times\cdots$.

Writing each combination out as a ratio of factorials and cancelling the repeated terms collapses this product down to the compact form $\dfrac{n!}{n_1!\cdot n_2!\cdots}$ — the two expressions count the exact same thing, just organized differently.

∎

Unordered Partitions — Groups Are Identical

Splitting $n$ objects into $m$ interchangeable groups of size $r$:

Divide the ordered count by $m!$

e.g. 6 friends into 2 unlabeled groups of 3:
$\frac{C(6,3)}{2!} = \frac{20}{2} = 10$

e.g. 10 people into 2 unlabeled groups of 5:
$\frac{C(10,5)}{2!} = \frac{252}{2} = 126$

“Unlabeled” means swapping both groups gives the same split.

▸ Show Proof

This answers the second half of the opening question: yes, removing the labels changes the count, and this is exactly why.

Start from the labeled count (proved on the left): pretend the $m$ groups have labels, "Group 1" through "Group $m$." That count includes every possible labeled assignment of objects to groups.

But once the groups are truly interchangeable, several different labeled assignments describe the exact same real-world split — specifically, every way of reshuffling which physical group is called "Group 1," "Group 2," etc. produces the identical unlabeled partition.

There are exactly $m!$ ways to assign $m$ distinct labels to $m$ groups, so every genuinely different unlabeled partition gets counted $m!$ times over in the labeled total.

Dividing the labeled count by $m!$ removes that overcounting exactly, leaving the true number of distinct unlabeled partitions.

∎

Key question: Do the groups have distinct identities? → Ordered. Are they interchangeable? → Unordered (divide by $m!$).

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Ordered — 2 labeled groups
8 students split into a morning group (3) and afternoon group (5). How many ways?
$C(8,3) = 56$
Example 2  —  Ordered — 3 labeled groups
9 people split into Team A (2), Team B (3), Team C (4). How many ways?
$C(9,2) \times C(7,3) \times C(4,4) = 36 \times 35 \times 1 = 1260$
Example 3  —  Unordered — 2 equal groups
6 friends split into 2 unlabeled groups of 3. How many ways?
$\frac{C(6,3)}{2!} = \frac{20}{2} = 10$
Example 4  —  Ordered vs. unordered
10 students split into 2 groups of 5. (a) Groups labeled. (b) Groups unlabeled.
(a) $C(10,5) = 252$
(b) $\frac{C(10,5)}{2!} = \frac{252}{2} = 126$
