Completing the Square

College Algebra  ·  §4.3  ·  Instructor: Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026

Core Ideas
The Identity $x^2+bx \;=\; \left(x+\dfrac{b}{2}\right)^{\!2} - \left(\dfrac{b}{2}\right)^{\!2}$  — rewrite the expression, not the equation. Substitute this into $x^2+bx+c=0$, then isolate the squared term and apply the square root method.
Why This Generalizes Applying the same identity to $ax^2+bx+c=0$ (dividing by $a$ first) gives $\left(x+\dfrac{b}{2a}\right)^{\!2}=\dfrac{b^2-4ac}{4a^2}$, which is exactly the quadratic formula. No tricks — just one identity.
Square Root Method $(x-h)^2=k \;\Rightarrow\; x=h\pm\sqrt{k}$. Two solutions when $k>0$, one when $k=0$, none (real) when $k<0$.
