Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

College Algebra  ·  §2.4  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

Train tracks are parallel, and a street corner is perpendicular. Both relationships are completely defined by slope. What does slope have to do with whether two lines intersect or form a right angle?
Parallel Lines

Parallel lines have the same slope:
$m_1 = m_2$   (and different y-intercepts)

e.g. $y = 4x - 1$ and $y = 4x + 7$:
Both have slope 4 → parallel

Writing a parallel line:
Keep the same slope; use the given point in point-slope form.
e.g. parallel to $y = 3x - 2$ through $(0, 5)$:
$m = 3$, $b = 5 \Rightarrow y = 3x + 5$

▸ Show Proof

Two lines are parallel exactly when they never cross. So it's enough to show: unequal slopes always force an intersection — then by contrapositive, no intersection (parallel) forces equal slopes.

Take two lines $y=m_1x+b_1$ and $y=m_2x+b_2$ with $m_1\neq m_2$. Setting them equal to find a crossing point: $$m_1x+b_1=m_2x+b_2.$$

Solve for $x$: $(m_1-m_2)x=b_2-b_1$, so $x=\dfrac{b_2-b_1}{m_1-m_2}$. Since $m_1\neq m_2$, this division is always valid — there's always a solution, meaning the lines always cross somewhere.

So if two lines have different slopes, they must intersect. Flipping that around: if two lines never intersect (they're parallel), their slopes cannot be different — they must be equal.

∎

Perpendicular Lines

Perpendicular lines have slopes that are negative reciprocals:
$m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1$   i.e. $m_2 = -\dfrac{1}{m_1}$

e.g. slope $= -2$ → perpendicular slope $= \dfrac{1}{2}$

Writing a perpendicular line:
Flip and negate the slope; use the given point.
e.g. perpendicular to $y = 2x + 1$ through $(2, 3)$:
$m = -\frac{1}{2}$; $y - 3 = -\frac{1}{2}(x-2) \Rightarrow y = -\frac{1}{2}x + 4$

▸ Show Proof

Think of slope $m_1=\dfrac{a}{b}$ as a right triangle: go over $b$, up $a$, and the hypotenuse lies along the line.

Rotate that triangle $90°$ about its vertex. Rotating a right angle swaps which leg is horizontal and which is vertical, and reverses the direction of one of them. So the triangle that was "over $b$, up $a$" becomes "over $a$, down $b$" — the new hypotenuse lies along the line perpendicular to the first.

That new hypotenuse has slope $m_2=\dfrac{-b}{a}$ (rise $-b$ over run $a$).

Multiply the two slopes: $$m_1\cdot m_2=\dfrac{a}{b}\cdot\dfrac{-b}{a}=-1.$$ Rotating by $90°$ is exactly what "perpendicular" means, and it's exactly what produces the negative-reciprocal relationship.

∎

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Identify parallel lines
Are $y = 4x - 1$ and $y = 4x + 7$ parallel, perpendicular, or neither?
Same slope ($m = 4$) → Parallel
Example 2  —  Find perpendicular slope
What is the slope of a line perpendicular to $y = -2x + 5$?
$m_1 = -2 \Rightarrow m_\perp = \dfrac{1}{2}$
Example 3  —  Write a parallel line
Write the equation of a line parallel to $y = 3x - 2$ through $(0, 5)$.
$m = 3$, $b = 5 \Rightarrow y = 3x + 5$
Example 4  —  Write a perpendicular line
Write the equation perpendicular to $y = 2x + 1$ through $(2, 3)$.
$m = -\frac{1}{2}$; $y - 3 = -\frac{1}{2}(x-2) \Rightarrow y = -\frac{1}{2}x + 4$
