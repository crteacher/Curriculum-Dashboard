Probability with Permutations & Combinations

College Algebra  ·  §S.11  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

You flip a coin 10 times. What is the probability of getting exactly 4 heads? You know the total number of outcomes is $2^{10} = 1024$. You just need the favorable outcomes — and those are exactly the sequences with 4 heads out of 10 flips. Which counting tool gives that number?
The Framework
$$P(\text{event}) = \frac{\text{favorable outcomes}}{\text{total outcomes}}$$

Total outcomes: multiplication principle, $n!$, or $2^n$ for coin flips

Favorable outcomes: $C(n,r)$ or $P(n,r)$ — whether order matters

Coin flip probabilities:

$$P(\text{exactly }k\text{ heads in }n\text{ flips}) = \frac{C(n,k)}{2^n}$$
$$\text{e.g. } P(\text{exactly 4 heads in 10 flips}) = \frac{210}{1024} = \frac{105}{512}$$

At least 1 head:

$$1 - P(\text{0 heads}) = 1 - \frac{1}{2^n}$$
▸ Show Proof

This answers the opening question directly: which counting tool gives the favorable-outcome count.

Total outcomes: each of the $n$ flips independently lands heads or tails — $2$ possibilities per flip. By the multiplication principle, the number of possible flip-sequences is $\underbrace{2\cdot2\cdots2}_{n\text{ flips}}=2^n$.

Favorable outcomes: a sequence has "exactly $k$ heads" precisely when we've chosen which $k$ of the $n$ flip-positions turned out heads (the rest are automatically tails). The number of ways to choose which $k$ positions out of $n$ are heads is exactly $C(n,k)$ — that's the tool the opening question is asking about.

Combining favorable over total: $P(\text{exactly }k\text{ heads})=\dfrac{C(n,k)}{2^n}$.

At least 1 head: the only way to fail to get at least one head is to get zero heads (all tails), which happens in exactly $1$ way out of $2^n$: $P(0\text{ heads})=\frac{1}{2^n}$. "At least 1 head" and "0 heads" are complementary — exactly one happens every time — so their probabilities add to $1$: $P(\text{at least 1 head})=1-P(0\text{ heads})=1-\dfrac{1}{2^n}$.

∎

Committee and Selection Problems

One specific person included:

$$P = \frac{C(n-1,\,r-1)}{C(n,r)} = \frac{r}{n} \qquad \text{e.g., 3-from-10: } P = \frac{3}{10}$$

Two specific people both included:

$$P = \frac{C(n-2,\,r-2)}{C(n,r)} = \frac{r(r-1)}{n(n-1)} \qquad \text{e.g., } \frac{C(8,1)}{C(10,3)} = \frac{8}{120} = \frac{1}{15}$$
▸ Show Proof

This complicated-looking ratio of combinations simplifies down to something you could have guessed intuitively — here's why.

Write both combinations as factorials: $C(n-1,r-1)=\dfrac{(n-1)!}{(r-1)!(n-r)!}$ and $C(n,r)=\dfrac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}$.

Dividing the first by the second (which means multiplying by the reciprocal): $$\frac{C(n-1,r-1)}{C(n,r)}=\frac{(n-1)!}{(r-1)!(n-r)!}\cdot\frac{r!(n-r)!}{n!}=\frac{(n-1)!\cdot r!}{n!\cdot(r-1)!}.$$

Since $n!=n\cdot(n-1)!$ and $r!=r\cdot(r-1)!$, the $(n-1)!$ and $(r-1)!$ factors cancel completely, leaving just $\dfrac{r}{n}$.

That matches intuition: if a committee of size $r$ is chosen completely at random from $n$ people, every individual person has exactly the same $\dfrac{r}{n}$ chance of landing on it — no person is special. The two-specific-people version works out the same way, cancelling down to $\dfrac{r(r-1)}{n(n-1)}$.

∎

Ordered Selection (PIN / Password)

Use $P(n,r)$ (permutations) for favorable outcomes when order matters and repetition is not allowed.

$$\text{2-digit PIN, no repeats: } P = \frac{P(10,2)}{10^2} = \frac{90}{100} = \frac{9}{10}$$
$$\text{3-digit PIN, no repeats: } P = \frac{P(10,3)}{10^3} = \frac{720}{1000} = \frac{9}{10}\cdot\frac{8}{10} = \frac{18}{25}$$
