Multiplication Principle

College Algebra  ·  §S.7  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

A pizza shop offers 3 crust types and 4 toppings. Instead of listing every combination, is there a faster way to count? What principle makes this possible?
The Multiplication Principle

If a task has $n_1$ ways to do step 1, $n_2$ ways to do step 2, and so on, then the total number of ways is:

$$n_1 \times n_2 \times n_3 \times \cdots$$

e.g. 4 sandwich types $\times$ 3 drinks = 12 lunch combinations

e.g. Flipping a coin 5 times: $2^5 = 32$ total sequences

Key: the choices at each step must be independent.

▸ Show Proof

Why the total is $n_1 \times n_2 \times \cdots$:

Picture the outcomes as a tree. After step 1 there are $n_1$ branches. Each branch then splits $n_2$ ways for step 2, giving $n_1 \cdot n_2$ branches so far. Each of those splits $n_3$ ways for step 3, giving $n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdot n_3$ branches, and so on.

Why does $n_1$ groups of $n_2$ items equal $n_1 \cdot n_2$? That's just repeated addition: $\underbrace{n_2 + n_2 + \cdots + n_2}_{n_1\text{ times}} = n_1 \cdot n_2$.

The independence condition is essential: if later choices depend on earlier ones the branch counts at each level would not all be $n_2$, and the product would not apply.

∎

Factorial and Ordered Selection

Factorial: $n! = n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times 2 \times 1$
Counts the number of ways to arrange $n$ distinct objects.
e.g. $6! = 720$ ways to arrange 6 books.

Ordered selection without replacement:
Choose $r$ from $n$, where order matters:
$n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times (n-r+1)$   ($r$ factors)
e.g. President, VP, Treasurer from 8 people:
$8 \times 7 \times 6 = 336$

▸ Show Proof

Factorial from the Multiplication Principle:

Arranging $n$ distinct objects in a row is a sequential task: slot 1 has $n$ choices, slot 2 has $n-1$ remaining choices, slot 3 has $n-2$, and so on until slot $n$ has 1 choice left. By the Multiplication Principle:

$$n! = n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \times \cdots \times 2 \times 1.$$

Ordered selection of $r$ from $n$: Stop after filling $r$ slots instead of all $n$. Slot 1: $n$ choices, slot 2: $n-1$, ..., slot $r$: $n-r+1$ choices. The product of these $r$ factors can be written compactly as:

$$n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$$

because $n! = n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1)\cdot(n-r)!$, so dividing by $(n-r)!$ cancels the last $n-r$ factors and leaves exactly the $r$ we want.

∎

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Basic multiplication principle
A lunch menu has 4 sandwiches and 3 drinks. How many different lunches are possible?
$4 \times 3 = 12$ combinations
Example 2  —  Coin flips
A coin is flipped 5 times. How many total sequences of results are possible?
$2^5 = 32$ sequences
Example 3  —  Ordered selection
From 8 students, how many ways can you choose a President, VP, and Treasurer?
$8 \times 7 \times 6 = 336$
Example 4  —  Arrangements (factorial)
How many ways can 6 books be arranged on a shelf?
$6! = 720$
Practice Problems
1.
A menu has 3 appetizers, 5 entrees, and 2 desserts. How many 3-course meals are possible?
$3 \times 5 \times 2 = 30$
2.
A coin is flipped 4 times. How many total sequences?
$2^4 = 16$
3.
How many ways can 5 students be arranged in a row?
$5! = 120$
4.
From 10 people, how many ways can you pick a 1st place and 2nd place winner?
$10 \times 9 = 90$
5.
A lock has 4 digits (0–9, repetition allowed). How many combinations?
$10^4 = 10000$
6.
How many 3-letter codes (A–Z, no repeat) are possible?
$26 \times 25 \times 24 = 15600$
