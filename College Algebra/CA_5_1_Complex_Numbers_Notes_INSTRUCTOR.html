Complex Numbers

College Algebra  ·  §5.1  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  INSTRUCTOR

We define $i = \sqrt{-1}$, so $i^2 = -1$. If $i^2 = -1$, what would $i^3$ equal? What about $i^4$? Do you notice a pattern?
Defining i

Imaginary unit: $i = \sqrt{-1}$,   $i^2 = -1$

Powers of $i$ cycle with period 4:
$i^1 = i$   $i^2 = -1$   $i^3 = -i$   $i^4 = 1$

Simplify $\sqrt{-k}$:
$\sqrt{-k} = \sqrt{k} \cdot i$   (for $k > 0$)
e.g. $\sqrt{-25} = 5i$

Standard form: $a + bi$
$a$ = real part    $b$ = imaginary part

Operations & Quadratic Connections

Adding/subtracting: combine real parts and imaginary parts separately.
$(4+3i)+(-1+2i) = 3+5i$
$(5-2i)-(3+4i) = 2-6i$

Quadratic with negative discriminant:
When $D = b^2 - 4ac < 0$, the roots are complex.
e.g. $x^2+2x+10=0$:
$x = \dfrac{-2\pm\sqrt{-36}}{2} = \dfrac{-2\pm 6i}{2} = -1 \pm 3i$

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Simplify a square root
Simplify $\sqrt{-25}$.
$\sqrt{-25} = \sqrt{25} \cdot i = 5i$
Example 2  —  Add complex numbers
Add $(4+3i) + (-1+2i)$.
$(4-1) + (3+2)i = 3 + 5i$
Example 3  —  Subtract complex numbers
Subtract $(5-2i) - (3+4i)$.
$(5-3) + (-2-4)i = 2 - 6i$
Example 4  —  Quadratic with complex roots
Solve $x^2 + 2x + 10 = 0$.
$D = 4 - 40 = -36$;   $x = \dfrac{-2 \pm 6i}{2} = -1 \pm 3i$
