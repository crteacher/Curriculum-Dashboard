Permutations and Combinations

College Algebra  ·  §S.8  ·  Christopher Halverson  ·  Fall 2026  ·  STUDENT

From a class of 20, you need to pick 3 people for a committee — does it matter which person is #1, #2, or #3 on the list? Now suppose instead you are picking a President, VP, and Treasurer. Does the order of assignment matter now? This distinction — does order matter? — is the entire key to this section.
Permutations — Order Matters

$$P(n, r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!} = n(n-1)\cdots(n-r+1)$$

The number of ways to choose $r$ objects from $n$, where order matters.

e.g. President, VP, Treasurer from 10 people:
$P(10,3) = 10 \times 9 \times 8 = 720$

e.g. Arrange 5 books on a shelf:
$P(5,5) = 5! = 120$

Combinations — Order Does Not Matter

$$C(n, r) = \frac{n!}{r! \cdot (n-r)!} = \frac{P(n,r)}{r!}$$

The number of ways to choose $r$ objects from $n$, where order does NOT matter.

e.g. Choose a 3-person committee from 10:
$C(10,3) = 720 / 6 = 120$

Symmetry rule: $C(n,r) = C(n, n-r)$
e.g. $C(10,7) = C(10,3) = 120$

Worked Examples
Example 1  —  Compute P(n, r)
How many ways can you fill 1st, 2nd, and 3rd place from 7 runners?
$P(7,3) = 7 \times 6 \times 5 = 210$
Example 2  —  Compute C(n, r)
How many ways can a 4-person committee be chosen from 9 people?
$C(9,4) = \dfrac{9 \times 8 \times 7 \times 6}{4!} = \dfrac{3024}{24} = 126$
Example 3  —  Permutation or combination?
A team of 5 is selected from 12 players. Does order matter? Which formula?
Order does NOT matter (a team is a team).
Use $C(12,5) = 792$
Example 4  —  Symmetry rule
Compute $C(15,13)$ using the symmetry rule.
$C(15,13) = C(15,2) = \dfrac{15 \times 14}{2} = 105$
Practice Problems
1.
Compute $P(6,2)$.
$6 \times 5 = 30$
2.
Compute $C(6,2)$.
$\dfrac{6 \times 5}{2} = 15$
3.
Choosing a President and VP from 8 people — permutation or combination?
Permutation (order matters — different roles)
4.
Choosing a 2-person team from 8 people — permutation or combination?
Combination (order doesn't matter)
5.
Compute $C(8,6)$ using the symmetry rule.
$C(8,2) = \dfrac{8 \times 7}{2} = 28$
6.
Compute $P(5,3)$.
$5 \times 4 \times 3 = 60$
