The Math Behind Drawing the Lines

How can one party win 40% of the votes but 60% of the seats? By drawing the right lines in the right places. Gerrymandering is old — the word dates to 1812 — but the math used to detect and prove it is surprisingly recent.

Massachusetts, 1812. Governor Elbridge Gerry signed a bill drawing a state senate district so contorted that a newspaper cartoonist drew a salamander around its outline. The portmanteau "Gerrymander" stuck. Modern practitioners use computers and voter data to accomplish the same thing far more precisely — and mathematicians have had to develop new tools to expose it.
The United States (and many other countries) uses a single-member district system. The country is divided into geographic regions called districts, each of which elects exactly one representative. Whoever gets the most votes in a district wins — even with less than a majority.

Districts are redrawn every 10 years after each census, when population shifts are measured. In most states, the state legislature draws the lines — the very politicians who stand to benefit or lose from those decisions.
Single-member district
One representative elected per geographic region.
Redistricting
The process of redrawing district boundaries, typically after a census.
Apportionment
Determining how many representatives each state gets based on population.
Think about it: If a state has 100 voters split 60 Democratic / 40 Republican, and the state gets 5 seats, what's the most "fair" seat split? Is 3-2 fair? Is 5-0 possible?
Gerrymandering is the practice of drawing district boundaries to favor one political party (or group) over another. It exploits a fundamental tension in district-based elections: the geometry of the map can matter as much as the actual distribution of votes.

There are two main flavors: partisan gerrymandering (favoring a party) and racial gerrymandering (diluting or concentrating the votes of a racial group — which courts have addressed differently).
Think about it: In a winner-take-all system, winning by 1 vote is the same as winning by 100. Losing by 1 is the same as losing by 1,000. How could you exploit this?
The two core tools of gerrymandering are packing and cracking.

Packing: Concentrate the opposing party's voters into a few districts where they win by huge margins. Those landslide votes are "wasted" — they don't help anywhere else.

Cracking: Spread the opposing party's voters across many districts so they're never quite enough to win any of them.
Visual example — 25 voters, 5 districts of 5
Suppose ● = Party A voter, ○ = Party B voter. A wins 15, B wins 10 — 60/40 split.
The grid below shows the same 25 voters drawn into 5 districts three different ways.
In the simple 5×5 grid above, the voter layout is already perfectly segregated — A on the left, B on the right — so any reasonable district assignment gives A 3 and B 2. Real gerrymandering works with messier distributions. The key insight: packing B voters into 2 overwhelmingly B districts "wastes" their votes, while cracking them across districts prevents them from winning anywhere.
Given a fixed distribution of voters, what's the minimum number of seats a majority party could win, and what's the maximum? The gap between these extremes shows how much power the mapmaker holds.
Setup: 5 districts of 5 voters, 15 Party A / 10 Party B
Maximum for A (most efficient map): Win each district 3-2. That uses 15 A votes perfectly to win all 5. Result: A wins 5-0.

Minimum for A (worst-case map drawn by B's mapmaker): Pack A into 2 districts with votes to spare (5-0, 5-0), crack the rest so B wins 3 districts 3-2. Result: A wins 2-3.

Same voters, same real preferences: the mapmaker chooses whether A wins 5-0 or 2-3.
With a 60-40 voter split, the seat split can range anywhere from 2-3 to 5-0. The map can flip who controls the legislature without changing a single voter's preference.
One legal standard for district fairness is compactness — a compact district is roughly circular and doesn't have long tendrils reaching out to grab specific neighborhoods. But "compact" needs a precise definition to be useful in court.
Polsby-Popper Score
PP = 4π · Area / Perimeter²
A perfect circle scores 1.0. A very elongated or jagged district scores near 0.

Convex Hull Ratio
CHR = Area of district / Area of convex hull
How much of the smallest convex shape around the district is actually in the district?
Compactness scores are a useful flag, but not a complete answer. A state's natural shape (rivers, coastlines, mountains) can force non-compact districts even without gerrymandering. And a perfectly compact district can still be drawn to pack or crack voters.
Think about it: Can you draw a district that scores well on compactness but is still gerrymandered? Hint: think about a circular city surrounded by rural voters.
The efficiency gap, developed by Nicholas Stephanopoulos and Eric McGhee in 2014, is an attempt to give courts a single number that captures partisan gerrymandering. It's built on the concept of wasted votes.
A vote is wasted if it didn't help elect anyone:
• Every vote for the losing candidate is wasted.
• Every vote beyond what was needed to win (margin + 1) is also wasted.

EG = (Wasted votes for Party A − Wasted votes for Party B) / Total votes

A positive EG means Party A wastes more votes — indicating the map favors Party B.
Example: 3 districts, Party A vs Party B
District 1: A gets 80, B gets 20. A wins — B wastes 20, A wastes 80−(20+1) = 59.
District 2: A gets 45, B gets 55. B wins — A wastes 45, B wastes 55−(45+1) = 9.
District 3: A gets 45, B gets 55. B wins — A wastes 45, B wastes 9 again.
A wasted total: 59 + 45 + 45 = 149
B wasted total: 20 + 9 + 9 = 38
Total votes: 300
EG = (149 − 38) / 300 = 0.37 — a large efficiency gap favoring B
Gill v. Whitford (2018): Wisconsin Democrats challenged the state's 2011 district maps using the efficiency gap, arguing an EG of 13%+ indicated unlawful partisan gerrymandering. The Supreme Court ultimately ruled on procedural grounds without deciding the efficiency gap question — but the case put partisan gerrymandering math at the center of constitutional law for the first time.

Critics of the efficiency gap argue it can flag natural geographic clustering (urban Democrats concentrated in cities) as gerrymandering. The search for the right mathematical test continues.
Think about it: What would it mean for a state to have a 0% efficiency gap? Is that the same as "fair"?
